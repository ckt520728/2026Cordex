#### Primary progressive aphasias

5.1 General summary

There are three different variants of primary progressive aphasia (PPA): nonflu-

ent PPA (nfPPA), semantic dementia (SD), and logopenic PPA (lPPA). Patients

with nfPPA display (subtle) disorder (dysarthria, apraxia of speech, effortful

speech) and slow speech, impairments in phonological word fluency and in

copying task, as long as they include a planning component. Patients with SD

are characterized by problems understanding single words. They may also

exhibit visual agnosia being unable to identify pictures. Their copying and recog-

nition performance, however, may be almost perfect. The lPPA variant is charac-

terized by word-finding problems but otherwise fluent speech. These patients

show severe impairments in verbal working memory (digit span forward as mea-

sure of the phonological store) and mild impairments in verbal recognition tasks.

Assessment of PPA should entail a free description of a standardized pic-

ture to get a clinical impression of spontaneous speech. Moreover the results

from the word fluency tests of the Consortium to Establish a Registry for

Alzheimer’s Disease, Neuropsychological Assessment Battery (CERAD-

NAB), the language comprehension and digit span forward should be

inspected. Reading and writing skills are helpful for the differential diagno-

sis: patients with SD show surface dyslexia and dysgraphia, whereas patients

with lPPA and nfPPA might display phonological dyslexia.

NfPPA and lPPA may be treated by facilitation strategies using semantic,

phonologic, or action-related facilitation cues. A recent study combining

word facilitation training and tDCS revealed that the progress of lPPA and

nfPPA may be delayed by such a treatment. SD patients did not benefit from

that approach, however, this could have been due to the specific protocol for

the tDCS intervention (not stimulating the primary brain regions affected in

SD). In the case of nfPPA patients script training might be tried out to

improve speech and word finding. Up to now, there are no convincing treat-

ment studies for SD patients.

If the message plan has been processed, word retrieval starts. Success in

word retrieval may be impaired for different reasons: loss of the intention as

a dysexecutive or working memory related impairment, problems in mapping

visual information to speech (picture naming impairment, which may be

related to visual recognition deficits), and problems in accessing the verbal

stores. On a clinical level, any of these causes will lead to anomia (word-find-

ing problems). Problems in accessing the verbal store may be due to a storage

deficit, either of the meaning of the words or of the phonological word form.

If the meaning of the words is impaired, patients are typically able to speak

fluently but the content of their language will hardly be understandable. If an

acute event (e.g., a stroke) causes such a syndrome, this is called fluent apha-

sia or Wernicke’s aphasia. If the storage of the word meaning is intact and

correctly accessed, then the intention of the communication has to be realized

by the production of words in sentences. This implies that the rules of gram-

mar can be used, and the phonological word form can be accessed correctly

and in-time. Patients that are unable to execute these two steps are called

Broca aphasics, if there is an acute event causing the impairment. Because the

brain areas responsible for the storage and generation of phonological word

forms are adjacent to the areas planning and executing the generation of sylla-

bles (word form elements) most of these patients also suffer from an apraxia

of speech and some dysarthria. Table 5.1 shows the major aphasic syndromes

5.3 Nonfluent primary progressive aphasia

5.3.1 Clinical core features

According to Gorno-Tempini et al. (2011), the clinical diagnosis of nonflu-

ent/agrammatic variant PPA presupposes that at least one of the following

core features must be present: agrammatism in language production or effort-

ful, halting speech with inconsistent speech sound errors, and distortions

(apraxia of speech) (Table 5.3). In addition, at least two of the following

three features must be present: (1) impaired comprehension of syntactically

complex sentences, (2) spared single-word comprehension, and (3) spared

object knowledge.

Data regarding the incidence and prevalence of nfPPA are hard to find. It

has been estimated that one-half of the frontotemporal lobe degenerations

show one of the language syndromes and that one-half of them have the

nfPPA syndrome. This would imply an incidence rate of 0.40.7 per 100,000

inhabitants and a prevalence of 0.53.0 per 100,000 inhabitants. Because

these figures are based on postmortem findings and nfPPA may be caused by

different neuropathological processes, the total prevalence should be about

0.653.9 per 100,000 inhabitants and the incidence would be 0.50.9 per

100,000 inhabitants. The clinical onset of the disease is approximately 60

years (with a range from 30 to early 80 years), the mean survival period is

about 7 years, with a large variance. The gender ratio seems to be equal.

5.3.3 Language comprehension

The language impairment of nfPPA patients is not confined to the production

of words and sentences (Table 5.1). They also have problems understanding

complex sentences but not of single words, at least during the beginning of

their disease. Peelle et al. (2008) showed that nfPPA patients have problems

understanding grammatically complex sentences and constructing sentences

from written words, which are related to the content of a picture (Weintraub

et al., 2009).

Charles et al. (2014) compared the specific understanding problems of

nfPPA patients with those of lPPA, SD, and bvFTD patients. lPPA and SD

patients have problems with sentence understanding. As such, the main

question was what aspects of language might be responsible for these com-

prehension differences. Therefore Charles et al. (2014) varied two features

which could have an impact on comprehension. The first feature concerns

working memory. If working memory is the cause of an understanding def-

icit then a more or less linear relation between the length of the sentence

and the impairment should be present. Working memory is influenced by

duration and complexity. Hence Charles et al. (2014) used sentences such

as “The octopus was jolly and it trailed the seahorse” and “The fox that

followed the domesticated cat was fierce.” Both sentences are similar in

length, however, in the second sentence, the main message is interrupted

by additional (center) information. Charles and colleagues predicted that if

working memory is the source of the understanding deficit then the patients

should be more impaired in the second sentence. However, understanding

can also be impaired by problems in understanding semantic relations of

agents and contexts. Evaluate, for example, the sentence “It was the

excited boy that the girl watched.” This sentence was also presented by

Charles et al. and has the same length as the two aforementioned sen-

tences. In this sentence, however, the agents are changing (there is a cleft

shifting from “excited boy” to “watching girl”). Therefore this sentence

can only be understood if the recipients can follow the contextual structure

of its meaning.

The result of this study was clear-cut. All PPA patient groups had

more problems in understanding sentences than the healthy controls. All

patient groups also had problems with parentheses in the center of a sen-

tence but had no specific problems with the length of the sentences. On

the other hand, only the performance of the nfPPA group decreased sig-

nificantly relative to the existence of agents or context shift by clefts in

the sentences. Thus the verbal understanding of all patients’ groups

dropped relative to the existence of an interruption of the sentence but

only the nfPPA group suffered from additional problems when the gram-

matical structure forced them to reorganize the sentence according to

meaning changes.

5.3.4 Other cognitive features of nonfluent primary progressive aphasia

In the study from Gunawardena et al. (2010), bvFTD and nfPPA patients

were both impaired in categorial word fluency and in phonological word flu-

ency, however, nfPPA patients were significantly more impaired during

word fluency tasks than bvFTD patients. nfPPA patients also suffered from

higher deficits in the Stroop interference task (a test that involves naming),

in the trail making test B (TMT B; a test that involves verbal working mem-

ory), and in the Boston naming task. On the other hand, bvFTD patients

showed significantly higher deficits in accessing semantic relations of pre-

sented pictures compared to nfPPA patients. This pattern of results highlights

the dominant problem of nfPPA patients, accessing, and maintaining the pho-

nological word form, in contrast to their better semantic understanding.

Marra et al. (2007) compared bvFTD, nfPPA, and AD patients using

numerous neuropsychological tests. As expected, AD patients generally

exhibited significantly higher impairments in delayed recall and recognition

tasks. nfPPA patients were impaired in phonological word fluency and

Stroop interference compared to the two other groups. These results concur

with those from Gunawardena et al. (2010). According to Blair, Marczinski,

Davis-Faroque, and Kertesz (2007), who compared patients with nfPPA,

bvFTD and AD over a period of 2 years, the language abilities of patients

with nfPPA decline faster than in bvFTD and AD patients.

Butts et al. (2015) compared the three variants of PPA by means of sev-

eral neuropsychological tests. In memory tests, nfPPA patients outperformed

the lPPA patients, showing no difference to the SD patients. One exception,

however, was the ReyOsterrieth figure copying test in which SD patients

achieved significantly better scores than nfPPA patients. Moreover the

nfPPA patients were almost significantly slower in the TMT A tests com-

pared to the SD patients. For the language tasks, nfPPA produced fewer

words in phonological word fluency compared to the SD patients.

Showing more pronounced declines of language functions and abilities

for communicating, nfPPA patients are relatively free of behavioral distur-

bances. Marra et al. (2007) not only relied on neuropsychological tests but

also included the neuropsychiatric inventory to compare nfPPA, bvFTD, and

AD patients. nfPPA were almost free of any behavioral disorder, except for

showing increased apathy. On the other hand, bvFTD patients documented

more agitated behavior, apathy, disinhibition, and eating disorders.

5.4 Semantic dementia

5.4.1 Clinical core features

The clinical features of SD are impairments in confrontation naming, single-

word comprehension, and one of following three: poor object or person

knowledge, surface dyslexia, spared repetition, and spared motor speech.

The diagnostic criteria for SD can be found in Table 5.5. Gorno-Tempini

et al. (2011) stated that for the diagnosis of SD both of the following core

features must be present: impaired confrontation naming and impaired

single-word comprehension. At least three of the following other diagnostic

features must also be present: impaired object knowledge (particularly for

low frequency or low-familiarity items), surface dyslexia or dysgraphia,

spared repetition, spared speech production (grammar and motor speech).

5.4.2 Cognitive impairments in semantic dementia

The impairment in confrontation naming and reading is not due to impaired

access to the phonological word form but due to a loss of the recognition of

the meaning of the perceived objects. Thus it is caused by a progressive

deterioration of the semantic memory store. In one of the first systematic

papers on SD, Hodges, Spatt, and Patterson (1999) compared two patients

with this clinical syndrome and one patient with corticobasal degeneration or

corticobasal syndrome (CBD/CBS) (compare Chapter 4: Atypical

Parkinson’s diseases: progressive supranuclear paralysis and corticobasal

degeneration). They used naming, functional semantics, real tool use, and

novel tool use in their comparison. Naming concerned 20 pictures, functional

their meaning, real tool use concerned the use of well-known objects and

novel tool use concerned the use of newly constructed tools (Fig. 5.5).

5.4.3 Phonological working memory impairment in semantic dementia

Returning to the seminal work of Alan Baddeley, cognitive neuropsycholo-

gists measure phonological working memory using the digit span tasks.

During the last four decades, it has become apparent that the phonological

buffer and the phonological loop are not only influenced by formal charac-

teristics of the word sounds (compare Section 5.5. on lPPA which is dis-

cussed later) but also by comprehension of the words and their semantic

relations. Storing sequences of words in the phonological working memory

shows a similar course as the learning of wordlists revealing an impact of

proactive and retroactive interference. High frequency words and words with

high imageability can be retained better than infrequent words or abstract

words. The maximal pseudoword span is smaller than the maximal word

span, arguing for an impact of the meaning of the word for its reproduction.

As argued earlier, a progressive loss of word meaning together with an unim-

paired phonological word lexicon is a major problem of SD. This can be used

for differential diagnosis because the two other PPAs do not show problems in

single-word comprehension (at least not in the beginning of the disease). The

impact of understanding the meaning of words on verbal working memory per-

formance may also be even more specific. Brain atrophy in SD is concentrated

in the anterior and inferior temporal lobe, whereas brain atrophy in lPPA is

focused in the parietotemporal junction. The former brain region is related to the

meaning objects, whereas the latter region is related to the understanding and use

of numbers and calculation. Jefferies, Patterson, Jones, Bateman, and Lambon

Ralph (2004) published a paper on verbal short-term memory in SD based on

the performance of four patients. They were able to show that the patients were

unimpaired in digit span forwards. Even using higher numbers (above nine) SD

patients did not differ from healthy controls in repeating digit span sequences.

However, matching words of similar frequency with numbers revealed that SD

patients were impaired in comparison to healthy controls. The severity of the

impairment increased with low frequent, low imageable (abstract) words, and

decreased for frequent words. The deficit in word comprehension had a direct

impact on the word span, which insinuates that the phonological buffer is

endorsed by the semantic structure of the to-be-remembered items.

Further AD patients suffer from an increasing loss of semantic memory,

although the onset of this only occurs in later phases. Several studies have

shown that AD patients present deficits in semantic word fluency but not so

much in phonological word fluency (see Sections 1.6.4 and 2.4 on AD for a

further discussion). If this is correct, then AD patients should also show a rela-

tive decline in their ability to repeat low imageable words compared to high

imageable words. Peters, Majerus, De Baerdemaeker, Salmon, and Collette

(2009) compared two word-span task performances in healthy elderly partici-

pants and AD patients for high and low imageable words (Fig. 5.8). AD

patients and elderly participants recalled word sequences worse than young

participants, however, they did not differ in their performance for high image-

5.5.3 Episodic memory deficits in logopenic primary progressive aphasia

To what extent patients with lPPA suffer from memory impairments has been

answered with somewhat contradictory results. A recent review argues that lPPA

patients suffer from impairments in delayed free recall, especially in the context

of verbal learning tasks (Eikelboom et al., 2018). Some studies reviewed by

Eikelboom et al. (2018) showed that verbal recognition performance in lPPA

patients is not disturbed. Moreover according to these studies, brain atrophy was

restricted to areas outside the mediobasal temporal lobe. Because the neuropatho-

logical cascade of AD starts much earlier than visible or quantifiable brain atro-

phy, the latter finding does not exclude hippocampal dysfunctions. Butts et al.

(2015) compared lPPA, nfPPA, and SD patients for verbal and visual memory

performance. nfPPA outperformed lPPA patients in most verbal memory tests,

whereas the difference in visual reproduction was less pronounced. Other studies

focused explicitly on nonverbal memory performance to exclude the language

impairment of this patient group as a confounding factor. Ramanan et al. (2016)

discovered that the most powerful discriminator between nfPPA and lPPA

patients was a nonverbal recall measure (Rey Complex Figure delayed recall),

with 81% of PPA patients correctly classifying at the presentation. AD and lPPA

patients performed comparably on this measure. The findings demonstrate that

nonverbal recall emerges as the best discriminator of lPPA and nfPPA when con-

trolling for language deficits. Unfortunately the Rey Complex Figure recall may

also involve verbal labeling of aspects of the whole figure during the copying

process, which may increase delayed recall performance. Moreover recognition

testing is not part of the usual application of this memory test and there are no

population norms for recognition performance available.

Patients with surface dyslexia try to pronounce words by graphemephoneme

correspondence rules and are therefore slow in reading and have problems espe-

cially in producing irregularly spoken words when reading them. Reading speed

decreases with increasing word length since they are unable to read automati-

cally. Therefore they use graphemephoneme correspondence rules to come up

with a phonological interpretation of the specific letter combination (the word

they are reading) that fits a possible semantic meaning of the word. Patients

with deep dyslexia (the opposite pattern of impairment) are unable to read pseu-

dowords because their deficit concerns the knowledge about the grapheme phoneme correspondence rules. They read automatically, without using the

rules, by their extensive knowledge of the words. Automatic processing depends

on how often one has done a specific task. Therefore patients with deep dys-

lexia have problems reading words they have not encountered often during their

life (e.g., foreign words) or that carry an abstract meaning (infrequently read).

Some patients with deep dyslexia not only lose the graphemephoneme corre-

spondence rules but also suffer from problems accessing the semantic meaning

of the words. As these patients read based on automatic phonological knowl-

edge, the impairment is often called phonological dyslexia instead of deep dys-

lexia. Because such patients do not understand the words they are reading, they

have problems with ambiguous interpretation of syllables and words (the

German words “Baum’art” and “Bau’markt” are good examples of needing to

know the meaning of the words to come up with a correct phonological

interpretation).

5.8 Treating the language variants of dementia

Patients with SD and nfPPA do not suffer from AD and therefore the acetyl-

cholinesterase inhibitors (AChEI) are not helpful in these patients. Treating

lPPA patients with AChEI may be tried out, but there are no clear-cut data

indicating that such medication is helpful for the language symptoms of this

patient group (Kertesz et al., 2008). At least one neuropathological study has

shown that patients with lPPA suffer from massive cortical cholinergic

denervation, which suggests that AChEI may have an impact in lPPA

(Mesulam, Lalehzari, et al., 2019). Studies that used memantine revealed

unclear results (Boxer et al., 2009; Chow et al., 2011).

During the last years, a number of single case studies have been pub-

lished using different cueing methods originally developed for stroke patients

with aphasia (Beeson et al., 2011; Louis et al., 2001; Macoir et al., 2015;

Shankle et al., 2009; Tsapkini et al., 2018). The global scheme of the cueing

or semantic therapy approach is to help patients pronounce words using addi-

tional information (picture, sentence completion, writing, reading, etc.). This

procedure is repeated several times for the same word and the cues are

reduced step by step (if possible). Cueing facilitates word retrieval and can

have enduring as well as partially generalizing effects on words that have

not been treated. Most of these single case studies show that cueing may

improve the language abilities of the patients with different language variants

of dementia; however, one open parallel study with a nontreated control

group was unable to find a delay of decline in the treatment group (Farrajota

et al., 2012).

There are several studies, which combine transcranial direct current stim-

ulation and speech therapy in patients with different variants of PPA (Ficek

et al., 2018; Gervits et al., 2016; Wang, Wu, Chen, Yuan, & Zhang, 2013).

Tsapkini and Hillis (2013) performed an RCT cross-over design including 36

patients who received 15 daily sessions of stimulation coupled with speech

therapy. Twenty of the patients received the combined treatment, whereas

the remaining sixteen received a sham tDCS but the same amount of speech

therapy. The anodal electrode was placed at F7 according to the 10/20 EEG

electrode position system and the patients were stimulated with an intensity

of 2 mA for 20 minutes. Speech therapy included the task to name and spell

pictures presented on a computer screen. If the patients were not able to

name the pictures, they were asked to describe their function or to give the

name of similar object. If they still failed to produce the word orally, they

were provided with the correct word and were asked to repeat the word three

times. As a next step the patients were asked to write the name of the object.

In the event that the patients were unable to write down the name or wrote it

incorrectly, the clinician wrote the word and the patients were asked to copy

the word three times. After 3 weeks of treatment (15 sessions), the patients

were assessed for trained words and for untrained word. Assessment was

repeated 2 weeks after the first treatment period and then the patients

received the other intervention (changing from real tDCS plus speech ther-

apy to sham tDCS and speech therapy or vice versa.) Two months after the

second treatment there was a follow-up assessment. Patients receiving real

tDCS and speech therapy improved significantly for trained and untrained

items when compared to the group only receiving sham tDCS and speech

therapy. Furthermore the higher benefit was stable 2 months later at the

follow-up visit. The significant add-on effect was not present for the patients

that first received sham tDCS and speech therapy and later on real tDCS and

speech therapy. In the cross-over phase, both groups improved almost simi-

larly. Comparing the different clinical groups, the nfPPA patient seem to

benefit most, the lPPA also benefitted but to a smaller extent and the SD

group showed no increase in function.

Although the number of treated patients is still small, tDCS combined

with speech therapy seems to be a possibility to delay the function loss in

language, at least for the nfPPA and lPPA patients. It would be interesting to

know if the SD patients had been more successful if the temporal cortex had

been stimulated instead. Cortical atrophy in SD patients is not focused in the

frontal cortex, therefore the design of the Tsapkini and Hillis (2013) study

resulted in the wrong brain region being stimulated via tDCS for this group.

The placing of the electrodes may play a role in explaining the lack of

improvement in the SD group; however, the specific impairment itself may

also explain the disappointing result for this group. SD patients suffer from a

deficit in the semantic interpretation and understanding of words. During the

study the patients were treated by naming objects and writing down these

names. Naming is based on the process of retrieving the word and of identi-

fying the object. The last process is no problem for the nfPPA and the lPPA

patients, who are unable to retrieve the phonological word form, whereas the

SD patients would be able to retrieve the word, if they understand the mean-

ing of the object. In a review Cadorio, Lousada, Martins, and Figueiredo

(2017) analyzed the effect of cueing or semantic therapy in patients with the

three variants of PPA without additional noninvasive brain stimulation.

Semantic therapy concerns lexical retrieval (word finding) during confronta-

tion naming, semantic elaboration of target concepts (a lion is an animal

that. . .), comparing and contrasting stimuli (the difference between a lion

and a tiger is. . .), and sorting pictures and words by semantic subcategory.

Thus the treatment in the Tsapkini et al. (2018) study discussed earlier com-

bined semantic therapy with tDCS. Cadorio et al. (2017) found that lPPA

and nfPPA patients benefit from semantic therapy, for example, improve

their naming abilities also for words that had not been part of the training

set, whereas SD patients do not show generalizsation. A second result of the

review was that in all three patient groups the training effects endured for at

least 1 month; however, this concerned only the trained items in the SD

patients. Obviously patients with SD need another kind of cognitive treat-

ment as patients with nfPPA and lPPA. In the case of SD, recognizing the

objects to be named might already be a prominent problem and object identi-

fication is not a primary target of semantic therapy. It should be added that

nfPPA patients show agrammatism and may have more problems with verbs

(naming of actions) than with nouns. For lPPA not the word class, but the

length of the verbal statement may play a role (due to their prominent verbal

working memory deficit). Thus in future, the treatment of speech therapy

should be adapted for the different variants of the PPA and for patients with

pure word-finding deficits semantic therapy might be the best option.

Script training may be such a more specific approach focusing on the

loss of pronunciation. In a typical script training, patients with aphasia see a

model that speaks the words that should be produced. Via verbal shadowing

and by a process of imitation of the mouth movement the patients are helped

to speak the words themselves. Intensive training of specific scripts can lead

to the ability to pronounce the words without the model. Moreover seeing

and hearing the model during own speech can be helpful to tell something to

others, which has been prepared before by the help of professionals or rela-

tives. Hubbard, Nelson, and Richardson (2020) reviewed the evidence not

only for script training in acute aphasics but also for the small number of

studies on PPA. At least in nfPPA the results are promising, however, still

based on single case series. Interestingly SD do not benefit from script train-

ing and lPPA often do not engage in watching the articulatory movements,

implemented in script training, but more often use reading cues, if getting

access to this technology. For lPPA patients articulation is not the major

problem, it is the retrieval of words in cognitively challenging situations.

Thus also the results of script training point into the direction that patients

with different variants of aphasia also need different approaches in their

treatment. It would be interesting to see whether lPPA patients would benefit

especially from a sentence processing training (Thompson, 2019), where the

patients are taught to increase the complexity of the agent—theme relation in

a sentence, or from a verbal working memory.

5.9 Summary

There are three different variants of PPA: nfPPA, SD, and lPPA. Patients

with SD show fluent speech but impairments in understanding and often

visual agnosia. Recognition memory is not or only mildly impaired. Patients

with nfPPA display (subtle) speech disorders (dysarthria, apraxia of speech,

effortful speech), slow speech production, impairments in phonological word

fluency and in copying tasks, as long as they include a planning component.

The lPPA variants is characterized by word-finding difficulties whereas

286 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

patients show fluent speech production. These patients show severe impair-

ments in verbal working memory (digit span forward as measure of the pho-

nological store) and mild impairments in verbal recognition tasks. Patients

with SD are characterized by problems understanding single words. They

may also exhibit visual agnosia and are unable to identify pictures. Their

copying performance, on the other hand, may be almost perfect. Assessment

of patients with PPA should entail a free description of a standardized pic-

ture to get a clinical impression of spontaneous speech.

#### Reading and writing skills are helpful for the differential diagnosis:

patients with SD show surface dyslexia and dysgraphia, whereas patients

with lPPA and nfPPA might display phonological dyslexia.

NfPPA and lPPA may be treated by facilitation of words using different

modalities such as semantic, phonologic, or action-related facilitation cues.

There are some single case studies which imply that this technique, used in

patients with aphasia due to stroke, may also be helpful in reducing the pro-

gression rate in nonfluent patients. A recent study combining tDCS and

intensive speech therapy revealed that disease progression in lPPA and

nfPPA may be delayed by such a combined treatment. SD patients did not

benefit from that approach, however, this could have been due to the specific

protocol for the tDCS intervention (not stimulating the primary brain regions

affected in SD). In the case of nfPPA patients script training might be tried

out to improve speech and word finding. Up to now there are no convincing

treatment studies for SD patients.

5.10 Cerebral atrophy and neuropathology

Neuropathologically the three variants of the PPAs may be due to different

neurodegenerative diseases, but there is an affinity of lPPA to show AD

the tauopathies, more specifically nfPPA shows a four-repeat tauopathy. A

somewhat closer look on the neuropathological processes related to these dis-

5.11 The language variants of dementia and the acquired aphasia

The description and explanation of acquired language disorders have a long-

standing history and belong to the core of cognitive neuropsychology. The

goal is to reconstruct the behavior of the patients by postulating processing

modules and possible interactions between these modules, which have

become selectively impaired by brain damage. With respect to the acquired

language disorders, two well-known neurologists Paul Broca and Carl

Wernicke were the first starting with this approach of localization of lan-

guage functions based on specific impairment patterns. There are several

reviews regarding the development of such explanatory models and Heilman

(2006) published an excellent one, summarizing the progress focusing exclu-

sively on the clinical data, that is, on patients with a vascular cause for their

language deficit. Only a few years before this review was published, system-

atic research on the different PPAs started. From a neuropathological view,

there is a clear and outstanding difference between these two causes for

aphasia: vascular lesions almost inevitably involve also white matter struc-

tures and not only cortical areas. Thus vascular lesions concern the proces-

sing modules and the connections between them. On the other hand, they are

focal lesions. Neurodegenerative pathology primarily affects cortical neurons

including their dendrites and axons and not independent white matter tracts.

Although it may start in specific areas of the brain, at a stage in which a

patient shows clinical impairment, many areas of the brain will already be

involved, albeit with different intensity. Thus in some respect, they suffer

from cortical lesions which are less focal than those after a stroke.

In the beginning of this chapter we used a vascular schema to classify the

aphasias into three different neurodegenerative variations. In the following para-

graphs we will further discuss the similarities and differences between the vascu-

lar and the neurodegenerative aphasias and base this discussion, for example, on

the excellent paper from Mesulam, Thompson, Weintraub, and Rogalski (2015).

Speech effort, reduced fluency, one-word answers, and mild to moderate

problems in understanding sentence constructions has been defined as the core

feature of Broca’s aphasia. The nfPPA variant matches this description and its

localization (Fig. 5.18). Atrophy in nfPPA is focused in the inferior and opercu-

lar frontal cortex. The patients not only suffer from speech effort, reduced

speech velocity, and simplified sentences constructions but also from word-

finding difficulties. There are three different kinds of impairments in word pro-

duction, which should be distinguished. The first impairment concerns the

access to the phonological representation of the to-be-produced word. Most of

these patients can determine the meaning of these words with the help of pic-

tures or if asked to match appropriate pictures to written words. Many of these

patients are also able to avoid accessing the missing word by using circumlocu-

tion. The main problem then is to recover the phonological word representation

Primary progressive aphasias Chapter | 5 291

during the intention and having already started to speak. One may call such an

impairment a one-way deficit: it is present mainly if the task is to access the

phonological representation from “inside.” There is also another group of

patients that can write down the words but are unable to articulate them.

Obviously these patients know what they want to say but they are unable to

recover the motor engrams of the words. They suffer from apraxia of speech

and/or from dysarthria (if also or mainly unable to articulate the words). As a

consequence, understanding language production with the help of a flow-chart

diagram (Fig. 5.18, left stippled rectangle) presupposes at least three different

processing units on the production side:

 A storage or specific connecting fiber pattern representing the phonologi-

cal word output lexicon. Damage to this storage or connection pattern

will lead to word-finding difficulties and circumlocution of the missing

word. Moreover these patients might suffer from activation of semanti-

cally related words and produce semantic paraphasias.

“One-way” word-finding deficits, apraxia of speech and dysarthria have

been linked in stroke patients to lesions of the premotor frontal and anterior

insular cortex, areas which are often affected by atrophy in nfPPA patients.

Atrophy of neuronal repertoires is responsible for accessing and producing

words, however, it is not the only feature of nfPPA. These patients also have

problems understanding sentences. A first explanation of this problem might

be that these patients show working memory impairments. Therefore they

get lost when hearing long sentences. However, this is probably not the only

explanation, otherwise patients with severe verbal working memory deficits

would show similar difficulties in sentences understanding, which is not the

case. Such patients (e.g., lPPA patients) have problems attending to long sen-

tences. The problem of nfPPA, however, is related to the grammatical con-

struction of the sentence (Charles et al., 2014). A more precise description of

the nfPPA sentence comprehension problem is

 the understanding of semantic relations encompassing different agents or

contexts in one message. Such an integration depends on grammar and

the proper interpretation of the word’s function. It has been shown for

stroke patients with Broca’s aphasia that they are impaired in compre-

hending such sentences.

This might also be a reason why nfPPA patients focus on single-word

answers, avoiding (complicated) sentence structures. They compensate their

loss of automatic grammatical knowledge.

Language production problems do not necessarily involve grammatical

contextual understanding and sequencing of words as in nfPPA. They

might also be restricted to the production of single words (word-finding

problems) and understanding complex sentences irrespective of their

grammatical construction. These are the two major problems of anomic

aphasia and lPPA (Fig. 5.18, stippled rectangle in the center). Both pro-

blems are not necessarily linked: there may be a problem (1) in accessing

the phonological word form for its production independent from (2)

understanding complex meaning structures. We already dealt with and

tried to explain the former problem above. From a differential neuropsy-

chological point accessing the phonological word form is the leading defi-

cit in lPPA. These patients are already impaired in repeating sentences,

although they often understand their meaning. Thus they are able to pro-

cess the verbal input on a phonological input level and to forward the

result of such processing to the conceptual units defining the semantic

understanding of the words. As these patients start to produce language,

which is grammatically correct and answers the questions, and because

they try to exchange the lacking word with a correct and understandable

word during circumlocution, the semantic module must be intact.

However, the specific phonological sound patterns fade prematurely from

working memory disabling word repetition. This can be explained by a

deficit in the phonological buffer. Earlier we discussed related findings

for lPPA patients using different verbal span tasks. The impairment in the

phonological buffer explains why these patients may not understand

grammatically easy albeit long sentences: the integration of the different

meaning substructure into one central concept which exceeds their work-

ing memory performance. In this respect there are no significant differ-

ences between these patients and patients suffering from anomic aphasia

due to stroke, however, anomic patients do not necessarily suffer from

sentence repetition deficits.

Which structure in Fig. 5.18 corresponds to the phonological buffer

(compare Section 5.5.2 of this chapter), a concept developed in experimen-

tal psychology outside the field of research on aphasia? We can exclude

the conceptual module (comprehension per se is intact), we can exclude the

phonological input lexicon (the patients understand words) and we can

exclude the phonological output lexicon (there is only a partial correlation

between word-finding and repetition problems). This leads to the conclu-

sion that in aphasiological terms the phonological buffer concerns the inter-

action of different modules. Phonological working memory is influenced

by phonological input representation, semantic knowledge concerning the

to-be-repeated items, and the velocity of speaking.

As reviewed earlier, patients with lPPA and with nfPPA often suffer

from phonological or deep dyslexia and not from surface dyslexia. They

have problems coming up with phonological interpretations of pseudo-

words as well as problems with reading abstract, infrequent words,

although they can read and understand frequent words at a normal speed.

Their reading problems clearly resemble their language production pro-

blems: no problems processing the phonological word form, but problems

using routines relying on rule production systems and/or working memory

performance. As long as understanding and production is possible without

rule-based systems, lPPA and nfPPA patients they can correctly interact

verbally (but with the syndrome specific restrictions). As soon as rule-

based systems (grammatic sequencing, unusual passive sentence structures

involving reconstruction of meaning in working memory, etc.) are neces-

sary, they fail in verbal comprehension. The problem of relying on rules

for correcting automatic language production may also explain the phone-

matic errors produced by lPPA patients. High frequent associations of syl-

lables and phonological sound structures may intervene with the correct

phonological structure of the word and the impairment of rule-based

tions. Consequently the patients produce primarily phonological errors

Precedingly we focused on language production, let us now turn to the

question of verbal comprehension. This is the domain in which the most

obvious differences exist between acquired stroke-based and neurodegenera-

tive causes for aphasia. If the source of the aphasia is a neurodegenerative

process, the clinical syndrome is termed SD. After a stroke, the aphasia is

referred to as Wernicke’s aphasia. Patients suffering from either SD or

#### Wernicke’s aphasia differ in the following aspects:

 Wernicke’s aphasics do not understand sentences and words, whereas SD

patients do not understand words (but seem to understand sentences).

 Patients with SD are typically not impaired in repetition of words and

sentences, whereas patients with Wernicke’s aphasics are impaired in this

 Wernicke’s aphasics show severe patterns of paragrammatism and jargon,

whereas SD patients produce semantic paraphasia but rarely jargon.

 SD patients often try to correct themselves or search for the correct word,

whereas Wernicke’s aphasics have no insight in their language problems.

Thus the main difference between SD patients and Wernicke’s aphasics

is that SD patients suffer from a loss of understanding the meaning of words

(conceptual semantic word meaning system), whereas Wernicke’s aphasics

suffer from a sentence understanding deficit (forwarding the information

from the phonological input system to the meaning module) and occasionally

of single words (Fig. 5.18).

Early in the disease, the comprehension deficit of the SD patients com-

prises only infrequent and low imageable words. In this stage, wrong but

semantically related words may become activated, therefore SD patients

produce primarily semantic paraphasia. Later on, however, the specific

instances of categories are also involved. This leads to an increasing usage

of global and descriptive words (instead of goose - animal, etc.). If the

understanding of some (an increasing number of) single words is impaired,

hearing the context of the words, that is, the sentences they are embedded

in, is helpful because the interpretation of single words is easier when more

information is present. Therefore understanding sentences is less impaired

in SD patients compared to understanding words. Moreover being able to

understand on a phonological level and noticing that the to-be-conveyed

message cannot be understood on this level will lead to correcting attempts.

These correction attempts can be observed in SD but not in Wernicke’s

In the case of Wernicke’s aphasics, the access to the phonological

representation of words is impaired. Thus they do not understand

sentences as they are unable to access their meaning based on the

sequences of spoken or written words. Having lost the ability to parse the

phonological representations, they are unaware that their verbal output has

changed, therefore they may speak fluently but unintelligibly. In addition,

the loss of the phonological representation of words causes the output to

become phonologically disturbed by jargon and neologisms. Intending to

speak and suffering from problems in processing the phonological word

form on the conceptual level and at the same time in accessing the phono-

logical word form may be called a “two-way” word-finding deficit: input

and output from the conceptual level is impaired. In reality, Wernicke’s

aphasics sometimes understand words, but not sentences, quite well.

Automatic understanding of high frequency words seems to be possible

without accessing the phonological representation of these words (like in

phonological dyslexia) (this automatic understanding is represented by

the rightmost blue arrow in Fig. 5.18). As the conceptual level in pure

Wernicke’s aphasia is not impaired, the understanding of some words is

still possible.

As stated earlier, a major difference between Wernicke’s aphasics and

SD patients is that the former engage in neologism and jargon, whereas SD

patients do not. However, there are situation in which SD patients start to

develop Wernicke’s like paraphasia as well. Jefferies, Grogan, Mapelli, and

Isella (2012) used paced reading to analyze the impact of meaning loss on

the phonological output lexicon. Paced reading should lead to more stress in

patients that have impaired access to word meaning. Because the meaning of

the word restricts its phonological interpretation, paced reading may lead to

reading errors. Possible interpretations of the words start to compete due to

the restriction of time for reading and activated letters might migrate

between words. Therefore the number of phonologic paraphasia should

increase. Jefferies et al. (2012) also varied the composition of wordlists:

there were some lists with high frequency words and some with low fre-

quency words. SD patients were expected to have more problems under-

standing low frequency words. As such the wrong migration of letters

between words should happen more often in low frequency wordlists. The

results corresponded with their expectations: SD patients produced extensive

phonological errors in low frequency words. Thus Jefferies et al (2012)

found that phonological paraphasia is due to an impairment of semantic

understanding in paced word reading.

Furthermore when discussing these differences, Mesulam et al. (2015)

mentioned that the existence of SD also suggests that language comprehen-

sion is based on neuroanatomical areas, which goes beyond the assumptions

based on Wernicke’s aphasia: the anterior parts of the temporal lobe seem to

play a major role in word understanding, which had not been noticed based

on lesion data from acute stroke.
