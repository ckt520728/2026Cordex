How Your Brain Works

The brain is considered as a very complex organ, and there are still millions of processes and functions of the brain that cannot be fully understood by humans up to now. The human brain is one of the most unique things ever found on earth. Though it is not the largest in all the animals, it enables people to imagine various scenarios and solve problems. Here are just a number of things that the human brain is capable of doing:

The brain accepts and processes a myriad of information from the different senses, such as various tastes, sounds, sights, smells, and textures (sense of touch) all at the same time.

The brain enables people to feel various emotions, reason out and dream.

The brain controls the heart rate, body temperature, breathing, and blood pressure.

The brain is responsible for handling physical movements like sitting, walking, standing, and talking.

All of these activities are regulated and controlled by an organ that is just about the size of small cauliflower head. The following section describes parts of the central nervous system.

The Central Nervous System

The Central Nervous System (CNS) is responsible for a complex network of organs that work together to process the different activities that go on in the human body. The spinal cord, brain, and the peripheral nerves form an integrated information control and processing system. These structures are able to work together to regulate the different unconscious and conscious processes of the body. Neurobiology or neuroscience is considered as the scientific study of the nervous system and the brain. Though there have been a lot of breakthroughs and discoveries in this field, the human brain is still not fully understood, which is why millions of experts continuously find ways to understand the brain better.

The basic unit of the nervous system is known as the neuron, or the nerve cell. The brain is comprised of about 100 billion neurons. These basic structures are able to get and transmit different electrochemical signals that will then prompt the body to act accordingly.  Though neurons have the same characteristics and makeup with the other cells in the body, they have an electrochemical property that helps them to send messages to other neurons, as well as send signals from up to a few meters away.

Neurons are what they call “electrically excitable cells.” Neurons come in different types that are specialized in nature.  There are sensory neurons that respond to light, touch, sound, and other stimuli that make themselves felt through the sensory organs. The neurons send the signals to the spinal cord, which then transports to the brain. Motor neurons are the neurons that receive the signals coming from the spinal cord and brain, which then affect the output of glands and also cause muscles to contract.  In neural networks, interneurons connect different neurons with each other that are inside the same region of the spinal cord or brain.

Neurons are not capable of undergoing cell division. In general, neurons arise through special types of stem cells. Astrocytes, which are a type of glial cell, have been seen to have the capability of turning into neurons through pluripotency, which is a characteristic of stem cells. Once a human reaches adulthood, neurogenesis stops in almost all parts of the body, except for the olfactory bulb and the hippocampus. Studies have found that there are a lot of new neurons that are generated even among adults in the said areas.

#### There are 3 basic parts of a neuron:

Soma or the Cell Body

This contains the needed components in a cell, including the nucleus, ribosomes, mitochondria, and endoplasmic reticulum. Once the cell body dies, the entire neuron also dies.

Nerve Endings or Dendrites

These are the branch-like projections at the endings of the cells that are responsible for making connections with other cells. These nerve endings also help the neuron to be able to communicate with other cells, as well as “feel” the environment.

This is the long projection (similar to a cable) that is responsible for carrying the nerve impulse or the action potential along the cell. Axons could either be covered by a myelin sheath or not, depending on the type of neuron. Myelin is comprised of protein and fat and helps to hasten the transmission of an impulse in a long axon. Peripheral nerves typically have myelinated neurons (motor and sensory neurons), while the spinal cord and brain have non-myelinated neurons.

Chapter 2 – Training Your Brain

Enhancing the power of your brain is possible through proper and constant training.  We should maximize the capacity of our brain.  Below are practical and effective techniques that you can use to train your brain.

The Loci Method

A popular memory-enhancing technique is known as “Loci Method.” This is an ancient but effective method in training the brain.   A Greek philosopher named Simonedes developed it.  The word “Loci” comes from the Latin word “locus” which means location. This method has to do with spatial association between a place and the content that needs to be memorized.

To illustrate the Loci technique, Simonedes numbered the different locations in his home then reviewed several numbers in his mind in each location. For example, if you asked him what location were the numbers 25, 58, 77, 124 and 187, he could tell you the rooms where those numbers are associated.  He also tried another strategy where he associated words with certain locations in his house.  Research shows that the memorized items are what they call “mental files.” By having a list, Simonedes was able to recall the objects he had in his home. This technique can also be used in a practical manner, such as when you do your weekly groceries or reviewing for an exam.

Meditation is a practice where you train your mind to achieve something.  It comes from the Latin word “meditation” which means to think, contemplate or ponder.  Experts are convinced that meditation has a lot of positive impacts on the body, particularly the brain. There are a lot of professionals, celebrities, and even those in the business world who regularly have meditation sessions because they have felt the different positive effects of meditation.

Why Meditate?

Research has proven scientifically that meditation is a simple and safe way to achieve balance in one's emotional, physical, and mental states. More and more doctors are even encouraging their patients to meditate in order to cure illnesses that are stress-related. Most people experience different types of stress every single day, from work and even at one's home. Studies have shown that one of the biggest positive impacts of meditation on the body is how it helps the body let go of the stresses it has held on to.

In normal situations, life-threatening events trigger the stress response that then helps the body to act without thinking.   Thus, people survive situations due to fast reflexes. The fight-or-flight reaction (or otherwise known as adrenaline rush) also arises when people are exposed to situations that are unexpected. When the adrenal glands release adrenaline and other hormones enter the blood stream, blood pressure, respiratory rate, pulse, and blood flow towards the muscles increase. When a person is in grave danger, these reactions can help one to survive. But when people receive too much stress constantly and are unable to de-stress themselves, their fight-or-flight reactions are diminished, their bodies and minds become too stressed, and this could pose dangers to the health.

Additional Benefits of Meditation

Through meditation, people will be able to achieve a deeper sense of contemplation and relaxation. For people who wish to be free of constant stress, worry and pressure, meditation can help them become a lot more relaxed, calm, happy, and at peace. Researchers have found that at least 10 minutes of meditation every day can help to release stress. Here are other positive impacts of meditation to a person:

It helps to lower blood lactate levels in the body, which then helps to reduce anxiety attacks.

Meditation helps to increase serotonin production in the body, which is responsible for regulation of behavior and moods. People who have low serotonin levels are more likely to suffer from headaches, depression, and insomnia.

It helps to build self-esteem or self-confidence.

It helps to reduce tension and stress.

It helps to increase one's vigor, energy, and strength.

It strengthens the mind and increases concentration.

It helps to create a feeling of deep relaxation and general well being.

It helps people to lose weight.

It helps to keep the blood pressure normal.

It helps to decrease the incidence of heart disease.

Regular practitioners of meditation have reported that they are able to feel a lot calmer and in control of their situations. They also feel happier and more relaxed.  People may also realize that they are able to concentrate better when they meditate regularly. Another benefit of meditation is that people will also learn how to go with the flow and learn how to separate insignificant things from the significant ones.

Neuroplasticity

Have you ever wanted to do something but were afraid to try or got discouraged because it was too difficult to do, like learning a new language, increasing one's ability to focus, and becoming a lot better in problem solving?  What about for patients who want to regain control over body functions after a stroke and gain control over lost brain function?  Now, all these are possible.  The mind is still able to create lasting changes in terms of function due to what they call “neuroplasticity.”

The term is actually derived from the words “plastic” and “neuron”. The term neuron refers to the basic component of the nervous system, as mentioned in the previous chapter. The term “plastic” means to modify, mold, or sculpt. Together, the term neuroplasticity refers to the possibility of the brain to reorganize functions through the creation of new neural pathways in order to adapt to changes as needed.

A good way of thinking how the brain is able to make new neural pathways is by likening it to a radio. When you are trying to find a radio station that you like, you have to turn the knob carefully as you do so. While turning the knob, you may come across one station that you like but with the static, you have to turn the knob slowly digit by digit until you get the clearest reception. In almost the same way, the brain does the same thing when you are trying to learn something new. The more that you put your efforts into focusing on a single thing, the brain also tries to modify the neural pathways in order for the synapses to fire together better. This modification can then help a person to sharpen his or her new skill.

Medical experts used to believe that the brain is not capable of making new neural cells. The old belief was that each person is born with just a number of neurons and when one dies, it cannot be replaced anymore.  There are parts of the brain that can regenerate new cells. Through PET scans and MRI technology, it has been found that new neurons and neural pathways are generated all throughout a person's life. Even those who belong to the older age group even have measurable changes in terms of brain organization. The changes are not that easy to achieve but they can be achieved through focus on an area that is considered as defected.

Spine exercises when done correctly actually help with preventing the onslaught of back disorders. You will be able to see that you have a stronger and healthier spine and nervous system. You will also feel and look better.

Exercises You Can Try

One of the best ways to help the back become healthy and pain-free is to come up with an exercise program that helps to increase the strength and flexibility of the different muscles that support the spine. Medical professionals, as well as chiropractors have recommended the following exercises. It is important to ask help from your physician or chiropractor before you start on any type of fitness program.

Complete Breathing

In order for the body to be fully healthy, aside from the essential water and food, air is important.  Humans are capable of surviving without food for several weeks but just a few days without water. Moreover, humans cannot survive in mere minutes without air to breathe. Life begins and stops with just a single breath.

In breathing, there are 3 different phases that have to be considered: Inhalation, Exhalation and Pause in Breathing.

The inhalation is the phase that forms the active part of breathing. The respiratory muscles contract during this phase, while in the exhalation, the muscles relax.

The first two phases flow into the other without stopping. Each exhalation should be about twice as long as the inhalation and the pause should arise naturally once the exhalation phase is done. The impulse to inhale should then arise naturally. This is what is known as the “Full Yoga Breath” or Complete Breathing. This is how we get the source of life.

Deep, quiet, and regular breathing is needed in order for a person to become healthy. Complete breathing has a calming and harmonizing effect to the well being of a person. When breathing is rapid and shallow, the effects are the opposite: tension, nervousness, pain, and stress are felt. A common mistake in breathing is that the abdomen is drawn in when the chest starts to expand rather than letting the abdomen relax forward. Deep breathing is impaired when you draw your abdomen in. Wearing restrictive clothing also inhibits the natural movements involved in deep breathing.

In Yoga, all exercises, including breathing exercises, should be done slowly and without the extra tension. Breathing should be silent and done through the nose. The air is filtered, warmed, and moistened this way. With all of the exercises in Yoga, it is important that everything is practiced in a state that is physically and mentally relaxed. Muscles can stretch better without any tension when the body is physically relaxed. A mentally relaxed state will help a person to practice the breathing exercises with complete concentration.

Neck Press against an Object

First, lie on your back with a pillow under the head. The head should be in neutral position. Then, push your head firmly down into the pillow in order to lengthen and straighten the spine. You have to keep your chin tucked and your head facing upward. Hold the position for 5 slow counts until the neck muscles become a bit stretched. Relax for a few seconds then repeat 9 more times. You can do this once in the morning and then another in the evening. This is great at releasing tension in your neck.

Extensor Muscle Strengthening Exercise

Start off first by standing straight with a good posture. Then, place a ball behind the back (the upper back). While keeping the feet apart, push the legs and feet hard to press against the ball. The hips, spine, and knees should be in the same position. Hold the position for 5 slow counts with a 2-second rest in between. Repeat ten times. Mental or cognitive distraction is the situation when one's attention is withdrawn from processing information needed to focus on a task being done. It refers to doing different tasks at the same time (multitasking) then a lesser task takes away one's attention from the main task. For example, it happens when a billboard or text message distracts you while you are driving.  Cognitive distraction is dangerous as one's focus should only be on the vehicle. Experts say that the sources of distractions are difficult to measure and observe, as the mental status of a driver is also difficult to assess.

In the world of driving, there is an increasing amount of people who are concerned with various companies taking the initiative to create more hands-free communication devices that could be used while driving, together with entertainment and information systems. These may pose certain dangers as they could distract the minds of drivers from the most important thing: driving safely.

Research says that the brain is not very efficient at multi-tasking. It cannot do two things simultaneously; it only switches rapidly from one task to another. The switching cannot be felt by the person, which is why there is a diminished perception of danger. In cases of driving and texting, drivers can miss up to 50% of the driving environment when they do the two tasks at the same time. According to the study by Carnegie Mellon University, drivers who used cell phones while driving has a diminished brain activity. The parietal lobe, which is connected with the processing of visual images that are moving decreases activity by 37% and that is risky.

Introduction to Memory

Memory is a term that refers to the different processes that are utilized in order to get, store, keep, and retrieve any information. There are three major processes in memory, namely: encoding, storage and retrieval.

In order for people to be able to have new memories, the information gathered should be turned into a usable form. This is made possible through the process known as encoding. Once the gathered information has been encoded successfully, it will then be stored in the memory to be retrieved later on. Most of the stored memory actually lies outside of what we are aware of. The process of retrieval helps to bring the stored memories into our consciousness.

There are different memory models that have been proposed, but the three-stage model is often utilized to show and explain the basic function and structure of memory. It was first proposed by Atkinson and Shiffrin back in 1968, and the theory states that there are 3 different stages of memory: sensory, short-term, and long-term.

“When people ask me if I exercise, I tell them I do crunches everyday – especially Captain Crunch and Nestle Crunch.” – Anonymous fromwww.funnyfitnessquotes.tumblr.comAn aspect of optimal brain health and mental performance that’s often overlooked or taken for granted is physical activity.  Even if people do acknowledge its importance, many still don’t get the connection between the physical and mental aspects of health and performance.

When you exercise regularly, your mental performance benefits in a number of ways including reaction time, perception and interpretation of visuals, mastering a movement or muscle memory and executive processes like working memory, planning and scheduling, inhibition and coordination of many different things.

On a personal level, my mental health and performance is optimal when, among other things, I get to run 5 to 10 kilometers 2 to 3 times every week or bike a total 10 to 20 kilometers every week.  I find that as I’m able to expend much of my pent-up energy through either activity, my self-diagnosed ADHD tends to diminish greatly leading to increased concentration and productivity.  When I lack regular exercise, my self-diagnosed ADHD kicks up several notches, making it very hard for me to sit down long enough to do anything meaningful.

Hmmm…I didn’t mean to conjure images of curly Richard Simmons donned in a tight fitting aero costumes or Jane Fonda in the same get up.  But such exercises that the two people I mentioned and others like it can significantly help you achieve great brain health and mental performance.

Aerobics or “cardio” as others would call it, is a kind of exercise activity that improves the heart’s ability to deliver oxygenated or oxygen-rich blood to muscles by training both the lungs and the heart to work together well to increase the supply of both blood and oxygen to body parts that need it most for longer periods of time.  It also helps the body to release beneficial hormones like adrenaline and endorphins for the nervous system that help boost one’s mood, increase one’s overall sense of well being and relieve pain.

EXERCISE INTENSITY AND DURATION

Your cognitive or mental performance can improve with 20 minutes of moderately intense aerobic activity that lasts for as much as 60 minutes.  Any longer than that and you risk getting fatigued, which is counterproductive in terms of mental performance.  This is the case for average people.  For those who are more fit physically, aerobic activity exceeding 60 minutes can still be beneficial for cognitive or mental performance.  The beneficial effect can be attributed to increased supply of blood to the brain as well as higher levels of neurotransmitters.  In summary, aerobic or cardio sessions between 20 to 60 minutes is optimal for most average people.  If you have an important meeting or job to do later on, limit your preceding session to at most 50 minutes.

How long you exercise is just half of the physical exercise equation, particularly in relation to optimal cognitive performance.  The other half is intensity as measured by age-specific target heart rates, which can be quite technical and cumbersome to explain.  To simplify things, you can use the breath test to measure exercise intensity.  If you’re able to talk normally as if you’re just bumming around or doing nothing, your exercise intensity is low…very low.  If you’re unable to converse normally – catching your breath even just to speak a word or two – while exercising, it means you’re exercising at a high intensity level.  If you’re still able to maintain normal conversation while exercising but with a bit of strain in terms of breathing, that’s moderately intense exercise for you.

Moderate exercise intensity is appropriate intensity level for optimal mental performance.  At this intensity level, your body’s sympathetic nervous system is triggered and the level of your adrenaline is raised – both of which can significantly improve cognitive performance.  If you’re exercising at a low intensity level, no activation of the sympathetic nervous system or increase in adrenaline takes place.  If you exercise at a high intensity level, you’ll tend to need more oxygen and become so tired that you may not be able to concentrate well later on.

Another plus factor for moderate intensity exercise is lower risk of limiting your body’s reproduction of new nerve cells compared to a higher one for high intensity exercises.  Dr. Andrew Naylor, a renowned biologist, conducted an animal study that showed over-exercised animals produced significantly less nerve cells compared to those that didn’t even exercise – only half!  It seems that excessive levels of endorphins and stress hormones can hamper the brain’s ability to create new nerve cells.

In summary, moderately intense aerobics or cardio sessions 4 to 5 times weekly lasting for 30 to 45 minutes each session is optimal for enhancing brain health and mental performance.

LEVEL OF FITNESS

Physical exercises are crucial for maintaining fitness, which is believed to help significantly lower the risks of reduced cognitive performance after the age of 55 compared to those who may be healthy but don’t exercise or aren’t physically fit.  It’s because when you’re fit and exercise regularly, you have good blood circulation to the brain – and that is key to optimal cognitive functioning as you age.

People who exercise regularly for physical fitness also tend to be more conscious about their overall health and as such, are more proactive in managing their lifestyles and diets.  As such, their risk of declining brain health and mental performance are significantly lower than those you aren’t fit  and healthy.

Nutritional Approach To Maximizing Brain Power

There’s a saying that you are what you eat.  To a certain extent, you perform mentally according to what you eat as well.

Nutrition also plays a crucial role in terms of optimizing brain health and mental performance.  Assuming you regularly exercise your mind and body and get enough sleep, you still won’t be able to enjoy optimal cognitive conditioning and performance if you’re significantly lacking in nutrition.  When it comes to optimal mental health and performance, a synergistic cooperation between physical fitness, mental exercises, lifestyle (e.g. sleep and stress) and nutrition.

Aside from mental and physical exercises, you also need to eat the right stuff.  Getting enough important micronutrients can go a long way in terms of achieving and maintaining optimal brain health and mental performance. So if you eat very well, you’ll be able to function very well mentally too.  Eating right is important because key physiological processes – neurotransmitter synthesis and action, neuron and glial cell metabolism, nerve impulse propagation and homocysteine metabolism – that significantly influence your brain health and mental performance are very dependent on getting the right nutrients on a regular basis.

Given the importance of proper nutrition, eating poorly – diets that are high in refined sugars and saturated fats such as cookies and donuts – can significantly impair mental performance.  Consider this:  Have you ever felt sleepy or sluggish after downing a liter of milkshake, eating a battalion of cookies or downing half-a-dozen donuts?  How has that feeling affected your immediate mental performance?  That being said, can you see how eating poorly can impact – even almost immediately – your cognitive performance?  And that’s just for starters!  If you gorge on high sugar treats regularly, it won’t be long until you suffer from diabetes, which has also been known to impair memory over the long term.

It shouldn’t be a surprise that eating right leads or greatly helps improve mental performance.  That being said, here are a couple of ways to eat well for optimal mental flexibility and peak cognitive performance.

FISH FOR MENTAL PERFORMANCE

Eating more of a certain type of fish can go a long way towards optimal brain health and mental performance.  Certain fishes like stripped bass, rainbow trout and wild salmon are rich in Omega 3 fatty acids, which we can’t produce on our own.  Further, these fishes have minimal amounts of mercury compared to tuna.  Omega 3 fatty acids helps maintain healthy cells, including brain cells.  And as you know, healthy cells equal healthy brains equal optimal mental performance.

GREEN MINDED

No, I’m not talking about off-colored jokes or about malicious thoughts.  I’m talking about eating more greens for an optimal mind.  Also, I ain’t referring to a salad made of your mum’s petunias and daisies – believe me when I say they don’t make for a good tasting treat.  It’s all about getting in more fruits and vegetables, both of which are known to make meaningful contributions to optimal mental performance primarily through their rich supplies of anti-oxidants.  These nutrients assist your body in fighting what are called free-radicals – toxic by-products of oxidative stress resulting from your body’s metabolic process.  These radicals are believed to be a big factor in developing Lou Gehrig’s disease, which has been in the forefront of many celebrities’ social media accounts last year in what was called the ice bucket challenge.  These bad radicals are also believed to contribute greatly in the onset of Alzheimer’s disease and Parkinson’s disease – both mental degenerative conditions – among others.

Eat more green leafy stuff and fresh fruits.  Your brain will forever be indebted to you for it if you do.

SUPPLEMENTS

In the last few years, scientific breakthroughs in brain health and mental performance have resulted in the abundance of what you may consider to be “mental steroids”, which are also known popularly as “nootropics”.  Also called “smart drugs”, these supplements come in both herbal and synthetically designed compounds, the most popular ones being NooStax, Alpha Brain and Focus Fast.

The verdict on the efficacy of such supplements are at best, mixed.  If you’re seriously considering using these, do your homework by researching well first prior to buying these supplements, which by the way aren’t cheap.

DRINK TO YOUR BRAIN’S HEALTH

Make no mistake about it:  proper hydration is important for health.  And that includes your brain’s.  Adequate hydration is an absolute must for optimal mental performance.  And when we say adequate, we mean drinking enough water daily.  And as important as quantity is quality.  If you drink enough of bad quality water, you just might end up with a bum stomach or worse, an infectious disease.

If you’re chronically dehydrated, how is your mental performance affected?  Dehydration, even a mild one, can negatively affect your mental performance and mood according to 2 studies conducted by the University of Connecticut’s Human Performance Laboratory.  Particularly, the studies showed that a mild dehydration – losing about 1.5% of normal water volume – practically has the same effect on both a person walking for about 4o minutes and one who’s sedentary.  What’s more, the studies showed that men and women are affected differently by even just a mild case of dehydration.  Although both men and women experienced anxiety, tension and fatigue, the women had it worse.  The men also experienced more of difficulty with working memory and being vigilant while the effect in women were perceived difficulty in accomplishing tasks as well as difficulties in concentrating.

It’s not enough to drink adequate amounts of water – you should drink adequate amounts of good quality water.  As mentioned earlier, contaminated water can lead to something as trivial as a bum stomach to something more serious like food poisoning or hepatitis, both of which will definitely hinder you from performing at your mental peak.  Other diseases you can contract from dirty water include taeniasis, hymenolepiasis and enterobiasis, which can affect you mentally via symptoms such as irritability, insomnia, hyperactivity and nervous manifestations.

Dirty water can also contain lead, which is a common contaminated of tap water, especially from those that came from old tubes that were still made of, you guessed it right, lead.  People who have been exposed to significant amounts of lead are reported to experience mental performance issues such as irritability and difficulty learning, among other things.  Therefore, you should be vigilant in ensuring you only drink clean and safe water.

Without a shadow of a doubt, medicines more often than not saves lives.  Improper and excessive use of such, however, can exacerbate a pre-existing condition or worse, give rise to potentially fatal new ones.  How?

First, by taking medicines that have potentially dangerous side effects based on your medical condition or current medication.  That’s why it’s very important that you consult with a doctor before taking any medication – including supplements.  For example, some herbal supplements used to enhance mental performance have brain-thinning properties that if taken together with a drug called Coumadin, can lead to brain oozing.

So if you’re under medication, be responsible enough to check with your doctor first before taking any other medicines and supplements.  This will not just optimize your brain health and mental performance, it can also save your life.

Alcohol consumption can offer a bit of a mental conundrum, so to speak.  It’s because on one hand, researchers involved in a study conducted in Rotterdam observed that daily but moderate consumption of alcohol helps reduce the risk for developing Alzheimer’s disease and dementia.  On the other hand however, long-term excessive alcohol consumption has also been proven to make the brain smaller and affect important mental functions like coordination of movement, memory and learning.  Drinking too much alcohol also depletes the body’s supply of thiamin, a shortage of which can cause paralysis of nerves used in eye movements, deterioration of muscle coordination and confusion, among others.

My advice?  Keep off the booze.  The risks seem to outweigh the possible benefits.  Anyway, you can make up for booze’s purported benefits through healthier alternatives.

DON’T BE A ZOMBIE, I.E., DON’T EAT BRAINS

For some reason, many people love eating exotic foods and when I say exotic I mean food made from animal parts (or even animals themselves) that most people in civilized societies stay away from.  These include raw food, intestines, liver, lungs, heart and brains.

Eating such foods can expose you to some types of particular risks.  Eating sushi and sashimi that was prepared by your friendly neighborhood sewerage maintenance guy, i.e., poorly and unhygienically prepared, can lead to a nasty viral or bacterial infection and bum stomach.

Some people – strange ones if I may add – think that eating more brains helps promote brain health much like the belief that eating more heart can give them a better one!  Unfortunately, that isn’t true.  While racoons’ brains are considered to be a delicious treat in some parts of the United States, they also carry the risk of getting Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease, which can lead to microscopic brain holes and lesions due to this particular treat’s disease-causing agent content.  The really bad news is that the body’s natural enzymes aren’t strong enough to kill these not-so-secret agents and being infected with prions is usually fatal.  So for your brain’s (and life’s) sakes, go brainless – eating wise that is.

“Your genetics load the gun.  Your lifestyle pulls the trigger.” – Dr. Mehmet Oz, celebrity doctor

Lifestyle is one friend that is often taken for granted and a potential enemy that’s often underestimated.  I know this all too well because as courtroom dramas often blurt out:  Guilty as charged!  Back when I was much younger, I can party the whole night, sleep for an hour or two and work out at the gym before heading to work – and my performance didn’t even suffer!  Or so I thought.  Over time, getting as little as 4 hours of sleep makes me a working zombie for the next day or two.  Things particularly went downhill for me mentally with lack of sleep after turning 30.

Taken for granted that you’re genetically gifted with some alien or mutant super mental powers that help you get away with very little sleep and a lot of all-night partying when you were younger, such a lifestyle – and others like it – still hold the key for you to enjoy optimal brain health and mental performance and ultimately, your ability to realize your full potential.  What good is having an I.Q. like that of Albert Einstein or Stephen Hawking when you can barely stay awake when it matters most?  Worse, even such genius is useless if you die early due to lifestyle-related diseases.

Remember, your genetics may endow you with superhuman mental powers but lifestyle is the proverbial key that can unlock such power.

Your brain (and overall) health as well as mental performance can either improve or deteriorate with chronic exposure to stress, depending on the kind to which you’re exposed to.  There is the good stress, which is also known as eustress and on the other hand, there’s distress or bad stress.  Stress that’s managed very well can be eustress, which leads to personal and physical growth and better health.  Distress, which is what people commonly refer to as stress, is stress that’s managed very poorly – if at all – and that leads to physical, personal and health deterioration.

A very good example of eustress is training to run a marathon.  Those who have and continue to run marathons know what it’s like to sacrifice and persevere under mental and physical stress in order to graduate from running 5-kilometer fun runs to running marathons and even ultra-marathons, i.e., runs of at least 50 kilometers.  In the beginning, there’s much discomfort or stress.  The key to successfully increasing running mileage was managing such stresses very well.  For example, from comfortably running 5 kilometers, they gradually increase training load to a level that’s no longer comfortable but still manageable – probably a 1o-kilometer run load.  When runners first increase their mileage, their leg muscles become sore due to the increased workload or stress but such soreness is temporary and manageable.  Over time, they’re able to build the stamina and physical fitness to run a full marathon.

Contrast this to runners who try to complete a 10-kilometer run as soon as possible by increasing their training load abruptly and excessively.  Normally, such a rapid increase in training load leads to quitting altogether due to over fatigue or worse, injuries.  This is distress.

When it comes to mental performance and brain health, distress – or stress – can come from quite a number of sources.  One is clutter.  A study conducted by Princeton University’s Neuroscience Institute that was published in the highly respected publication Journal Of Neuroscience, as reported in unclutter.com, stated that:

“Multiple stimuli present in the visual field at the same time compete for neural representation by mutually suppressing their evoked activity throughout visual cortex, providing a neural correlate for the limited processing capacity of the visual system.”

Okay, I must admit that whole statement was kind of geeky and nerdy so allow me to rephrase it in layman’s terms:  clutter makes for a very messy environment that significantly hampers you from processing information and focusing well.  On an even simpler note, imagine being held up with a gun pointed to your head.  The criminal wants you to sing the Star-Spangled Banner backwards while a snake slithers in your pants.  Yes, clutter can be that distracting!

Clutter is both physical and mental.  Often times, the former one is much easier to deal with and address.  It’s the mental ones that are usually more difficult to clean up.  You see, physical clutter can easily be addressed by re-organizing your stuff.  If you consider such an activity as easy as a surprise quiz on stochastic (what?) calculus, have no fear.  There are lots of books on helping you organize your stuff like Donna Smallin and Marilyn Bohn’s The One-Minute Organizer (A To Z Storage Solutions) and Go Organize, respectively.

One producer of mental stress is the habit of procrastinating or putting off things that can be done now for later.  I was once an avid fan of procrastination when I was much younger, particularly when it comes to working out at the gym.  Whenever I’d come home tired from work, which is often the case, I’d put off working out early the next morning to be able to rest more and recharge, believing that doing so will lead to more energy for eventually working out.  It never happened – as I kept on putting off the gym work, the harder it was for me to start working out.  The result was a huge weight gain.  Worse, when I finally got the chance to start working out, I wanted to lose the weight quickly because of I wanted to make up for lost time due to my procrastination.  I crash dieted along with working out at high intensity that eventually, I succumbed to over fatigue and stopped working out.  As expected, I even gained more weight.  Such is the power of procrastination!

One very potent source of mental stress is the inability to say no to unnecessary commitments, obligations and responsibilities.  In case you forgot, you’re still human and despite the lie that says anything you can think of you can do, you can’t.  Even if you think all day that you can be productive 24/7 without sleep, it won’t happen.  Knowing your limitations help you limit your responsibilities, commitments and obligations to those that are truly necessary for you to achieve your life goals and achieve them well.  When you learn to say no to such things, you say yes to effective stress management and of course, optimal brain health and mental performance.

Now let’s talk about eustress or good stress!  A stress-free life is a very unproductive life that’s characterized by now personal growth!  As with muscles that atrophy without adequate amounts of stress, so is your brain’s health and mental performance without good mental stress.

Your ability to do your job well didn’t come overnight and neither did your ability to pass your school exams when you were still studying.  They only came about after you continuously took on good mental stress such as problem solving or learning how to do your job, which at some point became second nature to you.  After the initial but manageable stress, your brain has adapted well resulting in better mental performance.

One of the best – and most fun – ways to eustress your brain is through reading.  Personally, I love to read because I am a bookworm and I find that such a voracious appetite for reading – even when my eyes want to retire for the night – has helped me immensely in terms of writing and speaking very well on a wide range of topics.  I wouldn’t have been able to write this book if it weren’t for the countless number of hours spent reading a ton of books even when it felt uncomfortable.

We talked about playing brain-friendly games in an earlier chapter and that’s another great source of mental eustress for optimal mental performance.  Regularly playing mental games regularly increasing the difficulty levels, you challenge your mental faculties enough to make them used to performing at higher and higher levels – your brain becomes even stronger!  The 3 games I recommended earlier – Sudoku, crosswords and jigsaw puzzles – as well as other popular ones like games of the generals, chess, scrabble and checkers enable your brain to adapt to greater work loads but in a fun ways.  For more great ways to stress your brain in good ways through games, check out lumosity.com.

Make no mistake about it, getting enough zzzzzzz is absolutely critical for peak brain health and mental performance.  It’s because your brain is also a muscle and as one, it also needs enough time to recuperate and recharge from a hard day’s work.  With enough sleep, our minds tend to be sharp and alert, which allows you to focus on what you need to focus on at the moment, respond fast to any important developments and stay awake during important mental tasks.  On the other hand, chronic sleep deprivation compromises our alertness, ability to pay attention and focus, our capacity to quickly respond and makes it very hard to stay awake.  Such is the sluggish brain, which leads to poor mental performance.

Now that you know why getting enough sleep is important, just how much sleep is considered adequate?  Unfortunately, there’s no gold standard on which to base your actual sleeping hours.  However, it’s a generally accepted belief that 7 to 10 hours is the optimal, depending on the person’s age and workload.  The most practical – and fairly accurate way – of assessing how many hours of sleep you need for optimal mental performance is to wake up for the next 3 days without an alarm, taking note of how many hours of sleep you had and how you felt and performed mentally during the day.  This simple trial-and-error procedure can go a long way to help you know how much sleep you need every night for optimal brain health and mental performance.

According to a report by the American Association of Retired Persons (AARP) that involved 116,000 participants in a public-health study, there was significantly less mental performance decline in people that enjoyed strong relationships compared to those who didn’t.  Other experts also believe that physical and mental aging are accelerated by chronic isolation from people.  According to renowned neurologist Michael Merzenich, PhD from the University of California, San Francisco:

“As soon as you become captive in your room or your chair, you’ve got a problem.  You become removed from the possibilities for excitement, for learning and for engaging your rain with fun and surprise.  Your brain needs to get out and have those 1,000 daily surprises.”

So if you want to optimize brain health and extend your mental performance peak, make friends and spend time with them and your family!  And most of all, enjoy it!

Brain-Friendly Home

“Home is where you feel at home and are treated well.” – The Dalai Lama

One of the things I look forward to the most after a long, hard day at work was coming home, where I am free to be who I am, do what I want to do and experience unconditional love from my spouse and parents.  At the office, it’s very hard to relax even if all my work is done ahead of time for fear of being perceived as lax and lazy.  I also can’t be who I really am because understandably, most companies and business’ require a great degree of uniformity from the way things are done to how their employees dress up in order to make the company or business grow.

If I’m not mistaken, you also love coming home at the end of the day for the same reasons.  At home, you have the opportunity to recuperate from life’s daily stresses and rest after a hard day’s work.  It’s a great place – probably the only place – where you can really rest both mentally and physically.  But if you want to enjoy optimal brain health and mental performance, you’d want to make your home as conducive to rest, relaxation and recuperation as you possibly can.

One aspect of a truly conducive home is that it’s generally free from harmful materials and chemicals that can be toxic to you and your family’s health over time.  Normally, such chemicals are found in many of your ordinary household items such as paints, paint thinners, nail polish removers, glues and bleach.  Now, don’t get me wrong, you don’t necessarily have to throw them all away.  What’s important is you store them well in places that are secure and won’t be easily damaged or accessed by children.

Another way of reducing your home’s toxicity is by ditching regular paint for those that are lead-free.  Remember, constant exposure to enough lead can significantly affect your brain (and overall) health as well as mental performance including mood disorders and memory loss.  The dust particles from eroding lead-based paints that line your home’s walls can be easily inhaled and result in lead toxicity.

Finally, a simple redecoration or re-organization of your house every now and then can benefit you mentally.  It’s because doing so helps changes things up regularly, which can help new brain cells to grow.  If you’re currently feeling tired or sluggish mentally, why don’t you try rearranging your home furniture and fixtures?  More than making your life a lot more interesting, it can help you bust out of your mental rut too.
