Alzheimer’s disease: neuropathology, neuropsychological differential diagnosis, and treatment

2.1 General summary

In typical Alzheimer’s disease (AD), the progressive decline concerns the

cognitive triad of memory deficits, word finding problems, and spatial dis-

orientation. However, there are atypical variants of AD that start with lan-

guage, visual, or executive functions impairment. Neuropsychiatric changes

of AD encompass higher apathy, increased anxiety, and dependence, but not

depressive mood. Neuropsychological testing can distinguish between typical

AD patients and healthy controls rather well, but the differentiation between

different neurodegenerative diseases poses considerable problems.

Consolidation of information is highly impaired in AD patients and should

be used to separate AD patients from patients with unknown causes of cogni-

tive impairment. Still, even composite scores for verbal learning, recall, and

recognition suffer from the fact that they are affected by the stage of disease

progression. Assessment should additionally rely on a measurement of false

positives in a picture recognition task (PRT). Patients with AD display an

increased number of false positives. They also tend to have a lower primacy

proportion than recency proportion when analyzing the wordlist serial posi-

tion effect. Paired associate learning is sensitive for memory impairments

early in the disease progression. Neuropsychological assessment still looks

for cutoff scores independent from global impairment to enhance differential

diagnosis. However, arguing that there are no single tests or combination of

test results that allow for an accurate clinical diagnosis, does not mean that

the clinical investigation is invalid. It means that the combined information

from the different aspects of the assessment must guide the decision for the

correct diagnosis.

Statistically significant differences between groups of patients who will

show cognitive decline or convert to dementia can be shown up to 10 years

before the disease manifests. Delayed free recall is the most sensitive

memory test that is used. The combination of neuropsychological test results

and abnormal biomarkers from cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) or as MRI

increases the precision of the prediction, but still does not reach 100%.

When making predictions for single patients, the current memory loss of the

patient should be evident in several different tests, not only in one test, even

if this test offers various results (for learning, delayed free recall, and recog-

nition). If the functional loss is low, the chance of recovering from mild cog-

nitive impairment (MCI) is almost the same as the risk of declining. If the

loss is high, the risk of declining increases.

Discussing the results of the neuropsychological examination concerning

the prognosis with patients is centered around the term “risk.” It is important

to emphasize that a risk concerns something that might happen and not

something that will necessarily happen. The meaning of an increased risk

should be explicitly communicated. Furthermore several examples should be

provided to the patients to ameliorate the understanding of the difference

between increased risk and a causal consequence.

Lifestyle changes may decelerate the progression of cognitive decline in

patients with MCI and AD. Patients should be informed that physical and

cognitive training and following a Mediterranean diet might reduce the risk

of cognitive decline. However, such a lifestyle change must be intensive and

followed almost every day. In contrast to earlier views, recent studies have

shown that cognitive training and cognitive rehabilitation of patients with

MCI and dementia does have an impact on their global cognitive perfor-

mance. There seems to be a difference between MCI patients and those with

dementia: In MCI patients, improvement in memory seems to be greater than

in global cognitive performance, whereas in patients with dementia the main

effects concern global cognitive performance—possible due to floor effects

in memory testing. Cognitive training in MCIs could therefore be more spe-

cific than in AD.

2.2 Alzheimer’s disease—clinical signs and assessment

AD is the most frequent cause of dementia, accounting for approximately

50% of all dementias. More women than men are affected by AD (Mazure

& Swendsen, 2016). Although estimates of the prevalence of AD vary (prob-

ably due to uncertainties regarding its diagnosis), the rate of AD increases by

0.3% in the 6069 age group and up to more than 10% in the age of 80 and

older. There are two major variants of AD: an early variant with symptoms

that appear before the age of 65 (ICD 10 code of F00.0) and a late variant

(termed sporadic AD), in which symptoms onset occurs in the age of 65 and

older (ICD 10 code of F00.1). The early variant has a strong hereditary com-

ponent, whereas the genetic risk in the sporadic variant is not high (but still

present). Despite these apparent differences, we will not differentiate

Several years ago, Selkoe proposed a beta-amyloid cascade model (com-

pare Selkoe & Hardy, 2016) which tried to synthesize the neurobiological

knowledge of this disease. This model has also been summarized in several

articles that tried to translate the neuropathological process into different

clinical phases of the disease (Jack et al., 2010, 2013, 2018). In short, the

disease begins with the irregular cleavage of the amyloid precursor protein

(APP), which is bound to the cell wall with extensions into the extracellular

space (Fig. 2.3). This protein is important for the remodulation of neurons

especially during learning and pathological processes. APP is increased after

a stroke and may play a role in sprouting of dendrites and remodeling of

neurons. Normally the APP derived peptides are cleaved by an alpha secre-

tase. The remaining parts are soluble fragments which are eliminated by

extracellular removal as part of the cerebrospinal fluid flow. Due to unknown

reasons, the APP is sometimes cleaved by a beta secretase, whereby the

remaining part is subsequently cleaved by an additional gamma secretase. In

this case, the peptide beta-amyloid (142) moves into the extracellular

space. Beta-amyloid (142) may exert a toxic effect on neurons. It is soluble

as fragments, however, it tends to cluster together with other beta-amyloid

(142) fragments resulting in beta-amyloid (142) plaques. These plaques

attract additional soluble beta-amyloid (142) fragments with the

the stabilization of the axon, the concentration of tau and phosphorylated tau

neurodegeneration, the concentration is high. The levels of total tau and

Braak and Braak (1991) proposed different neuropathological stages for

the progression of AD. The formation of neurofibrillary tangle deposits starts

early in the entorhinal cortex and the hippocampus. As long as the deposits

are restricted to these areas, the progression can be labeled either as stage I

or II, depending on the amount of deposits. Afterward, the neurofibrillary

tangles spread into the limbic cortex, encompassing the mediobasal temporal

lobe, the cingulate, and the medial orbitofrontal cortex. The disease then pro-

gresses to stages III and IV. Later on, during stages V and VI the whole cor-

tex is affected; however, the frequency of tangles remains low in the striate

cortex of the occipital lobe and the motor and sensory cortex. The Braak

The accuracy of a CSF-based dementia diagnosis is high when AD

patients are compared with healthy controls. The accuracy remains high even

when comparing patients with a neurodegenerative disease resulting from a

completely different cascade of pathogenetic processes (such as PD). The

accuracy is considerably lower, however, when AD patients are compared

with patients suffering from dementia due to other causes. Tapiola et al.

(2009) compared patients with AD that had been verified postmortem to

healthy controls. They showed that the accuracy of the beta-amyloid (142)

diagnosis alone was 80.5% and the accuracy of phosphor-tau was 70.7%.

Using a ratio between tau and beta-amyloid (142) or phosphor-tau and

beta-amyloid (142) raised the accuracy to 85.4% or 90.2%, respectively

(Table 2.3).

Another study concerning phosphor-tau compared the accuracy of a diag-

nosis between AD and non-AD dementia according to CSF results based on

postmortem diagnosis verification (Struyfs et al., 2015). For this comparison

the accuracy was hardly ever higher than 75% regardless of whether the dif-

ferent CSF markers were used alone or in combination. Other studies show

that neither phosphor-tau nor beta-amyloid (142) alone allow for a reliable

prediction of which patients will develop AD over a 3-year timeframe

(Desikan et al., 2012; Dickerson et al., 2013). On the other hand, there is a

consensus that the combination of cognitive decline (measured by neuropsy-

chological tests) together with one or more impaired biomarkers (PET, CSF,

or genetic) allows for the diagnosis of AD, even if the patient does not

already suffer from Alzheimer’s dementia (Table 2.1, Dubois et al., 2014).

This is important because only an early diagnosis of AD permits effective

interventions to be implemented.

extremely high when comparing healthy controls with patients suffering

from AD. Because AD is defined by a severe memory deficit, it is not sur-

for detecting AD patients among healthy controls. Weissberger et al.

(2017) also analyzed the detection of clinically diagnosed patients with

MCI compared with healthy controls. This time Weissberger and collea-

distinguish patients with possible preclinical AD from healthy controls.

Discriminating between the patients and healthy controls can be quite chal-

lenging because the degree of cognitive impairment is much milder in the

patients. Weissberger et al. (2017) found that the sensitivity of delayed

recall was 75% and the specificity was somewhat higher, 81%. Thus the

sensitivity and specificity were considerably lower than the sensitivity and

specificity for detecting AD patients among a healthy control group.

However, since not all patients with MCI will suffer from a neurodegenera-

sensitivity and specificity of neuropsychological test results are negatively

impacted by the fact that they have to average findings from different test-

information that is to be remembered (separate learning trials) as well as a

recognition trial may improve the sensitivity and specificity of memory

note, the CVLT showed a sensitivity of 90% and a specificity of 84%

2.5 Specificity and sensitivity of other memory tests for detecting an amnestic syndrome of the hippocampal type (i.e., Alzheimer’s disease)

Most of the standard neuropsychological tests used in assessing dementia

were developed many years ago and not specifically designed for the investi-

gation of patients with AD, but often for younger patients suffering from

traumatic brain injury or temporal lobe epilepsy. For example, the CERAD-

NAB was developed more than 25 years ago and the construction of the

CERAD-NAB reflects the knowledge about hippocampal memory dysfunc-

tions dominating the scientific discussion during that time. In the following,

three additional aspects of memory impairment will be discussed that are not

part of routine CERAD-NAB testing: the serial position effect of wordlist

learning, the difference between recollection and familiarity and their role in

recognition memory, and the meaning of relational learning for differential

The word list serial position effect means that the first and the last words

of a word list have a much higher chance of being reproduced during learn-

ing than words stemming from the middle part of the list. One explanation

of this effect is that the first words of the list are transferred into to long-

term memory, whereas the last words are remembered by working memory.

This explanation has been criticized, however, because a specific strategy

might play a role for the phenomenon: participants might be inclined to

rehearse the first words during word list learning and rehearsing might be

the reason why these words are remembered better. Although this distinction

is important from a cognitive neuropsychological view, if the measurement

of long-term consolidation is the relevant task, it is irrelevant how long-term

consolidation is achieved.

Several studies have shown that AD and amnestic MCI patients suffer

from a reduced primacy effect (remembering less words from the beginning

of a word list) and an increased recency effect (remembering more words

from the end of the word list). For a short review, consult the introduction

section of Cunha, Guerreiro, de Mendonca, Oliveira, and Santana (2012).

Moreover, AD patients are less able to remember words especially from the

last part of a word list during free delayed recall, arguing that in AD patients

these words are less likely to be transferred in long-term memory (as pre-

dicted for an amnestic syndrome of the hippocampal type).

Some studies also show that the primacy and the recency effect might be

helpful for the differential diagnosis. In an early study Capitani, Della Sala,

Logie, and Spinnler (1992) documented differences between AD patients and

patients with frontal lesions based on an evaluation of the recency part of the

word list. Orru et al. (2009) compared clinical diagnosed AD patients with

patients with mixed dementia and those with vascular dementia. Although

the three groups did not differ in learning and delayed recall, AD patients

and those with mixed dementia exhibited a relatively increased recency to

primacy proportion, whereas patients with only vascular dementia showed an

increased primacy to recency proportion. This result suggests that patients

with AD pathology suffer from long-term consolidation and use their work-

ing memory capacities to replicate wordlists, whereas vascular patients suffer

from working memory impairments and use their remaining long-term mem-

ory process for the reproduction of the list.

Bayley et al. (2000) compared AD patients with different degrees of disease

progression with patients suffering from an electroconvulsive amnesia. AD

patients showed a reduced primacy portion compared with their recency portion,

whereas the patients with electroconvulsive therapy displayed a balanced

recency and primacy ratio. Foldi, Brickman, Schaefer, and Knutelska (2003)

also showed that AD patients, compared with those with late life depres-

sion, are characterized by a larger recency effect. In a recent study, Busse

et al. (2017) compared AD patients with patients suffering from Lewy

body disease with neuropsychological tests and wordlist learning. Patients

with Lewy body disease were impaired in visuoperception and visuocon-

struction (compare Section 3.5 on visual impairments in this patient

group), whereas AD patients exhibited fast forgetting of words after dis-

traction. Concerning the recency and primacy proportion, both patients

groups showed a low primacy proportion, however, patients with Lewy

body dementia also showed a decrease in the recency part of the wordlist

(which corresponds with the results of Orru et al., 2009 for patients with

vascular dementia). Busse et al. (2017) argue that the reduction of the

recency proportion may be caused by a working memory deficit in the

Lewy body disease patients. They base this argument on the correlation of

recency proportion and performance in the digit span task.

Moser et al. (2014) analyzed the primacy and the recency effect in

patients with mild and moderate AD and in patients with amnestic and non-

amnestic MCI. Patients with amnestic MCI or AD differed in wordlist learn-

ing and wordlist delayed recall from the healthy control and the nonamnestic

MCI group. Additionally and contributing to the separation between the

CERAD-NAB differs from many other wordlist learning tests in reorga-

nizing the sequence of the remembered words on a trial to trial basis.

Therefore the primacy effect and the recency effect cannot be easily evalu-

ated. The CERAD-NAB also does not distinguish between false positives

and false negatives during recognition testing, offering population-based

scores only for the global discrimination index (number of hits plus number

of correct rejections divided by the total number of items presented).

However, more recent memory models argue that true positives in recogni-

tion memory may be based on recollection and/or familiarity. In the case of

recollection, the patient consciously distinguishes whether he or she has seen

the presented item during the study phase. In the event of a familiarity-based

decision, the response relies on a feeling of knowing and recently encounter-

ing the item, but not on a true remembrance of the contextual information,

that is, when and where this item has been heard or seen before. As AD

number of false positives is an important variable for the differential diagno-

of false positives which can occur during recognition: “Yes” responses to

items that are semantically related to learned items and “yes” responses to

items that are completely new to the patients. According to their results,

increased responses to semantically related items do not really differ between

AD patients and patients with executive function deficits; however, only AD

patients show increased “yes” response to completely new and unrelated

items. This suggests either a very liberal response bias in patients with AD

resulting in a generalized inflation of positive answers or an overwhelming

effect of familiarity even when hearing completely new words.

McDermott paradigm to compare AD patients with healthy controls. In this

paradigm, participants read a series of words during the study phase and

repeated presentations to reject the lure words. In the other hand, healthy

controls could use recollection performance to become aware of the words

that were not part of the study list. On a theoretical level, this would mean

that in terms of recognition, recognition performance of AD patients relies

more or less exclusively on familiarity and not on recollection. Therefore the

likelihood of false-positive errors is highly increased in this patient group.

2.7 Summary of neuropsychological testing in patients suspected for Alzheimer’s disease

In summary, it can be stated that neuropsychological testing is able to distin-

guish between typical AD patients and healthy controls rather well, but the

differentiation between different neurodegenerative diseases leading to mem-

ory impairments poses considerable problems. Consolidation of information

is highly impaired in AD patients and should be used to separate AD patients

from patients with unknown causes of cognitive impairment. Still, even com-

posite scores for verbal learning, recall and recognition, or verbal and visuo-

constructive recall suffer from the fact that they are affected by the stage

of disease progression. They differ from CSF biomarkers in this respect.

The probability for diagnosing AD increases in patients with consolidation

deficits if they show normal phonological word fluency, impaired semantic

word fluency and have an average digit span percentile above 30.

Neuropsychiatric changes of AD encompass higher apathy, increased anxiety

and dependence, but not depressive mood. Despite these distinctive symp-

toms of AD, neuropsychological assessment still looks for cutoff scores that

do not depend on the global impairment to enhance the probability of their

differential diagnosis.

Neuropsychological assessment of patients suspected to suffer from (pre-

clinical) AD should additionally rely on a measurement of false positives in

a PRT. Patients with AD display an increased number of false positives.

They also tend to have a lower primacy proportion than recency proportion

when analyzing the wordlist serial position effect. Paired associate learning

is highly sensitive for memory impairments very early in the disease progres-

sion. However, impairments in PAL are also common in other neurodegener-

ative diseases, if the patients start to progress. Thus an impairment in PAL

does not seem to be specific feature of AD but a global measurement of the

severity of the memory decline.

Arguing that there are no single tests or combination of test results

that allow for an accurate clinical diagnosis, does not mean that the clini-

cal investigation is invalid. It means that the combined information from

2.8 Prediction of conversion to Alzheimer’s disease by neuropsychological tests

2.8.1 Prediction using the WMS logical memory test and the ADNI neuropsychological mild cognitive impairment criteria

The case presented earlier highlights another problem regarding the assess-

ment of neurodegenerative diseases: the question of the prognosis. In many

patients, only another future assessment will deliver the correct diagnosis.

On the other hand, the main interest of the patients is to obtain information

immediately about how their problems might develop in the future. Will

they progress to a state of dementia or not? This question can be reframed in

clinical terms as predicting whether the patient’s state will convert from

MCI to dementia and, in the case of typical AD, from amnestic MCI to AD.

One of the earliest studies, with a large number of patients from two dif-

ferent study centers (the Mayo Clinic and the Karolinska Institute), showed

an increased risk for MCI patients to concert to dementia, depending on the

number of cognitive domains being affected (Arnaiz et al., 2004): about 40%

of the patients with impairment in memory performance converted to demen-

tia after 5 years, however, this number rose to more than 50% when the

patient was impaired in more than one neuropsychological test).

Taken together, these results suggest a conversion rate of about 7%8%

per year for pure amnestic MCI patients and 12% for multidomain impaired

patients. It should be kept in mind that all these patients came to the hospital

because they or their relatives noted cognitive decline, and that other causes

for that experienced decline were excluded through an extended neurological

examination. Thus the results cannot be easily generalized to population

samples that do not possess an urgent enough sense of cognitive decline that

causes them to get in contact with a diagnostic unit.

Over the past 15 years, the ADNI research group, a very large Northern

America-based research network, was able to include a large number of par-

ticipants in their data set. They were categorized into those with and without

amnestic MCI according to the result of the logical memory test of the WMS

IV (being impaired corresponded with a score smaller than 21.5SD).

Patients who were impaired in other tests, but not in the logical memory test,

were categorized as having a nonamnestic MCI. Storandt and Morris (2010)

analyzed the predictive value of the logical memory test in a longitudinal

project including healthy controls. The brains of all subjects were analyzed

postmortem to determine whether they had developed Alzheimer’s dementia

(or not) to provide a definitive diagnosis. The predictive value of the logical

memory test was low, and specificity and sensitivity ranged from 55% to

50%. Other neuropsychological tests, like visual associate learning from the

WMS and Block design, showed a higher specificity (above 80%) but a low

sensitivity (between 30% and 40%).

Bondi et al. (2014) also used the logical memory test of the WMS and

the data of participants from the ADNI project to analyze the predictive

power of this test. As mentioned previously, a score less than 21.5SD is

the hallmark of amnestic MCI for the ADNI diagnosis. After about 4

years, 35.8% of the pure amnestic MCI patients converted to dementia,

61.7% remained stable and 4.2% reversed to a normal score. Fifty-seven

percentage of the patients with more than one impairment converted to

dementia and only 1% reversed. Additionally there was a group of unim-

paired participants and 9.3% of them converted to dementia. Bondi et al.

(2014) compared these percentages with a categorization based on two

measures for each cognitive domain tested. With respect to memory, this

was done by adding the performance in the auditory verbal learning test

(which includes learning trials and a recognition measure) with the text

reproduction of the WMS. The classification based on two measures per-

formed better than that based on the text reproduction alone: 46.5% of the

patients with amnestic MCI converted to dementia and none of these

patients reverted to an unimpaired state. In the case of being impaired in

more than one cognitive domain (being impaired in both memory tests

and in one other test), the conversion rate was even higher: 60% of these

patients converted to dementia within the following 7 years. These num-

bers are very similar to results from the Mayo Clinic and the Karolinska

Institute mentioned earlier (Arnaiz et al., 2004). The study by Bondi et al.

(2014) also compared the predictive power of the auditory verbal learning

test alone with that of text reproduction alone. The auditory verbal learn-

ing test was better in predicting the conversion. One may conclude that a

“one-shot presentation” of verbal information, as is the case in the text

reproduction test of the WMS IV, is suboptimal for diagnosing MCI.

Memory measures based on several learning trials and those that include

recognition elements yielded better results for making predictions regard-

ing the fate of the patients.

Alzheimer’s dementia. Depending on the study, the sensitivity was about

75% and the specificity was often around 80% (or even higher). Visuospatial

memory tests assessing free recall were slightly less accurate than verbal

2.8.3 How early can cognitive impairments be measured in patients converting to dementia?

How early can we detect cognitive differences between participants that

develop dementia and participants who do not develop the disease? This

question can be studied only in longitudinal studies which include normal

healthy participants or participants who express another nonbrain disease,

which increases the risk of developing dementia. Cervilla, Prince, Joels,

Lovestone, and Mann (2004) followed a group of patients with hypertension

over a time period of 12 years and used various cognitive tests to assess their

cognitive functioning at inclusion and during the following years. Forty-one

of the analyzed group of 387 patients developed dementia. Patients who

developed dementia significantly differed from patients who did not develop

dementia with regard to neuropsychological test results. Furthermore these

differences were already detectable 12 years prior to dementia onset. The

highest difference between the groups was found in the trail making test A,

while the lowest difference was in the PAL task. When the focus was only

on patients with a clinically based diagnosis of AD, performance in the PAL

task increased as a relevant test marker. With the same focus, the difference

in the TMT-A decreased, however, the TMT-A still showed the highest

effect size. Although the results of this study are interesting and suggest that

neuropsychological test results already start to differ 10 years before the

diagnosis. There was also a statistically significant difference in free recall

in the first group; however, this difference did not survive the statistical cor-

rection for age differences. Unfortunately the cause of the dementia was not

validated by other investigations; therefore we do not know how many of the

patients with dementia suffered from AD.

There is one other longitudinal study that should be mentioned here.

Mistridis et al. (2015) used the CERAD-NAB to follow cognitive decline in

the Basel Study on the Elderly longitudinal study. All participants were

assessed every 2 years. They divided the whole group into two subgroups. The

first group, the preclinical AD group, developed MCI. The other group did not

Performance on the DMS48 predicted conversion to AD with a sensitivity of

memory together lead to an optimal classification of the patients.

De Simone, Perri, Fadda, Caltagirone, and Carlesimo (2019) directly com-

pared the power of delayed recall and false positives in recognition memory

during wordlist learning to predict the conversion to dementia over the course

of 3 years. Stable MCI patients showed impaired free recall but improved

more than healthy controls during recognition testing. Recognition perfor-

mance correctly classified patients who showed a converting or a stable MCI

and the classification was more accurate than the classification based on free

recall. The ability to discriminate between hits and distractors was an espe-

cially useful diagnostic tool for predicting progression to AD dementia from

the prodromal stage of MCI. After 3 years, only about 30% of patients with

false positives in recognition memory still had MCIs, whereas above 70% of

the patients without false positives in recognition did not convert to dementia.

These small-scale studies seem to contradict the results of Mistridis et al.

(2015) who found that the discrimination index of the CERAD-NAB was not

very effective in predicting the conversion of the patients. However, the rec-

ognition subtests of the CERAD-NAB (and of most of the clinical wordlist

learning tests) are much easier to perform than the delayed free recall sub-

test. The relative easier task load of recognition may lead to ceiling effects

for recognition performance in participants with mild memory impairments

but, at the same time, they may already show impaired performance in free

recall tasks (which is prognostically meaningful but unspecific for AD). In

the small-scale studies by De Simone et al. (2019) and Didic et al. (2013)

the recognition task was more difficult than the CERAD-NAB recognition

task. Therefore the predictive power of recognition might have been higher.

Another problem that is related to this issue is that recognition memory tests

do not always distinguish between false positives and misses (e.g., the CERAD-

NAB) and most tests that do distinguish between false positives and misses are

verbal learning tests. It is well known that words do not produce strong familiar-

ity effects. More recent and more experimental recognition testing includes

visual distractors that invite patients to rely more on familiarity judgments than

on words. These paradigms have shown that AD patients produce many false

positive errors as a response to such distractors. Therefore testing of AD patients

should include recognition measures with highly prototypical pictures.

Without taking the more recent study results regarding the specific mem-

ory impairments in AD into account, it is unlikely that a prediction regarding

the development of the disease can reach higher levels of sensitivity and

specificity with neuropsychological measures alone. Future studies should

recall. It is highly probable that the power to predict conversion based on

recognition performance will then increase.

2.8.5 Problems for predicting conversion to dementia using neuropsychological test results

Almost all of these studies used either dementia as an “outcome” variable or

focused on the clinical diagnosis of AD (ignoring other kinds of dementia).

Caminiti et al. (2018) published a study on the predictive value of CSF and PET

markers in predicting the conversion to AD or other kinds of dementia after

roughly 19 months. There was a high conversion ratio in their study, because

more than 60% of the MCI patients developed dementia after less than 2 years.

The specific neuropsychological impairment pattern was only partially informative

for a differential prognosis: Of the 39 MCI patients who converted to AD, 28.2%

were single-domain amnestic MCI, 56.4% were multidomain amnestic MCI,

12.8% were single-domain nonamnestic MCI and 2.6% were multidomain non-

amnestic MCI. Four single-domain, three multidomain amnestic, and three multi-

domain nonamnestic MCI participants converted to frontotemporal dementia and

one multidomain amnestic MCI patient converted to Lewy body dementia. Two

single-domain amnestic MCI patients returned to cognitive normality (i.e., classi-

fied as reversers). Thus the sensitivity of amnestic MCI for developing AD was

about 82%, but the specificity was below 50%. This study did not use tasks spe-

cifically designed to test an amnestic syndrome of the hippocampal type, however.

Amnestic MCI was defined as suffering from any kind of memory problems.

Reul, Lohmann, Wiendl, Duning, and Johnen (2017) analyzed whether

the CERAD-NAB along with other tests, that is, a core test battery for

dementia as proposed earlier, was able to discriminate between early stage

AD and bvFTD patients. They followed 43 patients who later received a

clinically verified AD diagnosis and 26 patients who later received a clini-

cally verified bvFTD diagnosis. Verbal recall, figure copy and imitation of

face postures and limb gestures contributed to the differential diagnosis.

Seventy-seven percent of the bvFTD patients and 90% of the AD patients

could be classified correctly using these four test variables. Attention testing

(TMT-A), executive function performance and social cognition, however,

did not distinguish between the patient groups. Unfortunately the ability to

differentially predict conversion to AD specifically (as opposed to FTD or

vascular dementia) using neuropsychological test results has not been tested

in larger patient groups and has yet to be demonstrated.

2.8.6 A note on prediction from mild cognitive impairment to dementia based on biomarkers

In a study conducted by Caminiti et al. (2018), FDG-PET with SPM classification

together with additional post hoc postprocessing algorithms improved the correct

classification of pure neuropsychological investigations from 32 to 35 of the 39

patients who converted to AD. FDG-PET was, however, not more accurate than

CSF markers, that is, compared with phosphor-tau/beta-amyloid (142) or the

total tau/beta-amyloid ratio. Nevertheless, in this study, the sensitivity of FDG-

PET along with specific post hoc postprocessing algorithms increased the sensitiv-

ity from 82% to 90% (compared with a neuropsychological-based prediction).

Unfortunately the specific tests used for the neuropsychological assessment are

not described in the methods section of the study. On a global level, the accuracy

of a CSF biomarker-based prognosis for AD (vs other kinds of dementia) is

around 80% (depending on the length of the follow-up).

Unfortunately the new beta-amyloid PET tracers are not better predictors

than CSF analysis (Palmqvist, Mattsson, Hansson, & Alzheimer’s Disease

Neuroimaging, 2016). A recent longitudinal study by van Maurik et al. (2017)

showed that for patients with MCI, even the combination of different biomar-

kers and a MMSE score of 24 increased the prognosis of converting to demen-

tia after 3 years to maximum 89% (with two impaired MRI features and two

impaired CSF results), which is surprisingly low considering that the patients

already exhibited a MMSE score of 24. Furthermore the 1-year conversion

rate was below 26%. Without any abnormalities in the CSF, the risk of con-

verting to dementia for patients with an MMSE score of 24 decreased to 6%.

Including patients with an MMSE score of less than 29 (i.e., patients with

only subtle cognitive impairments) reduced the highest conversion prediction

with two impaired biomarkers to 16% after 1 year and to 64% for a 3-year

time frame. This is not much higher than the standard prediction made without

incorporating biomarkers and based solely on the knowledge that a patient suf-

fers from MCI (about a 10% conversion ratio per year). In general, predictions

based on MRI features were less precise than predictions based on CSF values.

On a more global level, the study by van Maurik et al. (2017) showed that

even the best clinically used biomarkers are far from perfect when it comes to

predicting conversion to dementia.

Richard, Schmand, Eikelenboom, Van Gool, and Alzheimer’s Disease

Neuroimaging, (2013) compared CSF- and MRI-based biomarkers with mem-

ory testing to determine their accuracy for correctly predicting the conversion

from MCI to AD. All patients were taken from the ADNI group, the mean

time for follow-up was 3 years 3months, and 181 MCI patients were included.

The phosphor-tau ratio was used as the CSF marker. Neuropsychological test-

ing, MRI and CSF were able to predict conversion if analyzed independently.

Still, neither MRI nor CSF biomarkers improved the prognosis after adminis-

tration of the auditory verbal learning test. This means that the biomarkers

were not superior in predicting who will develop dementia and who will not.

2.8.7 Summary

Statistically significant differences between groups of patients who will

show cognitive decline or convert to dementia can be shown up to 10 years

100 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

before the disease manifests. The majority of the studies identified that

delayed free recall is the most sensitive parameter for detecting future cogni-

tive decline, but this depends on the difficulty of the memory test that is

used. A significant difference between groups is not enough to predict

decline in single patients, however. When making predictions for single

patients, the current memory loss of the patient should be evident in several

different tests, not only in one test, even if this test offers various results (for

learning, delayed free recall, recognition). If the functional loss is low, the

chance of recovering from MCI is almost the same as the risk of declining.

If the loss is high, the risk of declining increases. For the CERAD-NAB, it

can be stated that remembering less than 15 words during the three learning

trials increases the risk for a further decline up to 50% during the next 3

years (in patients older than 74 years).

Most of the studies on predicting cognitive decline or converting to AD

used classical neuropsychological tests. There is a paucity of studies that

focus on more AD-specific and AD-characteristic memory impairments such

as the primacy index, paired associate learning and false positives in recogni-

tion. Studies using such tests might lead to an improved predictive power of

neuropsychological testing.

The combination of neuropsychological test results and abnormal biomar-

kers from CSF or as MRI increases the precision of the prediction, but the

prognostication still does not reach 100%. Therefore even if both results are

abnormal, it is correct to talk about an increased risk for progression and

conversion and not about a definite conversion.

Discussing the results of the neuropsychological examination concern-

ing the prognosis with patients is centered around the term “risk.” It is

very important to emphasize that a risk concerns something that might

happen and not something that will necessarily happen. Otherwise, the

patient might think he or she will develop a dementia either way, which

could lead to depressive mood and a sharp reduction of quality of life.

Such an inevitable interpretation of the term risk, however, is wrong.

Therefore the meaning of an increased risk should be explicitly communi-

cated. Furthermore several examples should be provided to the patients to

ameliorate the understanding of the difference between increased risk and

a causal consequence.

2.9 Is a treatment of Alzheimer’s disease and mild cognitive impairment effective and therefore necessary?

2.9.1 Lifestyle and pharmacological interventions in mild cognitive impairment patients and the conversion to Alzheimer’s disease

Not only among the general public but also in the world of neurologists, psy-

chiatrists, and psychologists, the diagnosis of dementia is often conceptual-

ized as a disease that cannot be treated. Summarizing the results regarding

Alzheimer’s disease: neuropathology, neuropsychological Chapter | 2 101

MCI treatment, the German guideline for dementia states that there is no evi-

dence for an effective pharmacological or nonpharmacological treatment to

reduce the risk of conversion toward dementia. This is a strong statement,

that is, on the one hand, correct because there are no randomized controlled

trials that show such a risk reduction. On the other hand, there are now many

indications that changing one’s lifestyle and enhancing somatic and mental

fitness can delay the progression of cognitive decline (Cremonini et al.,

2019; Hill, Goodwill, Gorelik, & Szoeke, 2019; Omar, 2019; Samadi,

Moradi, Moradinazar, Mostafai, & Pasdar, 2019). Epidemiological studies

have shown that there are at least some protective factors that may delay the

development of AD. One of the most important factors is education (Xu, Yu,

Tan, & Tan, 2015). People that have achieved higher levels of education

seem to be able to compensate for the progression of the disease for a longer

period of time and can therefore maintain independence. It is, however, not

entirely clear what this relationship between education and disease progres-

sion really implies. One interpretation is that in patients with higher educa-

tion deterioration also start from a higher level, therefore the disease

progression takes longer to reach such a state of mental decline where the

severity corresponds to a dementia syndrome. The other interpretation is that

higher education implies higher neuronal reserve capacities of the brain. This

would mean that the neuronal plasticity in these patients is higher than in

patients with less education (not unlike an epigenetic triggering of neuronal

regeneration). In this case, education would have a direct impact on neurode-

generation and slow its progression.

There are many studies that found an impact of education on the prevalence

of dementia. One very important study in this respect used genetically identical

twins and analyzed their social, professional and individual functioning during

their midlife years (Gatz et al., 2006; Mosing, Lundholm, Cnattingius, Gatz, &

Pedersen, 2018). In the case of genetically identical twins, the causes of demen-

tia must be environmental. The study found that the twin, who became affected

later, performed a more complex job decades before the dementia was diag-

nosed. This is an important finding because it implies that lifestyle modification

can prevent or delay dementia. Unfortunately this study encompassed patients

with all kinds of dementia and not only AD. Since higher job status is often

accompanied with different lifestyles and lifestyle is an important factor in car-

diac health and vascular brain lesions, we do not know if the results of this

study also have implications for the pathway to AD.

There are also several reviews that show that lifestyle factors and nutri-

tion may significantly impact dementia prevention or deceleration of cogni-

tive decline with a Mediterranean diet playing a major role (Aalbers, Baars,

& Rikkert, 2013; Lourida et al., 2013; Safouris, Tsivgoulis, Sergentanis, &

Psaltopoulou, 2015; Vassallo & Scerri, 2013). It is currently unclear whether

there is a direct causal role between the components of such diet and the

neuropathological process leading to AD or if the diet has a beneficial

impact on precursors and consequences of the AD-specific neuropathological

core process (e.g., increasing vascular health of the brain or decreasing neu-

roinflammatory processes related to AD).

When considering a direct effect of such a diet on the neuropathological

process of AD one could predict that nutrition effects the biomarkers typical

for AD. There is a very interesting experimental study on the possible effect

of nutrition on the progression of MCI patients. Bayer-Carter et al. (2011)

analyzed the effect of a diet with low saturated fat (,7%) compared with a

diet high in saturated fat (.25%) for 4 weeks. They assessed memory per-

formance and collected several blood and CSF markers including beta-

amyloid (142) and total tau concentration. The scientific background for

this study was that saturated fat may increase the risk of developing demen-

tia, whereas unsaturated fat (which is part of a Mediterranean diet) seems to

lower the risk. The study was double blinded, randomized and included a

control group. Special care was taken to ensure the patients did not gain or

lose body weight as this could have enhanced the risk of patients becoming

aware of their treatment condition. A total of 29 patients with MCI and 20

healthy controls were included and randomized into 2 by 2 groups. The

results showed that both groups with a low percentage of saturated fat in

their diets increased their memory performance. Furthermore the low to high

cholesterol ratio decreased in the low saturated fat diet group.

Additionally the beta-amyloid (142) concentration in the CSF increased

for the MCI group with the low saturated fat diet, but also for the healthy

controls with the high saturated fat diet. A later reevaluation of the beta-

amyloid (142) results, published in Baker et al. (2012), split the groups

into two groups: those with physical fitness training and those without. Only

the MCI patients with a low saturated fat diet and greater physical fitness

training showed an increase of beta-amyloid (142). The effects for the

healthy controls were just the opposite: only participants who received the

diet high in saturated fat and who completed minimal or no physical fitness

training displayed an increase of beta-amyloid (142) (Fig. 2.17). This latter

effect was not significant, but only a statistical trend.

The study by Bayer-Carter et al. (2011) was based on a small number of

patients (n5 29) and there were no follow-up data available. Thus we do not

know whether a diet low in saturated fat impacts the risk to convert to AD.

Nevertheless, it is very remarkable that 4 weeks of consuming a specific diet

increased delayed memory performance and seemed to have an impact on

the beta-amyloid (142) concentration in the CSF if the diet was combined

with fitness efforts. A recent review on diet and biomarkers in patients with

AD revealed that 13 of 15 studies manipulating the diet of the participants

(not necessarily patients in the state of AD) showed a significant change in

total tau or beta-amyloid (142) (Hill et al., 2019). Six of these studies

implemented a Mediterranean-style diet and described a reduction in the AD

biomarker burden, although the effect size was low.

2.9.2 Treatment of Alzheimer’s disease patients with neuropsychological interventions

In Germany, using neuropsychological training to treat AD patients was a

very controversial topic during the 19902010. Many colleagues were con-

vinced that this type of treatment would only frustrate patients and even

damage their self-esteem. Meanwhile, this once common opinion has slightly

shifted and neuropsychological interventions for dementia patients are begin-

ning to receive more acceptance. The comparison with PD patients receiving

physiotherapy, despite suffering from a neurodegenerative disease that can-

not be cured, has paved the way for a neuropsychological treatment of AD

patients. It has become more and more clear that patients only become frus-

trated when the therapist is not properly educated on how to choose the cor-

rect interventions and goal setting algorithms.

During the past few years, the reviews on neuropsychological treatments of

AD patients also changed their “tone.” Older reviews, in accordance with their

time, stated that evidence was low or even absent (Clare, Woods, Moniz Cook,

Orrell, & Spector, 2003; Spector, Orrell, Davies, & Woods, 2000), whereas

more recent reviews permit a more optimistic view (Alves et al., 2013; Choi &

Twamley, 2013; Spector, Orrell, & Hall, 2012). Two recent reviews proposed

a small effect of cognitive training in dementia using computerized interven-

tions (Hill et al., 2017) (Fig. 2.20) or cognitive stimulation (Huntley, Gould,

Liu, Smith, & Howard, 2015) (Fig. 2.21). It should be noted that both these

reviews and others (Carrion, Folkvord, Anastasiadou, & Aymerich, 2018;

Huntley et al., 2015) indicate a rather low, albeit significant effect size of

about 0.26 or 0.35 in studies with active control groups as comparators.

Furthermore these studies often include patients with dementia due to AD but

also due to vascular causes and therefore it is unclear if the results also hold

for purified (only AD or only by vascular diseases caused) patient groups.

Romero and Wenz (2002) developed an intervention termed

“Selbsterhaltungstherapie” (Self-Maintenance-Therapy), which uses existing

biographical information of the patients to help them recall former life events

and thus to sustain an autobiographic identity. This treatment uses already

learned information to train the recall process and to emotionally validate the

patients. Since former experience has a high meaning for the patients, their

motivation to recall the information should be high. A high motivation is cru-

cial for any kind of rehabilitative treatment. Patients and their relatives seem

to profit from this treatment as expressed through less depressive feelings.

Self-maintenance therapy is one specific approach of reminiscence ther-

apy, which is based on recall of life events. Reminiscence therapy is most

often performed as a group treatment involving the discussion of past activi-

ties, events, and experiences. Two recent meta-analytical reviews analyzed

the effectiveness of reminiscence therapy in AD. The Cochrane review of

Woods, O’Philbin, Farrell, Spector, and Orrell (2018) judged that most of

the previous studies on reminiscence therapy were well-conducted, but often

included only a small number of patients. Reminiscence therapy improved

global cognition immediately after the treatment, but this effect was small

and did not enduring over months. The positive emotional effects of reminis-

cence therapy were also small and concerned depressive mood and no other

behavioral disturbances. Moreover, this effect seemed to be higher in

patients living in care homes and not present in patients living in the commu-

nity. The review by Cuevas, Davidson, Mejilla, and Rodney (2020) added

that the number of treatment sessions play a major role in the efficacy of

reminiscence therapy and that encouragements to remember should be

adjusted for each patient (computer promptings might not have an effect in

patients who lack experienced with this technology).

Most of the studies on reminiscence therapy are based on comparisons

with untreated control groups. One large study compared cognitive training,

reminiscence therapy, individualized cognitive rehabilitation therapy, and a

Barban et al. (2016) combined cognitive training with reminiscence ther-

apy in a multicenter, randomized, and controlled trial involving 348 partici-

patients with amnestic MCI, or patients with mild AD. The training encom-

months. Thirty minutes were spent on cognitive training and 30 minutes on

questions about facts and events regarding the past of the participants.

Cognitive training targeted episodic memory, attention and executive func-

tions. The training led to improvements in delayed recall in all groups and in

improvement in executive functions only for the elderly controls. Amnestic

MCI patients and AD patients showed higher MMSE scores after the training

(the elderly controls could not improve in the MMSE) and AD patients

improved in neuropsychiatric disorders. Unfortunately the effects of the AD

patients were present only immediately after the training, whereas the effects

in the amnestic MCI and the elderly controls could still be shown during the

follow-up, that is, after 3 months.

2.9.3 Summary

There is evidence that lifestyle changes may decelerate the progression of

cognitive decline in patients with MCI as well as in patients with AD.

Therefore patients should be informed that changing their lifestyle in the

direction of physical and cognitive training and following a Mediterranean

diet might reduce the risk of progression or might reduce the speed of cogni-

tive decline. It should also be mentioned that such a lifestyle change must be

intensive and followed almost every day.

In contrast to earlier views, recent studies have shown that cognitive

training and cognitive rehabilitation of patients with MCI and dementia does

have an impact on their global cognitive performance. There seems to be a

difference between MCI patients and those with dementia: In MCI patients,

improvement in memory seems to be greater than in global cognitive perfor-

mance, whereas in patients with dementia the main effects concern global

cognitive performance—possible due to floor effects in memory testing.

Cognitive training in MCIs could therefore be more specific than in AD.

Some studies have shown that the intervention effects in amnestic MCI

patients are stable over a longer period, whereas they seem to disappear

quickly in the AD group.

Most of the studies included MCI patients and patients with dementia

suffering from various etiologies. Therefore the effect of neuropsychological

interventions in the treatment of “pure” AD patients is less clear.

Nevertheless, after purifying for etiology there is still at least one meta-

analytic review (Alves et al., 2013) indicating that cognitive stimulation may

improve global cognitive performance in AD patients.

Treatment of cognitive decline in MCI and AD has followed four strate-

gies: concentrating on single functions (e.g., memory), realization of cogni-

tive training in the context of repeating social events and meetings, focusing

on autobiographical knowledge and using external memory aids for recalling

the recent past. Focusing on single functions works better in MCI than in

AD patients, whereas implementing training aspects in social meetings seem

to work better in AD patients. This is especially true for reminiscence ther-

apy in nursing homes. Using a SenseCam for cued recall of recent biographi-

cal events is an interesting development which should be investigated

There is a definite lack of studies that focus on the more specific

impairment of AD patients: a loss of recollection overcompensated by a reli-

ance on familiarity, that is, an increased number of memory errors in this

patient group.

2.10 Alzheimer’s disease and neuropsychological models of false positives in recognition

Alzheimer’s patients differ from many (but not all) other patients suffering

from a severe amnesia in the number of memory errors they perform.

Patients with amnesia may show a conservative bias toward their own

remembrances, AD patients, however, often insist on memories that are not

correct. As stated in the abovementioned sections, the number of false posi-

tive responses during recognition testing may be even more specific for AD

patients than the free recall performance or global recognition scores when

compared with demented patients due to other organic causes.

One explanation for the increase number of memory errors is that AD

patients show a more liberal response bias, that is, saying “yes” more often

during recognition than other groups. In cognitive terms this would mean

that they show less top-down checking of memory activations, for example,

due to a global deficit in executive functions or a specific impairment of

postretrieval monitoring processes. A plausible explanation could be that

because of beta-amyloid (142) deposition the disease also involves orbito-

frontal cortical areas in Braak stages I and II.

Results from experimental studies on response bias in recognition perfor-

mance seem to coincide with this idea demonstrating a more liberal response

bias in AD patients compared with older healthy (Beth, Budson, Waring, &

Ally, 2009; Deason, Tat, et al., 2017; Russo et al., 2017). A more liberal

114 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

response bias may be due to a change in the decision criterion. However, dif-

ferences in memory performance may also play a role. To look for the

impact of objective performance differences, Budson, Wolk, Chong, and

Waring (2006) enhanced the difficulty of the recognition task by increasing

the number of words. Budson et al. (2006) found that such a variation in task

difficulty did not alter the response bias in both AD patients and elderly con-

trols, even if the task was so challenging that the controls exhibited similar

performances as the AD patients. The fact that AD patients showed a more

liberal response bias even in the condition in which healthy controls had

the same recognition problems as the patients suggests a memory perfor-

mance independent inflation of positive responses in the AD.

However, increasing the difficulty of the memory tasks for healthy

elderly to equalize their performance with that of AD patients may not be

the best way to study whether the inflated number of false positives is due to

a more liberal response bias. The healthy controls will notice that the task

becomes increasingly arduous and therefore they may consciously shift to an

even more conservative response bias to compensate. A better strategy to

analyse the origin of the high number of false positives is to increase the

delay between the study phase and the test phase in healthy controls until the

recognition performance is the same as in AD patients. Deason, Hussey,

Ally, and Budson (2012) followed this strategy and compared healthy young

adults, healthy elderly and AD patients. They found that delaying the test

phase increased the liberal response bias in the two healthy control groups to

the level of the AD group. Moreover, the discrimination performance

between correct and incorrect items also decreased. Thus the liberal bias in

healthy younger and older adults could result from relying primarily on

familiarity after a long delay between learning and recall, but not after short

delays where recollection is still effective enough. Thus after long enough

delays healthy controls may use similar memory processes as AD patients,

who depend on familiarity regardless of delay. Or in other words, since rec-

ollection is weaker for AD, they use familiarity-based responses more

quickly than patients without AD also suffering from dementia. Hudon,

Belleville, and Gauthier (2009), while using a “remember” and “know” para-

digm, found that AD patients used the “know” feeling more often to catego-

rize an item as an old and learned one, which argues for a dominance of

familiarity in their recognition memory. Furthermore although familiarity-

based processing is somewhat impaired in AD patients, recollection is much

more impaired. The deficit in familiarity processing however may differ

between stimuli and may concern only complex and similar items early in

the disease (Ally, Hussey, Ko, & Molitor, 2013).

“fluency.” Items that are highly fluent are processed easily and tend to evoke

larger feelings of familiarity than less fluent items. Gold, Marchant,
