Overview treatment options

The following table with guidelines presents a short summary of the sections on

treatment from each of the chapters dealing with a specific syndrome in this

book. The relevant sections from different chapters are indicated in the table,

and the reader will find more information in these sections. The amount of evi-

dence for a specific intervention is indicated in the table as follows: might

means weak evidence; can means somewhat better evidence; should means

moderate-to-strong evidence or ethical commitment for providing treatment.

For cross-checking especially of the pharmacological proposals of the

table consult the German guideline for dementia: https://www.awmf.org/

uploads/tx\_szleitlinien/038-013l\_S3-Demenzen-2016-07.pdf

Amnestic mild cognitive impairment \[preclinical stage of Alzheimer’s disease (AD)\] (Section 2.9.1)

Cognitive training Best evidence for multidomain training affecting memory, attention, and executive functions, which should be provided. Computers can be used, and the treatment can be performed in groups if the cognitive impairment is relatively low.

In the case of strategy training for memory rehabilitation, the itemspecific encoding approach as specific variant of an appropriate transfer processing training might be helpful to avoid inflation of memory errors. This strategy training combines semantic structuring (deep encoding) with the detection of item-specific features.

In patients suffering from stroke or traumatic brain injury at absolute minimum 15 training sessions are necessary, which should be provided in 35 weeks. If possible, the training should be continued at home with a family member or a friend.

Psychoeducation, counseling

In the case of a high risk for conversion to dementia counseling, how to proceed with professional work should be offered (e.g., as cognitive behavioral therapy).

Patients should be informed about progression reducing life-style changes: mediterranean diet plus intensive physical and cognitive exercising. Before starting with physical exercising, the patient should ask the general practitioner about restrictions related to cardiac or other diseases. Cognitive exercising at home may involve reading newspaper articles and telling the content hours later. Exercising should be realized on a daily basis.

Other treatments Food supplements (like Souvenaid) might be used.

The results for Ginkgo biloba extract EGb 761 are mixed (in contrast to patients suffering from dementia).

Pharmacological intervention

No definite results for any pharmacological intervention, acetylcholine esterase inhibitors seem to be ineffective

Alzheimer’s disease (Section 2.9.2)

Cognitive training Mild dementia: individually adapted multidomain cognitive training might be tried out.

Skill learning for everyday tasks individualized and provided as face to face intervention might to be effective.

SenseCam usage might be helpful.

Mild-to-moderate dementia: reminiscence therapy targeting biographical information can be performed (in groups and in nursery homes)

Cognitive stimulation training improves orientation and independence in nursery homes (in demented patients with various etiologies)

Psychoeducation, counseling

Relatives should be informed that a firm structure of the day reduces the experience of stress and that new environments and changes of rooms should be avoided.

Patients should be informed about progression reducing lifestyle changes: mediterranean diet plus intensive physical and cognitive exercising (see the information for amnestic mild cognitive impairment).

Other treatments One randomized controlled trials study showed that daily intensive physical exercising decrease disease progression.

One study argues that the usage of tDCS as additional stimulation might be ineffective for improving memory.

Pharmacological intervention

Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (mild-to-moderate stage) should be provided.

Memantine (moderate-to-severe stage) might be provided.

Ginkgo biloba extract EGb 761 delays cognitive decline and reduces neuropsychiatric disorders without relevant side effects. The clinical relevance of this reduction is still disputed. Moreover, not all studies focussed on AD but on dementia.

Parkinson’s disease (Section 3.2.5 and Section 3.3.3)

Cognitive training Nondemented patients

Multimodal intensive training can improve visuospatial attention and reduce impairment in tasks that predict car accidents during driving.

Working memory training adapted to the level of impairment of the patients shows a high effect size.

Combination of cognitive training (working memory and executive function) and motor training should be effective during rehabilitation and also at home.

Nondemented patients with specific movement disorders

In the case of freezing of gait, divided attention and visuospatial impairments may be the targets of cognitive training. Patients should also be trained to reduce distraction focusing on cognitive tasks when moving and navigating through complex spatial environments.

Demented patients

No studies performed

Psychoeducation, counseling

Nondemented patients

For patients suffering from depressive mood, psychotherapy or antidepressant might/can be necessary.

Demented patients

In the case of depressive mood, combined noradrenergic and serotonergic drugs might have a better effect because targeting apathy, sleepiness, and mood disorder.

Other treatments Several studies showed that intensive gait therapy improves gait in PD.

In the case of dysphagia, swallowing training and strategies for swallowing might be introduced.

Pharmacological intervention

Nondemented patients

L-Dopa or dopamine-agonists can be helpful for working memory and executive function but not for memory and visuospatial dysfunctions.

Avoid mirtazapine for treating the sleep disorder, because of aversive side effects in the presence of a REM sleep behavioral disorder or restless legs. Melatonin might be helpful.

Demented patients

Acetylcholine esterase inhibitors can increase attention and memory and decrease the risk of neuropsychiatric symptoms.

Lewy body disease (Section 3.4.4)

Cognitive training No studies performed

Psychoeducation, counseling

LBD patients often suffer from severe sleep disorder and show severe executive of visuoconstructive deficits in everyday life. Relatives should be informed about the meaning of visuoperceptive and visuoconstructional impairments and environmental changes at home such as arrows signs pointing to the toilet. Restricting access to closets should be part of counseling, because patient tends to misarrange items without being able to replace them correctly.

Other treatments Specific motor trainings have not yet been evaluated.

Pharmacological intervention

Acetylcholine esterase inhibitors and memantine can be useful. Avoid neuroleptic drugs if possible.

Avoid mirtazapine for treating the sleep disorder because of aversive side effects in the presence of an REM sleep behavioral disorder and restless leg. Melatonin might be helpful.

Primary progressive aphasia (Section 5.8)

Cognitive training Semantic therapy might be helpful: combination of word facilitation by semantic and phonological cues and reading or writing of the words seem to delay increasing word findings and production problems \[but only for logopenic primary progressive aphasia (lPPA) and nonfluent PPA (nfPPA)\].

Semantic therapy and tDCS stimulation above the left premotor cortex might have long-lasting effects concerning the progression of the word finding problems \[but only for lPPA and nfPPA, not for semantic dementia (SD)\]

Script training (shadowing of speech and imitation of mouth movements during speech) might improve articulatory speed and word production especially in nfPPA patients.

Verbal working memory training might be tried out in lPPA

Psychoeducation, counseling

Look for leisure activities, which can be done without much verbal communication.

In the case of nfPPA: reading aloud might help to delay the progressive loss of fluent speech and may be done together with a relative.

Relatives of patients with SD should be informed about the increased risk of behavioral problems.

Other treatments In the case of dysphagia, swallowing training and strategies for swallowing might be introduced.

Pharmacological intervention

Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors might be helpful in lPPA

Progressive supranuclear paralysis (PSP), corticobasal degeneration (CBD)/corticobasal syndrome (CBS), and behavioral frontotemporal dementia (bvFTD) (Section 4.5 and

Section 6.6)

Cognitive training No studies performed

Psychoeducation, counseling

PSP, CBD/CBS, bvFTD

Information of the relatives on the decline in affective empathy and social understanding capacities of the patients might reduce emotional distress induced by the behavior of the patients.

386 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

Relatives and care givers should be educated to avoid confrontative situation increasing the risk of disruptive behavior of the patients.

Free access to financial resources should be limited.

Relatives should be informed about the increase risk of “criminal” behavior on the side of the affected person.

Other treatments PSP, CBD/CBS

Intensive physiotherapy might enhance walking ability.

In the case of dysphagia, swallowing training and strategies for swallowing might be introduced.

Pharmacological intervention

No causally effective pharmacological drugs available but symptomatic treatment might be helpful in single cases.

High dose of L-dopa may improve motor functions in PSP.

Disinhibition in bvFTD might respond to a treatment with selective serotonin inhibitors.

Neuropsychiatric impairments might be treated with Risperidone.
