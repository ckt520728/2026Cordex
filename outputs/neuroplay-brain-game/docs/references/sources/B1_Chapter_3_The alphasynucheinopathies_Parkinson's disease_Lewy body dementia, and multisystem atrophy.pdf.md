The alphasynucleinopathies: Parkinson’s disease, Lewy body dementia, and multisystem atrophy

3.1 General summary

The major symptom of the alphasynucleinopthies is the slowing of move-

ment during the initiation and execution of actions, which is called bradyki-

nesia. The anamnesis of such patients should encompass the functions of

autonomous nervous system, sleeping (focusing on rapid eye movement

(REM) sleep behavior disorder), and smelling. Moreover a screening for

depressive mood is necessary.

To suspect Parkinson’s disease (PD) at least one of the following symp-

toms should also be present: resting tremor, rigor, or postural instability.

Nondemented patients with PD exhibit working memory deficits, cognitive

slowing in visual search tasks and visuoperceptive and visuoconstructive def-

icits. Working memory deficits should be tested with a spatial self-ordered

pointing task, cognitive slowing with the trail making test (TMT). PD

patients should be assessed with visuoconstructive tasks and failing in such

tasks may predict a higher risk for dementia. Deficits in visuoperceptive

tasks might be associated with developing hallucinations. The impact of cog-

nitive deficits on instrumental activities of daily living is unclear; however,

they seem to correlate with car accidents.

PD patients may benefit from cognitive training. Cognitive performance and

motor impairment interact closely because the PD patients require cognitive

processing to compensate for the motor decline. Thus treatment should also

focus on the execution of cognitive tasks during physiotherapeutic training.

For PD patients with dementia, the assessment concerns the same func-

tions as in nondemented PD patients, but the tasks should be adapted to the

severity of the deficits. Parkinson’s disease with dementia (PDD) patients

will display severe attentional deficits along with severe slowing in execut-

ing test instructions. Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors may improve cognitive

performance and decrease the tendency for visual hallucinations.

Patients with Lewy body disease (LBD) suffer from attentional deficits

including short- and long-term attentional fluctuations. Fluctuations and

visuoperceptive disorders are predictors for visual hallucinations, which are a

prominent sign of LBD. If visual hallucinations are present, one should

exclude low-level visual deficits, which may also lead to hallucination unre-

lated to LBD.

Presenting symptoms of multiple system atrophy (MSA) are autonomic

nervous system, cerebellar, and dysarthric disorders. However, MSA patients

only show subtle deficits in cognitive functioning. They tend to perform

impaired in tests of cognitive speed, higher order motor control, and cogni-

tive flexibility.

3.2 Parkinson’s disease

3.2.1 Clinical symptoms

During the past decades, the criteria for diagnosing PD in clinical routine

practice has changed several times; however, there are several features that

remain the core feature of PD (Fig. 3.1). The first and major characteristic is

the slowing of movement during the initiation and execution of voluntary

actions. This is often tested by repetitive finger tapping, hand opening, and

so forth, and by observing if the velocity of the movement and the amplitude

of the movement are decreasing. The slowing of the movement is called bra-

dykinesia. Bradykinesia alone is, however, not enough to suspect PD. At

least one of the following symptoms should also be present:

 Resting tremor with 46 Hz, that is, alternating 4 or 6 times per second

 Rigor, that is, involuntary increased muscle tonus during passive move-

ment, often also present as cogwheel phenomenon.

 Instability of postural reflexes. This also concerns gait, which is often

slowed and executed with a small left- or right-ward deviation.

There are some features of these symptoms which make them more char-

acteristic for the diagnosis of PD. For example, early in the disease the rest-

ing tremor might start distally and unilaterally, which is more typical for PD.

The symptoms should worsen with disease duration but remain different for

the both body sides. The resting tremor should be more pronounced during

cognitive distraction giving no attention to the shaking hand or leg.

The most important hint for PD is the response to L-dopa medication.

With L-dopa treatment, patients should exhibit near complete recovery from

the symptoms. L-dopa medication related recovery should be present for at

least 5 years and the disease progression should be, in general, relatively

slow (survival time longer than 10 years). If L-dopa response is considerably

low and is decreasing during the first years, one would expect an atypical

Parkinson’s syndrome.

trolled by the vagal nerve. The “infection” theory of alphasynucleinopathies

postulates that alphasynuclein misfolding starts in the enteric nervous sys-

tem. Misfolded proteins subsequently leave the affected neuron and are

absorbed by the next neuron via synapses. Inside the new neuron the mis-

folded protein begins to influence other proteins to misfold, which is how

the disease proliferates. If this theory is correct, the disease should reach the

brainstem at the major projection areas of the vagal nerve—the nucleus of

the solitary tract and the dorsal vagal nucleus at the level of the medulla

oblongata. Therefore the integration of autonomous signals may be ham-

pered, which would explain why, for example, hypotension may develop.

The dorsal motor vagal nucleus also controls the innervation of the muscle

innervating the vocal fold. A silent and monotonous voice is one of the earli-

est signs of PD. Two recent studies from Arora et al. (2018) showed that a

voice probe analyzed solely by statistical algorithms enabled high risk

patients for developing PD (i.e., those with REM sleep behavioral disorder)

to be distinguished from healthy controls. A comparison with other symp-

toms revealed that a silent and monotonous voice has a higher sensitivity to

detect patients suffering from early PD than motor signs and cognitive

performance.

The dorsal nucleus of the vagal nerve and the nucleus of the solitary tract

are located in the medulla oblongata (caudal to the pons) and adjacent to the

nuclei raphe magnus, raphe pallidus, and raphe obscurus. The serotonergic

neurons of these latter nuclei projection densely to the intermediate zone of

the spinal gray matter and are known to participate in topdown pain modu-

lation. Kass-Iliyya, Kobylecki, McDonald, Gerhard, and Silverdale (2015)

recently compared the amount of pain perception for patients with alphasy-

nucleionopathies (PD and MSA) and patients with progressive supranuclear

palsy (PSP). Only a few patients with PSP suffered from increased pain per-

ception, whereas 15%20% of the patients with alphasynucleinopathies

experienced chronic pain. One interpretation of this finding is that in the lat-

ter group topdown control of pain is impaired due to a neuropathological

involvement of the raphe nucleus, whereas in the PSP patients the relevant

tau pathology is located more dorsal in the midbrain.

The nucleus of the solitary tract is small but long and extends into the

brainstem at the level of the pons. Here adjacent nuclei are the facial nucleus

and the paramedian pontine reticular formation (or nucleus praepositus),

which is the control center for horizontal eye movements. A mask-like face,

due to hypokinetic modulation of the facial muscles, is one of the core signs

of PD and its onset occurs very early in the disease. Concerning horizontal

eye movements, a recent study from Brooks et al. (2017) demonstrated that

MSA patients already displayed increased latencies for saccade movements

Further at the level of the pons, two important control centers for micturi-

Two other prodromal signs of PD should be mentioned in this context.

The first one concerns depression. Depressive mood is often associated with

PD \[but also with the atypical PD of PSP and corticobasal degeneration

(CBD)\]. A recent study of Almeida et al. (2017) showed that 28% of PD and

40% of MSA patients already suffered from a depressive mood at their first

visit and more than 50% developed a depressive mood during the course of

PD. The standard theory of depression relates the symptoms to a serotonin

and noradrenaline deficit, whereby antidepressant drugs enhance the avail-

ability of these neurotransmitters to reduce the symptom severity. A recent

positron emission tomography (PET) study revealed that a serotonergic defi-

cit due to a loss of serotonergic neurons seems to be more important during

the early state of PD than a loss of dopaminergic neurons (Wilson et al.,

2019). In this study in the earliest patients no dopaminergic deficit was evi-

dent, but a serotonergic loss in the brainstem, midbrain (5different raphe

nuclei), and limbic centers was displayed.

It was already mentioned that the high frequency of pain perception

might be related to a neuropathological involvement of the nucleus raphe

magnus. The nucleus raphe dorsalis is located at the level of the pons and

dorsal to the midbrain where it projects to several cerebral areas responsible

for the regulation of mood. The locus coeruleus, a noradrenergic nucleus, is

also located in the upper pons, whereas the substantia nigra, the origin of

dopaminergic modulation, is located more rostral in the midbrain. Thus the

high frequency of depressive mood in prodromal PD (before the neuropatho-

logical process really touches the midbrain and the substantia nigra) fits with

the idea of a disease process that starts in brainstem and later progresses to

the midbrain and the cerebrum.

Finally different brainstem nuclei play an important role in controlling

motor atonia during REM sleep. A loss of this control, that is, showing

movements during REM sleep, that seems to be correlated with dream con-

tent, is a well-known syndrome called idiopathic REM sleep behavioral dis-

order (iRBD). Suffering from iRBD is a strong predictor for developing PD,

MSA, or LBD. A review by Boeve (2010) showed that iRBD is strongly

associated with the alphasynucleinopathies. In contrast iRBD is not affiliated

with tauopathies (e.g., PSP and CBD) or Alzheimer’s disease (AD)

(Fig. 3.3).

version rate from iRBD to an overt neurodegenerative syndrome is 6.3% per

3.2.2.1 Summary

A combination of a silent and monotonous voice, hypotension, and constipa-

tion reported by the patient should bias the remaining anamnesis toward ask-

ing for further disorders related to the autonomous nervous system (e.g.,

problems with erectile dysfunctions in male patients), sleep, smelling disor-

der, and depression. Moreover these patients and their relatives should be

assessed with an idiopathic RBD questionnaire (Table 3.1) and neuropsycho-

logical tests focusing on figure ground completion and object closure.

Apart from working memory, PD patients are often described as being

impaired in EFs. The term EFs encompasses different cognitive processes

and therefore studies on EF in PD patients cannot be compared directly. On

a conceptual level the term EF is remarkably broad and consequently diffi-

cult to define. Hence the use of EF does not really add to the description of

cognitive impairments in PD patients. The use of the term EF when diagnos-

ing cognitive impairments in PD patients should be avoided. Instead, the

important features—working memory, disinhibition, and action control—

should be focused on.

3.2.4 Cognitive impairment and the functional independence of the patients

Investigations about the relation between cognitive performance and depar-

ture from the workforce for PD patients are rare. Two recent reviews on the

topic of PD-provoked retirement do not even mention studies relying on

neuropsychological test results (most likely due to the fact that there are no

larger studies). Nevertheless they both simultaneously argue that nonmotor

symptoms, such as mental slowing and fatigue, play a role in this decision

(aside from demographic aspects and postural instability of the patients)

(Armstrong et al., 2014; Koerts, Konig, Tucha, & Tucha, 2016).

Driving abilities in patients with PD relative to their cognitive abilities

have been explored more extensively. Two systematic reviews by

Jitkritsadakul and Bhidayasiri (2016) and Devos, Ranchet, Emmanuel

Akinwuntan, and Uc (2015) revealed that poor driving performance corre-

lates independently with motor, visual, and cognitive impairments. On the

other hand, large scale, high level prospective studies on the relationship

between cognitive impairment and driving performance in PD patients are

lacking PD patients themselves seem to notice when to stop driving or at

least when avoid rush hour times or long tours. However, there appears to be

also a smaller subgroup of PD patients who do not notice when they should

discontinue driving.

The cognitive assessment of the PD patients to predict their driving

capacities differed between the studies, therefore it is unclear, which of the

neuropsychological tests is the best to predict the risk of an accident. The

study of Uc et al. (2007) revealed that different cognitive impairments may

be related to different kinds of accident risks: visuospatial deficits with

incorrect left turns and safety distance to other vehicles, impairments in

working memory and memory with navigational losses and problems in

remembering the position of other cars.

Leung et al. (2015) reviewed the effect of cognitive training in PD without

dementia following the Cochrane criteria. Thus they only included random-

that fulfilled the criteria of an RCT. The effect of cognitive training was ana-

lyzed for processing speed, EFs, working memory, memory, attention, visuo-

spatial functions, and global cognition. Although it seems to be difficult to

distinguish between these functions due to the fact, that, for example, the dif-

ference between attention, working memory, and EF is notoriously vague,

Leung et al. (2015) found clear differences in the effectiveness of cognitive

training related to cognitive subprocesses. Neither memory, attention, visuo-

spatial functions nor overall global cognition improved through cognitive

training. The effect of the training on EFs and processing speed was mild

albeit statistically significant (Hedge’s g of about 0.3). Working memory

outperformed all other cognitive functions with a Hedge’s g value of 0.74

(or of 0.62 after outlier control) (Figs. 3.10 and 3.11).

number of studies, the current body of RCT evidence indicates that cognitive

3.3.1 Criteria for Parkinson’s disease with dementia, risk factors for conversion, and meaning of cognitive impairment for activities of daily living

The Movement Disorders Society (MDS) task force criteria for PDD encom-

affected causing a decline in daily (social, professional, or own supply)

the daily life activities must be independent of the motor or autonomic symp-

toms caused by PD. The affected cognitive functions should involve atten-

tion, EF, visual-spatial functions, and free recall from memory. Language

functions are more or less unimpaired except for subtle word finding pro-

blems. Furthermore more than one cognitive function should be affected by

the cognitive decline. The specific meaning of these impairments and their

possible relation to the disease process were already discussed in the section

concerning cognitive impairments in nondemented PD patients.

The MDS task force also proposed several characteristic changes in

behavioral functioning (Emre et al., 2007). To fulfilling the criteria of

dementia, PDD patients should suffer from apathy, changes in personality

and mood including depressive symptoms and anxiety, hallucinations (mostly

visual), delusions, and daytime sleepiness.

How often do patients with PD develop dementia? Various studies

reviewed by Emre (2003) on prevalence and incidence of PDD have

shown that 6%8% of the PD patients are already afflicted with dementia

during their first diagnosis of PD. It should be mentioned that the age of

the patients is of extreme relevance here (probably not only due to the

age-related increased risk to suffer from dementia but also to factors like

However, up to an astounding 78% of the patients develop a dementia

for dementia is 5.1 times higher for Parkinson patients compared to

PDD. The first risk is advanced with higher age at the initial diagnosis.

Moreover patients with PDD often suffer from a longer duration of the dis-

ease. They often display depressed moods and low responses to L-dopa.

Additionally, the motor symptoms are also related to the risk of developing

PDD. Patients with rigor (especially if bilateral rigor is present) and akinesia

as primary motor symptoms, with a pronounced gait disorder and problems

ties of daily living through their motor deficits. In relation to motor deficits

and cognitive decline, the following question “Does cognitive decline add

to this impairment?” arises. Bronnick et al. (2006) analyzed this question in

a large group of PDD patients (n5 461) who scored 2.7 on the Hoehn and

Yahr scale. A score of 2.7 on the Hoehn and Yahr scale means that the

patients exhibited mild to moderate motor impairments. Bronnick et al.

some clinical judgments on word finding problems and spoken language

abilities. After factor analysis, three different cognitive components could

be extracted: one for attention, one for language performance, and one for

verbal memory. A similar factor analysis for activities in daily living

(ADL) also revealed three factors: one for physical ADL (dressing, eating,

etc.), one for instrumental ADL (household activities), and one for leisure

activities. Correlational analyses showed that attention performance was

associated with physical ADL and verbal memory with leisure activities. In

a more recent study, Liepelt-Scarfone et al. (2013) compared a small group

of 30 PDD patients with and without impairments in instrumental activities

of daily living for motor and cognitive impairment. PDD patients with

impairments in instrumental ADL showed more postural instability,

increased anxiety and significant deficits in attention and in visuoconstruc-

tion. Together with the study conducted by Bronnick et al. (2006), the find-

ings argue that even in a mild to moderate state of motor impairment the

impact of cognitive decline still contributes to the degree of independence

in instrumental ADL.

3.3.2 Prediction of conversation to Parkinson’s disease with dementia by neuropsychological tests

As previously stated, Pentagram copying was the only cognitive task that

predicted cognitive decline for newly diagnosed PD patients in the seminal

study from Foltynie et al. (2004). Several other studies investigated the pos-

sibility to predict conversion to PD dementia using cognitive tests. Johnson

et al. (2016) followed up 64 PD patients over a period of more than 5 years

and found that deterioration began with working memory impairments (digit

span backward), followed by visuospatial impairments, whereas episodic

memory tests and attention test declined slowly, but without a tipping point.

Janvin, Larsen, Aarsland, and Hugdahl (2006) studied different subtypes

of mild cognitive impairment in patients with PD. They distinguished

between cognitively intact PD patients, those with single domain impair-

ments (amnestic vs nonamnestic) and those with multidomain cognitive

impairments. After 4 years, only patients with nonamnestic cognitive impair-

ments (single domain or multidomain) showed a significantly increased risk

for advancing to dementia. The conversion rate of initially cognitive unim-

paired individuals who developed dementia during the 4 years was high (the

conversion rate was equal to 20%). Only the combination of cognitive tests

into compound scores differed between the PD patients that progress, single

tests, however, did not differ. On the other hand, depressive mood at the

beginning of the study alone showed an increase risk for converting.

Studies comparing AD patients and PD patients in different states

revealed that patients with AD show specific impairments in memory, espe-

cially in cued recall and in recognition (Hildebrandt et al., 2013; Saka &

Elibol, 2009), whereas PD patients in a state of MCI or dementia suffer from

impairments in visuoconstructive functions and in psychomotor slowing

when compared to AD patients in the same global cognitive state. In addi-

tion, after reviewing the predictive power of neuropsychological assessment

for converting into a dementia state, Kehagia et al. (2013) argue that visuo-

constructive performance is more important than, for example, word fluency

or recognition memory performance for the prognosis. This may indicate

that the progression to dementia in PD is accompanied (or caused) by the

involvement of the cholinergic neurotransmitter. Although patients without

dementia suffer primarily from a dopaminergic modulation deficit of middle-

sized spiny basal ganglia cells, those with dementia also show an impairment

in cholinergic modulation.

There are four major neuropsychiatric characteristics that announce the

development of dementia or start as soon as the state of dementia is reached.

The first characteristic concerns apathy, which is almost always enhanced

during the transition to dementia. Development or increase of depressive

mood is a very common sign of the conversion to dementia and is herewith

the second major neuropsychiatric characteristic. Moreover PDD patients

often suffer from delusions and especially hallucinations which are the third

and fourth major characteristics. In this case it is not clear whether the medi-

cation also plays a role. Some studies documented a relationship between

dopaminergic medication and hallucination; however, these findings were

only reserved for several studies. In clinical practice, the level of dopaminer-

gic medication is reduced or changed from dopaminergic agonists to L-dopa.

This is, however, not always helpful. Hallucination and minor hallucinations,

that is, illusionary visual perceptions, are common in PD patients and may

be present late in the disease course in approximately 40% of the patients

irrespective of the pharmacological treatment.

3.3.3 Treatment of Parkinson’s disease with dementia

Functional neuropsychological treatment studies on PDD patients are rare. A

recent review by Seppi et al. (2019) was unable to detect even one RCT for

cognitive impairment in this patient group. On the other hand promising

treatments for autonomic dysfunctions, depression, sleep wake dysregula-

tions, and so forth, have been published, but are still in their infancies. The

3.3.4 Summary

A major problem for the diagnosis of dementia in PD is that many of the

patients are already negatively compromised in instrumental activities of

daily living due to their motor impairment. Therefore the anamnesis should

focus on specific cognitive impairments which add to the detrimental effect

of the motor impairment.

The neuropsychological assessment concerns the same functions as in

nondemented PD patients, but the tasks should be adapted to the deficits,

which will be more pronounced. PDD patients will especially display severe

attentional deficits along with severe slowing in executing any test instruc-

tions. Moreover visuoperceptive and visuoconstructive deficits will prevail,

whereas recognition memory scores may still be borderline. Tests that should

be used for the assessment of visuoperception as the visual object perception

battery and the Birmingham Object Recognition Battery (BORB), both of

which will be discussed later in the section regarding Lewy Body Dementia.

3.4 Lewy body dementia

3.4.1 Clinical criteria and the relation to Parkinson’s disease dementia

LBD concerns a cognitive behavioral syndrome severe enough to be diag-

nosed as a clinically relevant cognitive impairment or even as a state of

dementia. LBD shares some important features with PD or PDD, however,

the motor deficits that would allow for a PD diagnosis are not present. As

far as cognitive and behavioral features are concerned, LBD differs from

PDD in quantitative but not in qualitative terms. LBD and PDD both show

early impairments in attention, visuoperceptive processing, and EFs. For

both syndromes, patients suffer from cognitive fluctuations and mainly visual

hallucinations. The main difference between the syndromes concerns the

occurrence of motor signs. The presence of motor impairments is clearly

indicative of PD. Therefore timepoint at which the typical motor symptoms

occur determines which diagnosis is correct: PD/PDD or LBD. Earlier con-

sensus criterion for this decision was that a diagnosis of LBD cannot be

made if Parkinson’s symptoms developed more than 1 year before the onset

of dementia. More recent attempts for a consensus criterion argued for a dif-

ferential diagnosis exclusively based on which symptoms developed earlier

(no 1 year lag for cognition) (Aarsland, Ballard, & Halliday, 2004). Many

contemporary authors argue that LBD and PDD should not be distinguished

at all, because they both share the same major neuropathological process

(development of Lewy bodies), whereby the main difference is where in the

brain the accumulation of the Lewy bodies starts.

Previous studies that tried to distinguish between LBD and PDD patients

based on neuropsychological tests were unable to find differences. For exam-

ple, Noe et al. (2004) compared 16 LBD, 15 PDD, and 16 AD patients. PDD

and LBD patients differed in retrospective analysis of their presenting symp-

toms at initial diagnosis. However, this difference faded over the course of

the disease. PDD and LBD patients could be distinguished from AD patients

through neuropsychological tests; however, the authors were unable to find

any differences in cognitive tasks concerning attention, visuoperception,

visuoconstruction, and memory between LBD and PDD patients. Troster

(2008) reviewed whether it is possible to distinguish between different

dementias through neuropsychological testing. He found that the differential

diagnosis for AD can be based on a specific neuropsychological profile;

however, there are no specific tests or combination of tests that distinguish

between LBD and PDD. In a review on mild cognitive impairment in PD

patients, Aarsland (2016) also denied the possibility to distinguish only by

cognitive testing between these two clinical entities.

Nevertheless there are some trajectories and clinical dissimilarities

between PD and LBD that makes a difference. On a cross-sectional level

LBD patients often deteriorate massively taking neuroleptic drugs, whereas

in PD patients such drugs are better tolerated (e.g., lead to smaller declines

in motor functions). Patients with LBD often show accompanying AD

3.4.2 Attention testing and cognitive fluctuations in Lewy body disease (and Parkinson’s disease with dementia)

Fluctuations of cognitive performance and alertness are one of the cardinal

symptoms in LBD, whereas AD patients do not suffer from cognitive fluc-

tuations. This clinical difference can not only be used to distinguish between

the patient groups but also for predicting which patient with MCI will

develop either AD or LBD (Sadiq et al., 2017; Scharre et al., 2016). There

are at least four different questionnaires that can be used to assess fluctuating

cognition. Further all four allow for a differentiation between LBD and AD

(for a review see O’Dowd et al., 2019). These questionnaires are not at all

time consuming. For example, the short version of Mayo Fluctuations Scale

(Ferman et al., 2004) only asks the following:

1. If the patient is drowsy and lethargic during the day despite getting

enough sleep the night before?

2. If the participant sleeps two or more hours during the day?

3. If there are times when the participant’s flow of ideas is disorganized,

unclear, or illogical?

4. If the participant tends to stare into space for long periods of time?

Palpably, two concepts of fluctuation are distinguished here—those con-

cerning parts of the day (first two questions of the fluctuations scale) and those

The alphasynucleinopathies: Parkinson’s disease, Lewy body Chapter | 3 161

concerning short periods during answering a question and thinking about a

problem (second two questions of the scale). These two major aspects of fluc-

tuating cognition may not necessarily covary in each patient and can even be

caused by different neurophysiological processes. In a study conducted by

Bliwise, Scullin, and Trotti (2014), LBD patients did not differ from PD

patients in a polysomnographically based Maintenance of Wakefulness Test.

However, LBD patients were severely impaired in their digit span performance,

which was assessed several times of the course of 2 days. More importantly

they showed higher variations in their digit span performance. Consequently

daytime based macrofluctuations in alertness and cognition in the different exe-

cution of the digit span displayed by the results may be more characteristic for

LBD than microfluctuations of sleepiness and drowsiness assessed by the

Maintenance of Wakefulness test. Bradshaw, Saling, Hopwood, Anderson, and

Brodtmann (2004) found, by means of the Clinician’s Assessment of

Fluctuation Scale, that LBD patients’ fluctuations tended to be lapses in aware-

ness and attention (i.e., going blank), which were more short-lived, occurred

spontaneously and were unrelated to situational demands. In contrast fluctua-

tions in AD were primarily memory-related deficits.

On the other hand macrofluctuations and microfluctuations play a role in

LBD. The validation study of the Mayo Fluctuation Scale showed that leav-

ing one of these two items out decreased its discriminatory power.

Electrophysiology, often used in clinical routine assessment, might, therefore

be helpful to differentiate LBD from other neurodegenerative diseases,

because changes in the electroencephalogram (EEG) during sleepiness and

drowsiness are present in healthy subjects and are indicated by a slowing of

the rhythmic pattern. O’Dowd et al. (2019) reviewed possible causes of fluc-

tuating cognition and how to measure them. In the background of their

review, they argued that LBD patients document a slowing in the EEG espe-

cially below the postcentral and not so much below frontal electrodes.

However, the postcentral shift toward slower frequencies became visible

only after a statistical quantification and not by clinical inspection of the raw

data. Moreover it is not clear if the slowing correlates with microfluctuations

of low arousal or with macrofluctuations concerning hours of the day or cir-

cadian rhythms. Cromarty et al. (2016) argued that LBD patients typically

exhibit increased posterior slow-wave activity relative to healthy controls

and individuals with AD. Furthermore according to Cromarty and colleagues,

this increase in posterior slow-wave activity correlates with the presence and

severity of cognitive fluctuations. Unfortunately this postulated difference is

based on an indirect comparison of the result of LBD patients and healthy

controls and AD patients and healthy controls. The studies that directly com-

pared LBD and AD patients yielded inconclusive results.

Another possibility to study cognitive fluctuations is through neuropsy-

chological test results either by repeating the test during the day and looking

for differing results or by analyzing the variability between the different

3.4.4 Treatment of Lewy body disease (and Parkinson’s disease with dementia)

A recent systematic review by Morrin, Fang, Servant, Aarsland, and

Rajkumar (2018) on the efficacy of nonpharmacological interventions in

LBD revealed that there are only a few studies regarding nonpharmacologi-

cal interventions in LBD. Further none of these studies had an evidence-

based medicine level higher than three, which means that there are no con-

trolled studies on this topic at all. The majority of these studies used brain

stimulation (deep brain stimulation, transcranial direct current stimulation,

etc.). Only one study focused on cognitive impairment and followed a single

case design, therefore offering limited evidence. The results of this study dis-

played a positive result for the cognitive intervention. However, on the back-

ground of this review, it is impossible to argue whether a nonpharmacological

treatment of LBD is possible or not.

There are no definite guidelines for a pharmacological treatment of LBD.

The tendency to develop visual hallucinations restricts the possibility of a

dopaminergic treatment. On the other hand, LBD patients are hypersensitive

to neuroleptic drugs, which might be helpful to reduce the hallucinations.

The acetylcholine2 esterase inhibitors may be beneficial in dampening the

tendency to hallucinate, however, this is not always the case. The German

guideline for dementia argues that memantine may reduce the neuropsychiat-

ric symptoms in LBD patients. It should also be noted that the efficacy

of acetylcholine2 esterase inhibitors was shown for demented PD patients

but not for patients with the clinical diagnosis of LBD. Thus the usage of

these drugs in LBD might not routinely covered by the health system

reimbursement.

3.4.6 Summary

Patients with LBD suffer from attentional deficits including short- and

long-term attentional fluctuations. If LBD is suspected, relatives and the

patient should be examined for such deficits (e.g., by using the Mayo

Fluctuation Scale) and through alertness and vigilance tests (as long as the

patients tolerate such investigations). Cognitive fluctuations and visuoper-

ceptive disorders are predictors for the existence of visual hallucination,

which is a prominent sign of LBD. The screening test of the VOSP, the

incomplete letters test of the VOSP, and the number location test of the

VOSP can be used to explore the existence of visuoperceptive impair-

ments. The screening test of the VOSP assesses the tendency to detect par-

eidolias due to a local attention bias, the incomplete letters test concerns

the ventral visual pathway and the numbers location test the dorsal visual

pathway. If the patient is able to, a more strenuous copying task should be

included as part of the assessment as opposed to the items of visuocon-

struction of the CERAD-NAB. The exploration for visual hallucination

should also include enquiries regarding REM sleep behavioral disorder and

concerning dream-like states during the night and illusion of movement or

objects in darkened room or in the evening. If visual hallucinations are

present, one should exclude low-level visual deficits (retinopathy), which

may also lead to visual hallucination unrelated to the diagnosis of LBD.

There is a risk that patients with LBD might decompensate in response to

being treated with neuroleptics. Thus acetylcholinesterase inhibitors might be

implemented first to suppress visual hallucinations, and only afterwards low

3.5 Multisystem atrophy

3.5.1 Clinical symptoms and neuropathological findings

The hallmark of multisystem atrophy or MSA is a dysfunction of the

autonomous nervous system accompanied by cerebellar symptoms or

Parkinsonism. Early signs of MSA concern genitourinary dysfunctions

and orthostatic dysfunction, whereas the motor problems develop later in

the disease course. Further MSA progresses quickly. Table 3.4 shows the

criteria for diagnosing MSA as possible, probable, or definite based on

the patient’s clinical symptoms (Palma, Norcliffe-Kaufmann, &

Kaufmann, 2018). The age-related criterion helps to distinguish the dis-

ease from early onset spinocerebellar degenerative diseases and the poor

response to L-dopa differentiate between MSA and idiopathic PD

(Stankovic et al., 2019). There are two major subtypes of MSA: patients

presenting Parkinson like motor symptoms and patients with cerebellar

motor symptoms. In clinical routine these subtypes are classified as

MSA-P or MSA-C. Table 3.4 also discloses that autonomic dysfunctions

are the hallmark of the diagnosis.

Contrary to PD, in MSA the alphasynuclein protein displays abnormal

depositions mainly in astrocytes (not so much in neurons) and is concen-

trated in areas of the autonomic nervous system (peripheral and central).

Within a few years, patients develop rapidly progressing balance, speech,

and coordination abnormalities (Palma et al., 2018). The clinical diagnosis of

MSA is not very predictive of the postmortem-validated diagnosis

(Stankovic et al., 2019). A recent study comparing MSA with the results of

postmortem neuropathological examinations found that only 62% of the

patients suffered from MSA and more than one-third from another disease

(Stankovic et al., 2019). The misdiagnosed patients suffered from LBD, PSP,

and PD. An incorrect diagnosis of MSA frequently occurs during the first

visit (B40%). However, the likelihood of a correct diagnosis increases with

the number of visits—up to 92% for patients with a probable MSA and 63%

for patients with a possible MSA.

Cognitive deficits have been an exclusion criterion for the diagnosis of

MSA, however, during the last decades, it has been revealed that MSA

patients may suffer from cognitive deficits from the beginning. The same

negative statement holds true for visual hallucinations, which are crucial for

the diagnosis of LBD. More recent studies have shown that visual hallucina-

tion may also be present in MSA patients. However, cognitive impairments

and visual hallucination are uncommon in MSA patients and, if present, not

very severe.

3.6 Visuoperceptive, visuoconstructive impairments, and visual hallucinations in typical and atypical Parkinson’s syndromes

We can summarize this chapter by stating that visual hallucinations and

delusional misidentifications are a characteristic feature of LBD (and PD);

patients with LBD have severe visuoperceptive problems and tend to neglect

global configurational patterns which results in increased false positives to

small features of spatial configurations. PD patients undergo similar pro-

blems, however, to a lesser extent than LBD patients. Hence suffering from

visual hallucinations and visuoperceptive impairments correlate in these

patient groups. This suggests either a common cause for both symptoms or

causation of one symptom for the other. A combination of visuoperceptive

impairment and decline of EF could be a prominent explanation for the risk

of developing visual hallucinations in PD (Santangelo et al., 2007).

However, atypical PD patients, those suffering from PSP or CBD, can

also disclose severe impairments with respect to EFs. Yet these atypical PD

patients usually do not hallucinate. Moreover these patients have visuoper-

ceptive impairments. Fiorenzato et al. (2019) compared 35 PSP patients with

Richardson’s syndrome with 30 MSA patients and 65 PD patients.

Fiorenzato et al. (2019) followed up about one-half of these groups for a

period of 18 months and found that PSP patients were more globally

impaired in neuropsychological tests (Montreal Cognitive Assessment, word

fluency tasks, Stroop test) as well as in visuoperceptive tasks. The follow-up

showed that EFs and judgment of line orientation declined faster in PSP

patients than in PD and MSA patients, however, the impairment regarding

the incomplete letters test of the VOSP was stable. This would argue that

other visuoperceptive tests are related to disease progression in PSP patients

as well as in LBD and PD patients. This is especially true for the two latter

diseases because LBD and PD patients tend to hallucinate.

Bak, Caine, Hearn, and Hodges (2006) compared MSA, PSP, and CBD

patients with healthy controls using subtests from the VOSP. CBD/CBS

patients were impaired in all VOSP subtests. Patients with PSP were

impaired in number location, dot counting, and silhouette naming. Patients

with MSA were not impaired in any of the tests. Ideomotor apraxia, a highly

sensitive and specific symptom of CBD/CBS, can be caused by a storage dis-

order for the meaning of gestures or action. However, ideomotor apraxia can

also be a consequence of a visuoperceptive disorder. This is always the case

if the testing comprises meaningless hand positions. During this task, the

discrimination and dot counting (Calderon et al., 2001; Mori et al., 2000;

Yokoi et al., 2014). Moreover LBD patients have difficulties responding to

local features of figural configurations and not so much to the global features

(Barrett et al., 2001; Uchiyama et al., 2012; Yokoi et al., 2014). On the other

hand, organizing the different subtests according to the percentage of

impaired patients from the Bak et al. (2006) study, clarifies that the impair-

ments of PSP and CBD/CBS patients predominantly involve the dot count-

ing, the number location, and the cubes tasks from the VOSP. Thus patients

with PD or LBD seem to be impaired in visuoperceptive tasks concerning

object perception, whereas PSP and CBD/CBS patients seem to be impaired

in visuoperceptive tasks concerning object localization and matching spatial

coordinate systems of vision and action. Patients with CBD/CBS are often

impaired in sensory discrimination and show signs of optic ataxia. Optic

ataxia refers to the inability to use visual information to sufficiently guide

the hand in the direction of the objects during grasping tasks (Jellinger et al.,

2011; Mendez, 2000; Rajagopal, Bateman, & Van Stavern, 2012), that is, a

task for testing object location.

Patients with semantic dementia (SD) also show visuoperceptive impair-

ments with the core symptom of visual object agnosia. These patients have

problems identifying objects, however, they do not suffer from problems in

copying them. The explanation for visual object agnosia in these patients

may differ from that of patients suffering from classical visual agnostic.

Classical visual agnosia is most likely due to a disconnection between visual

perception and the stored meaning of objects, whereby the latter is, at least

partly, attributable to a degeneration of neuronal repertoires storing the

meaning. Furthermore patients with SD do not hallucinate during the early

and middle stage of their disease. SD is related to left anterior temporal lobe

atrophy. A study from Chan et al. (2009) that compared patients with similar

right temporal lobe atrophy with SD patients, revealed that the former patient

groups sometimes presented visual hallucination and more behavioral disor-

ders. However, one problem with this study was that the right temporal lobe

patient groups was selected based on abnormal MRI scans and the amount of

atrophy was significantly higher than in the semantic demented group.

Therefore the somewhat increased risk of visual hallucination may be caused

by more focal atrophy or due to a later stage of the disease progress.

Posterior cortical atrophy (PCA) is a visual variant of AD starting with

visual impairment and is defined by atrophy in the parietal, occipital, and

posterior temporal cortex. Patients suffering from PCA show severe visuo-

spatial impairment in a variety of tests. As a result, distinguishing PCA

patients from CBD/CBS patients is sometimes difficult, especially in the

early phase of their disease. Compared to LBD patients, the visual

impairment of PCA starts earlier in the visual processing pathway (Metzler-

Baddeley, Baddeley, Lovell, Laffan, & Jones, 2010). Visual hallucinations,

however, are rare in patients with PCA and it has, therefore, been proposed

that the existence of visual hallucinations contradicts the diagnosis of a pure

PCA (Crutch et al., 2017).

When summarizing these different patterns of impairment, it should be

clear that visual hallucination cannot be caused primarily by EFs (if that

would be the case, PSP and CBD/CBS patients should exhibit even more

hallucinations than PD and LBD patients). Additionally, visual hallucinations

cannot be caused by any combination of executive impairments and visuo-

perceptive processing deficits (in this case visual hallucination should be

present in patients with SD and with CBD/CBS). On the other hand, visuo-

perceptive deficits alone do not lead to visual hallucinations. Otherwise,

visual hallucinations should be a presenting sign of PCA. Thus the specific

pattern of visuoperceptive impairments plays a central role in instigating hal-

lucinations in patients. Further EF deficits are also a necessary, however, no

sufficient condition for developing visual hallucinations.

Structural MRI scanning revealed different dominant atrophy patterns in

PSP and CBD/CBS patients when compared to LBD patients. Bozzali et al.

(2005) used diffusion tensor imaging in 15 patients with LBD and mild

dementia. They discovered a correlation between white matter dysfunction in

the occipitotemporal lobe and VOSP “incomplete letter” performance.

Gallagher et al. (2011) counted postmortem Lewy bodies in different regions

of the brain and compared the density of Lewy body counts with the exis-

tence of visual hallucinations. According to Gallagher et al., a correlation for

the middle frontal, the middle temporal gyri, transentorhinal and the anterior

cingulate cortices is present, however no such correlation exists for the parie-

tal cortex. They argue that visual hallucination might be the result of frontal

EF dysfunctions (loss of cognitive control) and a higher order temporal lobe

based visual perceptive impairment. On the other hand, in SD atrophy is con-

centrated in the anterior temporal cortex and not in the posterior or occipito-

temporal cortex.

A possible explanation for this dissociation between LBD/PD and CBD/

CBS/PSP/SD patients is offered by the seminal paper from Mishkin and

Ungerleider (1982). Since the publication of this paper, neuropsychology

distinguishes between two visual pathways as well as between different

levels of abstraction from the retinal input as result of cortical processing.

One pathway starts at the primary visual cortex and travels dorsally in the

direction of the somatosensory cortex, the other also starts at the primary

visual cortex, however, it projects toward the inferior and anterior temporal

cortex. The first pathway is related to visuospatial processes involved not

only in reaching, grasping, visual localizing, but also to copying and exe-

cuting affine spatial transformations like rotation, scaling, and so forth. The

other pathway is related to extracting object relevant features like specific

spatial configurations, color, and so forth. In addition, there is some lateral-

ity specialization for these pathways: the left hemisphere is specialized to

adapt to body movement and positions to objects (egocentric processing),

the right hemisphere adapts to target patterns (allocentric processing)

(Table 3.8).

In LBD/PD the ventral pathway (vision for object identification) seems to

be more impaired than the dorsal pathway. Consequently, it can be stated,

that visual hallucinations rely on a combination of disorders in the ventral

visual pathway and in EFs. A combination of abnormal processing in the

dorsal pathway with executive impairments does not cause visual hallucina-

tions, as shown by PSP and CBD/CBS. Moreover the ventral processing

impairment must be located in early stages of this pathway where the input

is not already abstracted from its visual features. Otherwise, SD patients

would also hallucinate.

What does that mean for clinical routine and neuropsychological differen-

tial diagnosing?

1. Atypical Parkinson’s syndromes like PSP and CBD/CBS may suffer from

visuoperceptive disorders, especially in visuoconstructive and visuospa-

tial tests, and not so much in visuoperceptive tasks assessing vision for

object recognition. This combination would argue for an impairment in

the dorsal visual stream, that is, in the “where” path of visual analysis.

For LBD and PD, the opposite pattern is the case, that is, an impairment

of the ventral pathway. Neuropsychological testing should therefore

involve visuoperceptive tests for both visual pathways if the diagnosis is

unclear. These additional tests should be able to dissociate performance

between these two pathways.

2. Typical PD patients usually do not suffer from low-level dorsal stream

impairments like optic ataxia or ideomotor apraxia. Therefore any prob-

lem in this domain should always be a red flag for the diagnosis of an

alphasynucleinopathic disease.

3. Neurodegenerative patients suffering from visual object agnosia without

clear visuoconstructive impairments often suffer from SD, but not from

LBD/PD or CBD/CBS/PD.
