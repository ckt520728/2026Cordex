Atypical Parkinson’s diseases: progressive supranuclear palsy and corticobasal degeneration

4.1 General summary

Characteristic signs of progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP) are apathy and

disinhibition (apart from vertical gaze paralysis and axial rigor). Apathy

concerns spontaneous behavior, whereas disinhibition concerns the

inability to finish repetitive behavior in relation to external or internal

signs. PSP patients are not able to stop movement sequences at an arbitrary

time point (applause sign) or to a milder degree to refrain from copying a

movement due to contextual cues. They often show subtle dysarthria and

slowing of speech. Neuropsychological examination should encompass

word fluency, cognitive flexibility, disinhibition, motor probes for apraxia,

and bradyphrenia.

Characteristic signs of corticobasal degeneration (CBD) are akinesia, dys-

tonia, and apraxia. Neuropsychological assessment should encompass tests

for ideomotor and limb kinetic apraxia, bimanual control with and without

visual control (alien limb and dystonia), dorsal visual processing functions

(judgment of the relative position of objects or task such as line orientation),

and for mental calculation and speech. Alien limb sign can be best assessed

by having the patients focus on the movement of one hand and observing the

movement done with the other hand. In such a situation the “alien” hand

starts to intervene with the actions of the not-alien hand. Dorsal visuopercep-

tive impairments (concerning spatial discrimination) with or without mild

impairments of ventral visuoperceptive functions (concerning complex fea-

ture perception) are also characteristic signs of CBD.

There is no definite pharmacological treatment for PSP or CBD; how-

ever, high dosages of L-dopa may be helpful at least to somewhat improve

motor functioning. There is also no definite functional treatment option;

however, physiotherapy might be helpful to reduce progression of motor

symptoms. There are no controlled studies on the effect of visuoperceptive

or cognitive trainings for PSP, CBD, or corticobasal syndrome (CBS)

4.2 Progressive supranuclear palsy

4.2.1 Introduction, definition, and epidemiology

Two of the four atypical Parkinson’s diseases (PD) are caused by neuropath-

ological processes that differ from those responsible for idiopathic PD. These

are the PSP and the CBD. In contrast to PD, tauopathy is the leading neuro-

pathological feature of PSP and CBD. In this section we will first discuss

PSP and later CBD/CBS. The diagnostic criteria for the clinical diagnosis of

PSP (Litvan et al., 1996) is that it is a progressive disease and patients must

show at least two of the following five cardinal signs: axial dystonia and

rigidity; bradykinesia and rigidity, unstable gait, and postural instability with

the tendency to fall (backward); pseudobulbar paralysis (dysarthria, dyspha-

gia); downward supranuclear vertical gaze paresis, frontal lobe symptoms

(bradyphrenia, perseverations, grasping reflex, utilization behavior, deceler-

ated information processing, disrupted executive motor skills); no or almost

no response to L-dopa. Table 4.1 illustrates an early diagnostical schema for

possible, probable, and verified PSP.

PSP equally affects males and females. In addition, there are no racial,

geographical, or occupational predilection (Litvan et al., 1996) with respect

to PSP. Approximately six people per 100,000 population suffer from PSP.

The onset occurs in the middle to old age, with the earliest age of onset

being 40 years. The average age of symptom onset is 63 years and survival

from onset averages 7 years, whereby the survival rate is susceptible to a

wide variance. Today, two main variants of PSP can be distinguished:

PSPRichardson’s syndrome (vertical gaze paresis, falls, cognitive decline,

PSP-R) and PSP-Parkinsonism (PSP-P). There are at least two additional

rare variants of PSP: PSPpure akinesia with gait freezing (PSP-PAGF) and

functional domains: ocular motor, postural, akinesia, and cognition

4.2.2.1 Gait and postural instability in progressive supranuclear palsy

A neuropsychological examination of the PSP (and CBD, discussed later)

patients should also involve tests of higher- and lower-order motor functions.

Table 4.3 depicts the motor deficits that are specific for PSP according to the

new consensus paper on PSP by Hoglinger et al. (2017).

To test postural instability, the patient is asked to move to the center of

the room and to stand still. The examiner stands directly in front of the

patients and informs the patient that he or she will pull him and that patient

is allowed to take one step to avoid falling. It is imperative that the examiner

stands directly in front of the patient in case the patient does fall. The exam-

iner, subsequently, gives the patient a short pull at the shoulders to assess

postural stability. Afterwards the examiner stands behind the patient and

repeats this procedure, pulling the patient backward.

Patients with PSP suffer from falling backward and show severe

impairment in balance when unable to rely on visual information. Using

computerized posturography analysis, Ondo et al. (2000) compared patients

with PD and PSP patients very early in their disease course. When the PSP

patient relied on valid visual information, no or only little significant differ-

ences were present between the two patient groups. However, during any

condition with incongruent visual information and platform sway, balance

performance of the PSP patients dropped dramatically. Therefore the clinical

assessment of balance performance should always be conducted with closed

eyes as this is helpful for differential diagnosis. PSP patients are expected to

have considerable problems keeping their body upright if pulled backward

whilst their eyes closed.

Tightrope walking, or tandem gait, presupposes a higher degree of bal-

ance performance than free moving. Therefore patients with PSP show

Eye movements can be inspected by asking the patient to follow the move-

ment of an object with his eyes. Many patients will attempt to use head move-

ments instead of eye movements. Therefore one should “fix” their heads by

holding their forehead in place with the hand not involved in moving the

object. Testing should include six different positions (upper left, lower left,

upper center, lower center, upper right, lower right). For PSP, the main ques-

tion is whether the patient is able to follow the object vertically. Whether the

impairment in performing downward movements is more sensitive than upward

movement is still somewhat unclear, however, recent results argue that the

direction of the movement is of minimal importance. On the other hand, the

velocity of the movement plays a decisive role: PSP patients will be unable to

follow quickly moving objects in the vertical direction but may be almost per-

fect when following slow moving objects. Vertical gaze paresis is an important,

albeit unnecessary symptom of PSP. In the early stage PSP vertical saccades

may be impaired, however, following a slow vertical movement is still normal.

There are several other aspects concerning the eyes and their movements

that are sometimes (not always) impaired in PSP patients. When looking

straight ahead, patients may not be able to hold fixation. Furthermore

patients show disinhibited saccades to irrelevant objects or square jerk

waves, that is, small horizontal eye movements toward left or right which

are corrected directly afterward. This impairment may be due to a neurode-

generation in the area pretectalis containing the superior colliculi, which are

involved in the inhibition and generation of saccades. Therefore the examina-

tion of the eye movements should involve a short period of looking ahead.

Examination of eye movements may also involve a test of saccades. PSP

patients show hypometric horizontal (and vertical) saccades (Vidailhet et al.,

1994) and saccadic disinhibition. As in the applause task (discussed later),

they are impaired in the antisaccade task, executing more incorrect prosac-

cades compared to PD and multiple system atrophy (MSA) patients

(Vidailhet et al., 1994; Zhang et al., 2016).

Moreover many of the PSP patients (even more than PD patients) exhibit

the “eye-wide opened sign.” The eye-wide opened sign is rather self-

explanatory, the eyes are opened wide and eye blinks cannot be observed for

an extended period. From a clinical point of view the eye-wide opened sign

is often the first observations stemming from the patients that leads to the

suspicion of a (atypical) PD and therefore structures how to assess the

patients with cognitive and motor tasks.

4.2.3.1 Tests and test results for bimanual coordination, apraxia, and disinhibition

Testing frontal cognitive/behavioral deficits in PSP should encompass a short

investigation of higher-order upper extremity motor functions (Hoglinger

et al., 2017; Lange et al., 2003). The following tests should be used: Lurija’s

in- and out-of-phase, Lurija’s fist-edge-palm (FEP) test, applause sign task,

and standard assessment of rigidity and ataxia. As higher-order motor tasks

are not always described in neuropsychological books on assessments, the

following paragraphs provide a short description of the tests.

During Lurija’s in- and out-of-phase test, the patient is asked to open and

close both hands either in a parallel or in an alternating phase. The move-

ments must be executed simultaneously, not sequentially. The task has two

different conditions; during the first condition the patient is able to see both

hands, during the second condition the patients only sees one hand, whereas

the other hand is located behind the patient. During the alternating phase, the

second condition poses a conflict between the visual impression of the hand

movement in front of the patients and the hand behind the patient.

Lurija’s FEP test is a motor task in which patients are required to copy

and learn the sequences of hand positions. There are different versions, how-

ever, from a neuropsychological standpoint, the most important one can be

described as follows: the first position is a fist with the fingers oriented

downward, the second position is an edge (the fingers are curved 90 degrees

toward the palm), and the third position is a palm position (the palm is ori-

ented downward).

Lurija’s in- and out-of-phase test and FEP test are not only test for

bimanual control and motor learning, but also provide a first indication

of the presence of apraxia and will be discussed in more detail in the section

The applause sign can also be assessed in different ways. For the most

neuropsychologically relevant assessment, the patient is asked to clap the

hands for several trials rhythmically three times. Then, the patient is asked to

clap with their hands on their thighs, once with the left hand and twice with

the right hand, for several trials (three times left 1, right 2 clap and then

three times vice versa task). Finally the sequence is reversed, twice left and

once right.

The results of these two motor tasks help to distinguish between PSP,

healthy controls, and patients with another neurodegenerative dementia. A

positive applause sign, that is, clapping more than three times or clapping

The assessment of upper extremity functions in PSP patients is not really

an examination of motor functions per se—it is more an assessment of disin-

hibition. PSP and Huntington’s disease were the two major diseases that

were deemed as subcortical dementia approximately 30 years ago. Their

major symptoms, in neuropsychological terms, were cognitive slowing, disin-

hibition, and apathy. Although the differentiation between cortical and sub-

cortical dementia vanished from the academic discussion during the last

decades, from a neurophysiological standpoint there are two possible expla-

nations for this combination of deficits: either the brainstem degeneration in

PSP results in deafferentation of noradrenergic fibers from the locus coeru-

leus or the loss of postsynaptic dopamine uptake in PSP impairs striatofrontal

interaction. A loss of noradrenergic fibers may result in decreased attention,

cognitive slowing, and problems in shifting attention. Patients with PSP are

relatively slowed compared to PD patients (Dubois, Pillon, Legault, Agid, &

Lhermitte, 1988), they show smaller P300 amplitudes in visual odd-ball para-

digms (Wang et al., 2000) and have problems in re-engaging attention after

task-shifting (Partiot et al., 1996). This might argue for a midbrain lesion

and consequently for a disruption of the brainstem’s ascending noradrenergic

fiber, which causes cognitive slowing. However, slowing is only one aspect

of cognitive impairment in PSP patients. The other major impairment is dis-

inhibition as indicated by the applause sign, instable fixation, increased num-

ber of prosaccades, increased number of errors, and perseverations in the

WCST. PSP patients suffer from bilateral premotor and mesial frontal atro-

phy (discussed later) and this atrophy may be the cause for increased disinhi-

bition. Disinhibition is also a major symptom of Huntington’s disease, a

disease without relevant lesions in the brainstem reticular activation system.

On the other hand noradrenergic input to the prefrontal cortex increases inhi-

bition, focused attention, and working memory performance (Ramos &

Arnsten, 2007). These belong to the core functions in which PSP patients

exhibit deficits. Moreover frontal atrophy seems to remain relatively

stable over time, whereas midbrain and frontal white matter tract atrophy

increases with disease progression and correlate with behavioral disorders in

PSP (Agosta et al., 2018). Thus the neuroanatomical cause of disinhibition

and slowing in PSP remains undetermined. Unfortunately all PSP patients do

not really respond to noradrenergic stimulation (Rajput & Rajput, 2001).

Thus a simple compensation of the loss of noradrenergic cells in the locus

coeruleus by taking drugs is not possible in PSP.

4.2.3.2 Neuroradiological signs for progressive supranuclear palsy

Although PSP is accompanied by atrophy in the lateral frontal cortex and in

the other areas of the brain (Agosta et al., 2018), the most important neurora-

diological sign is midbrain atrophy. There are several distinctive pareidolias

that may help to detect midbrain atrophy (e.g., the Mickey Mouse sign and

the humming-bird sign).

Fatterpekar et al. (2015) analyzed the possibility of diagnosing PSP

patients by using the width of the cerebral peduncle angle (Fig. 4.2). This

angle will widen with increasing midbrain atrophy. They showed that with a

threshold of 56 degrees the sensitivity was 86.7% and the specificity was

64% for the diagnosis of PSP (Fig. 4.3). As the measurement of the cerebral

peduncle can be easily conducted with almost all MRI presentation software

tools, it may be helpful to take this under consideration. On the other hand,

the specificity of this sign is low arguing that many non-PSP patients will

show cerebral peduncle angle enlargement. This is possibly due to increasing

age, which also has an effect on the cut-off score.

4.3.2 Motor impairment and apraxia in corticobasal degeneration or corticobasal syndrome

One important neuropsychological difference between PSP and CBD/CBS

patients is that the latter may suffer from apraxia. The presence of apraxia is

vital because apraxia is not a presenting sign in other neurodegenerative dis-

eases. Apraxia is traditionally subdivided in three subtypes: ideational

apraxia, ideomotor apraxia, and limb kinetic apraxia. CBD/CBS apraxia con-

cerns the latter two subtypes. Limb kinetic apraxia denotes a disturbance in

the melody of movement, mainly of hand movements. This corresponds with

dystonia, which is a major motor disorder of CBD/CBS. Ideomotor apraxia

refers to a disability to copy a movement or to perform a movement on com-

mand without having the related tool available. Ideomotor apraxia can also

refer to the inability to show expressive behavior on command. The main

symptom of ideomotor apraxia is the parapraxia, which is a distortion of the

movement elements. Parapraxia is caused by perseverations or by incorrect

imitation based on the direct visual perspective of the hand position and not

on the perspective of the person producing the hand position.

Leiguarda, Lees, Merello, Starkstein, and Marsden (1994) studied the fre-

4.3.3 Disorders in eye movements and visuoperception in corticobasal degeneration or corticobasal syndrome

Visual problems that concern eye movements and visuoperceptive perfor-

mance are common in CBD/CBS patients. Reflexive and voluntary saccade

latencies are typically prolonged in CBD/CBS patients and saccades are

hypometric (in any direction). Initiation of horizontal saccades can be

delayed, and some patients are unable to produce uncued voluntary horizon-

tal saccades, although this improves when they are given something to look

at. Eye blinking and head movements may be spontaneously used by the

patients to initiate voluntary saccades that are not directed toward objects but

only indicated by instructing for the directions. Typically the latency of sac-

cades, and not their velocity, is impaired. In contrast to CBD/CBS, PSP

patients exhibit a vertical saccade impairment and saccadic velocity (not

latency) is impaired. All these eye movement problems of CBD/CBS patients

are aspects of the akinetic-rigid syndrome, which develops during disease

course. The presence of square wave jerks is a uniform feature of CBD/CBS

(although PSP patients sometimes suffer from square wave jerks) and more

errors are found on an antisaccade task.

4.3.6 Neuroradiological signs for corticobasal degeneration or corticobasal syndrome

There are no definite MRI signs that are specific for CBD/CBS. Using the

MRI to endorse the clinical diagnosis of CBD/CBS would mean focusing on

atrophy patterns concerning various areas of the brain. A few studies com-

pared atrophy in CBD/CBS with atrophy in AD patients. CBD/CBS patients

suffer from diffuse cortical atrophy compared to healthy controls and from

specific atrophy in the mesial parietal and frontal cortex compared to AD

patients (Fig. 4.5) (Kitagaki, Hirono, Ishii, & Mori, 2000). These findings

are in accordance with our knowledge about lesions leading to the alien hand

sign. Therefore one should look for atrophy around the parietal and frontal

interhemispheric sulcus. Moreover most CBD/CBS patients show asymmet-

ric impairments for left and right hemisphere functions. Thus the MRI should

show enhanced atrophy on one side of the brain. Koyama et al. (2007) dis-

covered that in 81% of the CBD/CBS patients there is asymmetric cortical

atrophy in the parietal and frontal white matter structures. This coincides

with the findings that CBD/CBS patients suffer from ideomotor apraxia,

visuoperceptive deficits, and calculation impairments.

Kouri et al. (2011) focused on subtypes of CBD/CBS atrophy and com-

pared atrophy patterns in these clinical variants of CBD/CBS. They showed

that atrophy in the supplementary and presupplementary motor cortex and in

the dorsal cingulate is common to all different subtypes (Fig. 4.6). Left later-

alized lateral frontal and parietal atrophy is common in the classical variant

of CBD/CBS, whereas the more visuoperceptive variant shows atrophy the

left and right parietal and occipital cortex. In addition, such a pattern of atro-

phy corresponds to the distinctive symptoms of CBD/CBS: alien hand sign,

apraxia, and impairment of dorsal visual perception pathways.

4.4.2 Some remarks on pathogenesis of progressive supranuclear palsy and corticobasal degeneration

PSP and CBD are both linked to a dysfunction of the microtubule-associated

protein tau, which is required for microtubule assembly and stability

(Fig. 4.7) and is densely expressed in neuronal axons. Microtubules are pair-

wise structures composed of tubulin polymers. They form part of the cyto-

skeleton and provide structure and shape to neurons. They are rolled in like

a spiral spring. The tau protein acts as molecular glue, gluing paired poly-

mers of tubulin together. The phosphorylation of tau regulates its function.

In tauopathies, tau protein is aberrantly phosphorylated and has an abnormal

conformation that favors aggregation and inclusion formation. Fig. 4.8 illus-

trates the role of microtubules for the stability of the axons and the disinte-

gration of the axon and later of the neuron as a consequence of its

hyperphosphorylation.

Please note that in Fig. 4.9 the different colors or shadings of the micro-

tubule depict the pairwise structure of the microtubules formed by the poly-

merization of a dimer of two globular proteins, alpha, and beta tubulin, into

protofilaments. Tau stabilizes the pairwise structure by keeping the elements

of the pairs together.

4.5 Treatment

Dopaminergic drugs show only small or even absent effects for a remedia-

tion of the Parkinsonism-like symptoms of PSP and CBD (Rajput & Rajput,

2001). Therefore treatment with L-dopa must start with high doses from the

beginning and will quickly lose efficacy. Despite initial optimism, acetylcho-

line esterase inhibitors do not have enduring disease modifying effects

(Warren, Piggott, Perry, & Burn, 2005). Furthermore there is no evidence-

based treatment of dystonia in CBD.

The effectiveness of physiotherapeutic treatment in PSP patients was

recently reviewed (Slade, Finkelstein, McGinley, & Morris, 2019). Only a

few studies used a control group design, which is understandable given the

small number of patients suffering from PSP. The intensity of the interven-

tions varied from 10 to 40 sessions. The effect size of the interventions was

small or absent, which might be due to the relatively small number of inter-

ventions compared to the severity of the disease and its progressive charac-

ter. The review, therefore, states that it is unclear if physiotherapy is helpful

in PSP. Still, multidisciplinary rehabilitation using a or robot-assisted gait

training improved walking speed and reduced the number of falls (Intiso,

Bartolo, Santamato, & Di Rienzo, 2018).

4.7 How can disinhibition in progressive supranuclear palsy and corticobasal syndrome be explained in neuropsychological terms?

Deficits in executive functions have been proposed to be the core impairment

in various neurodegenerative diseases such as frontotemporal dementia,

Parkinson’s and atypical Parkinson’s diseases. We already discussed that

some of the findings demonstrating EF deficits might be related to cognitive

slowing and not so much to those of EF proper. Moreover the term EF is a

very broad, encompassing abilities such as updating, shifting, inhibition,

selective and sustaining attention, planning, problem solving (Müller, 2012).

In the following discussion, we suggest that if the updating function (work-

ing memory) and more complex performances such as planning are removed,

then executive functioning concerns conditional responding: mapping actions

to stimuli depending on the context they are experienced. The typical equa-

tion for conditional responding is as follows: respond to stimulus A in the

case of A and B, but do not respond or use another response in the case of A

and C. Hereby B and C denote the different context for the stimulus A. In

this case the response to A is conditionally related to the specific context of

A and behavior is based on decisions, which means it is not enough to use

simple reflex-like or automatic actions (Passingham, 1993). Major deficits of

#### EF can therefore be rephrased as deficits to take conditionals into account:

deficits in shifting as an impairment to integrate changed feedback from the

environment, deficits in inhibitions as an impairment to restrict actions to the

specific context, deficits in switching between alternatives as an impairment

in adapting actions to a predefined sequence.

In the case of PSP, disinhibition is most obvious in the variants of the

applause test: the patients cannot stop clapping their hand after having

reached the defined number of claps. What is the context here or how can

this impairment be refrained as a deficit in conditional responding? The

patient must associate his or her responses with an internal counting of the

number of claps and must stop the intention to clap after reaching the speci-

fied number. Thus the action is conditional on the results of internal count-

ing. Please note that this is different from other tasks cognitively measuring

disinhibition. For example, in the case of the Stop motor signal task, the con-

ditional is an additional external signal presented together with the Go signal

or very shortly afterward. Hence these kinds of inhibition tests measure the

238 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

ability to relate a response to two external conditionals: the Go and the

NoGo signal. The component which the applause test and the Stop signal

task share is that both test the ability to stop an action which is already

started. Their difference is that in the applause test the “conditional” is an

internal signal, whereas in the Stop signal task it stems from an external

The sustained attention task (SAT) is another measure for the ability to

inhibit the automatism to continue with the same action (as in the applause

test). In the SAT the participants watch numbers between one and nine and

must respond to all numbers with a button press except for the number nine,

which acts as a NoGo stimulus. Because the nine denotes a rare event (pre-

sented only in 11% of the trials), people tend to continue pressing the button

in these NoGo trials.

In contrast to these tasks affording a disruption of the tendency to con-

tinue responding, there are others in which specific motor responses must be

inhibited, although they seem to be the “natural” way to act. For example,

the Stroop task, during the Stroop task, the inner tendency to read must be

inhibited and instead the color of the word must be named. The

Incompatibility task producing the “Simon effect” has a similar structure: an

inner tendency to response on the side, where a stimulus (an arrow) is

appearing, has to overcome by the conditional, which is the direction of the

arrow. In this respect, these tasks concern a mixture of internal and external

conditional.

A review by Lee et al. (2012) (Table 4.23) shows that patients with PSP

suffer from deficits in external and internal conditional responding. For

example, PSP patients differ from MSA and from PD patients not only in the

WCST, based on the learning of external conditional, but also in the TMT B,

a test based on switching based on an internal sequence. However, the

impairment for using internal stop signals prevail in PSP patients: An abnor-

mal applause is not present in every PSP patient; however, it is very specific

(only a very few PD or MSA patients suffer from it).

A comparison of two of the motor tests of the FAB exemplifies that the

impairment of PSP patients to inhibit their responses due to internal and to

external conditionals, too. MSA and PD patients and PSP patients are both

impaired in the Go/NoGo task of the FAB, but this impairment is weak and

not present in all studies. On the other hand, PSP patients are often impaired

in the conflicting instruction task of the FAB (Paviour et al., 2005; Sitek,

Konkel, Dabrowska, & Slawek, 2015; Stamelou et al., 2015). This is an

interesting finding, because the two tests are physically identical: the patient

is shown either one or two claps. In the GoNoGo task the two-clap condi-

tion serves as a NoGo trial, however, in the conflicting instruction the one-

and the two-clap conditions has to be imitated with the opposite response.

The conflicting instruction task of the FAB, an internal conditional (the

observed action), must be inhibited to produce an opposite response. Thus

PSP patients are more impaired in tasks with an internal condition compared

to task with an external condition.

This difference between inhibition of action related to external and inter-

nal conditionals in PSP, however, is a quantitative one. The clinical picture

of CBD/CBS argues that internal disinhibition can go even much further. All

tests for disinhibition presented earlier presumed that execution of an action

and cognitive control are integrated into one acting agent. Evidently such a

model of disinhibition is unable to explain the alien hand sign as a core

symptom of CBD/CBS. The alien hand sign assumes that there is not one

cognitive agent in one organism, but one person experiencing the hand with-

out the feeling of ownership and without the feeling of agency. Nevertheless,

the alien hand sign can be conceptualized as a severe disorder of disinhibi-

tion: the movements of the hand are disinhibited from any conscious control

whatsoever (at least in situations where the patient is consciously focusing

on other tasks and loses track of the alien hand).

There is a mechanistic explanation as to why patients suffer from an alien

limb (Goldberg, Mayer, & Toglia, 1981; Passingham, 1996). Goldberg and

colleagues’ explanation assumes that actions can be differentiated into being

initiated either by environmental or by internal stimuli and representations.

Actions initiated by internal stimuli express changes of internal needs or

they respond to internal contextual changes (e.g., withdrawing the hand after

shaking hands). The intrinsic relation to internal goals directing and control-

ling the actions explains why the cognitive agent experiences a feeling of

agency during their execution. Internal stimuli and representations are also in

charge of controlling and inhibiting automatically executed actions triggered

by environmental stimuli if these actions do not fit the internal context. In

general, externally and internally triggered behavior must be balanced in

each action and the elimination of one component by a brain lesion induces

a disinhibition of the other component. Signs for the disinhibition of the

externally triggered actions by brain lesions impairing internal control are

“pathological grasping” (as measured in the prehension task of the FAB),

“utilization behavior,” and the full-fledged “alien limb sign.” These signs

encompass automatic activities of one limb executed toward external objects

that should be ignored according to the internal representation of the situa-

tion. Signs for a loss of external triggers to control internal ones belong to

the psychopathological field of rumination and compulsive behavior.

There are two animal experiments from Mushiake, Inase, and Tanji (1991)

and Passingham (1996), which show that internal triggers and external triggers

for actions rely on different subareas of the premotor cortex. Passingham

(1996) taught monkeys to raise their arm spontaneously to get a banana. The

execution of the arm movement was thus triggered by the internal wish for sus-

tenance and not prompted by an environmental event. After an artificial lesion

of the supplementary cortex, the monkeys still raised their hands to grasp for

food as long as they could see it. However, spontaneously raising the arm to

obtain food was no longer possible. Mushiake et al. (1991) recorded single

cells from the supplementary cortex and the lateral premotor cortex. They

found that activity in the lateral premotor was high during executing a move-

ment sequence or an action. However, supplementary cortex activity increased

when the movement was executed from memory after several minutes. In other

words: spontaneous or internally from memory triggered actions activated the

supplementary motor cortex and actions triggered by visual information acti-

vated the lateral premotor cortex. Studies in humans have revealed that lesions

in the supplementary motor or the presupplementary motor cortex and the adja-

cent cingulate cortex may lead to pathological grasping and the alien limb

symptom (Feinberg, Schindler, Flanagan, & Haber, 1992; Fukui, Hasegawa,

Sugita, & Tsukagoshi, 1993; Gasquoine, 1993; Gentilucci et al., 2000;

Goldberg et al., 1981; Hashimoto & Tanaka, 1998; Hassan & Josephs, 2016).

In his seminal book on the frontal lobe and voluntary action, Passingham

(1993) developed the functions of the frontal lobes. His main assumption

was that, starting with the premotor cortex, the frontal lobe allows for more

flexible (conditional) stimulus—response couplings. According to his view,

the internal conditionals are processed in the medial frontal lobe areas,

whereas the external conditionals are processed in the lateral premotor cor-

tex. Now have a look at Fig. 4.10, which depicts areas with prominent atro-

phy in PSP and CBD/CBS patients displayed by FDG-PET imaging (Beyer

et al., 2018). One commonly involved area is the superior medial frontal cor-

tex. In the case of PSP patients, atrophy is bilaterally focused in the anterior

cingulate cortex (Chiu et al., 2012), whereas in patients with CBD/CBS atro-

phy is focused more dorsally in the supplementary and the presupplementary

inhibition and that this aspect of executive dysfunction can be reframed as a

deficit in conditional responding. In the case of PSP, the atrophy concerns

the midbrain, the lateral frontal lobe, and the cingulate cortex. Lesion of the

lateral frontal cortex leads to disinhibition for responding to external stimuli

and lesions to the cingulate cortex to self-paced actions. On the other hand

atrophy of the supplementary cortex may lead to the alien hand sign: the

(left) hand disinhibited by the internal representation of the individual’s con-

text and needs that gets under the control of the environment triggering

unconscious actions (Hassan & Josephs, 2016).
