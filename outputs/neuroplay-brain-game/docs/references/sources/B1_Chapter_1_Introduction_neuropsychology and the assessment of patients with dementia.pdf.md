Introduction: neuropsychology and the assessment of patients with dementia

General summary

The number of patients with dementia is increasing because of increasing

life expectancy. Dementia is a clinical syndrome, not a cause of disease. Up

to now, there are no valid biomarkers for most kinds of dementia. Therefore

the differential diagnosis is based on the clinical assessment, whereby neuro-

psychology plays a major role. Neuropsychological testing has two goals: to

increase the correctness of the differential diagnosis and to predict the psy-

chosocial consequence of mild cognitive impairment and dementia (the abil-

ity to live at home and the need for external help). Hopefully soon, there

will be a third goal: to inform about the best functional treatment.

Screening for dementia is helpful for grading and following up. Raw

scores in screening tests are influenced by the background of the patients,

that is, education, gender, and age. Patients with a mini mental status exami-

nation (MMSE) score less than 21 start to develop impairments of instrumen-

tal activities of daily living (IADL). However, basic ADL only decline after

further disease progression.

Stating that the diagnosis relies on the anamnesis, which should encom-

pass cognitive deficits as well as autonomous functioning, walking, fine-

motor control, mood, and relevant diseases of the patient and their relatives.

The Consortium to Establish a Registry for Alzheimer’s Disease,

Neuropsychological Assessment Battery (CERAD-NAB) is a standardized

test battery, which is recommended as part of a core set of tests. Together

with results from other tests of the core set, a hypothesis for the differential

diagnosis should be developed. This hypothesis must be tested through tests

sensitive for the different kinds of dementia. The core test set should be used

in patients older than 60 years, in younger patients it might be too easy. For

such patients the assessment should rely on harder tests.

Effective treatment for dementia relies on early diagnosis because in later

stages the neurodegenerative process already caused irreversible atrophy.

1.1 Some epidemiological background information

The 10th International Classification of Disease (ICD-10) defines dementia

as a significant cognitive decline that lasts (or progresses) for at least

6 months. This decline might be due to an acute or a subtly increasing dam-

age to the brain and must be independent of an acute or temporary cognitive

impairment. If the cognitive impairment is rather temporary, the ICD pro-

vides the diagnosis of a delirium, defined as a short-term severe cognitive

disorder. Furthermore the cognitive decline must comprise different cogni-

tive functions. This is important because isolated cognitive disorders may be

classified under other specific headings like organic amnesia (F04 or R41.0),

neglect (R29.5), aphasia (R47.0), or attentional impairment (R46.4). Only a

combination of different cognitive disorders allows the use of the term

dementia. These impairments also must be severe enough to diminishing

social functioning at work or the ability to live independently. The last

important aspect, which plays a role in the diagnosis of dementia, is that the

cognitive decline has to be leading. In other words, it must be excluded that

the decline is due to an affective disorder like psychosis or depression. This

does not mean that patients with dementia may not also suffer from depres-

sion, it only means that the affective state is not a sufficient explanation for

the cognitive decline.

It is well known that the number of patients with dementia is increasing.

An increase in life expectancy automatically leads (more or less automatically)

to a higher number of patients with dementia (Reischies et al., 1997). In

Germany it has been estimated that around the year 2000 approximately 1.61%

of all inhabitants older than 65 years suffered from dementia. That would mean

that approximately 150,000 people developed dementia (not including patients

with vascular dementia) each year. Because vascular dementia embraces

approximately 30% of all dementias, another 45,000 cases of dementia must be

added to the yearly figure. Around the year 2000 there were about one million

people older than 65 years living with a dementia diagnosis in Germany.

Fifteen years later this number has increased to 1.6 million patients and it is

estimated that in 2050 this number will rise to three million (www.alzheimer-

europe.org/EN/Research/European-Collaboration-on-Dementia/Prevalence-of-

dementia/Prevalence-of-dementia-in-Europe). Out of these estimated figures,

about one-half of these patients will develop Alzheimer’s dementia (AD), 10%

a variant of frontotemporal lobe dementia and 10% Lewy body dementia or

Parkinson’s disease dementia. One should be aware that these estimations do

not reflect the real picture because dementia is often not additionally diagnosed

in patients where the organic cause is evident (e.g., stroke or Parkinson’s

1.2 Dementia is a syndromic and not a causal diagnosis

Dementia is not a causing diagnosis, but rather a term that refers to a cogni-

tive syndrome. The significance of the term “dementia” is often confused not

only by laymen but also by professionals who are new to the field. Dementia

means that patients possess a specific combination of cognitive impairments

and that these impairments are severe enough to have significant conse-

quences for one’s ability to work or live independently. Dementia does not

cause the cognitive deficits, it is simply a common heading for them. In

terms of causation, the loss of independence in everyday functioning is the

only thing that ensues once the label “dementia” has been assigned to a per-

son. More strictly speaking, it is not the diagnosis of dementia that implies

any causation. Instead, it is the cognitive decline that is associated with it:

Patients with mild dementia may still be able to live at home, sometimes

with some help even alone, whereas patients with moderate or severe demen-

tia will not be able to do so (or will need considerable nursing assistance).

To estimate the already present (but ignored) or the soon to be present psy-

chosocial consequences based on the severity of the cognitive impairment is

an important task of neuropsychological assessment. At the same time, this

task differs, in many respects, from the task of using neuropsychological test

results for a differential diagnosis, that is, determining the kind of dementia

the patient is developing. Therefore there are two aspects of the meaning of

the term dementia. The first meaning is syndromic and concerns a specific

combination of cognitive deficits that can be used to differentiate between

different possible causes of the state of dementia. The other meaning con-

cerns the severity of the cognitive decline and how the degree of severity

relates to the ability of the patient to live independently. Both aims of neuro-

psychological testing should not be confused as they partly require the use of

different assessment measures.

In the following section we will mainly focus on the first meaning, which

concerns the cognitive pattern of deficits. The theoretical assumption is that

the cognitive impairment pattern is related to the specific organic disease.

Neuropsychological assessment is, in this respect, very similar to neuroradio-

logical or neurophysiological assessment. It measures the results of a

When it comes to finding causes for diseases in the clinical routine prac-

tice, the ultimate goal of the diagnostic process does not end with the identi-

fication of a lesion (e.g., a thalamic lesion or widespread white matter

lesions corresponding to grade 3 on the Fazekas scale). Rather the real task

is to find the causes that contribute to the development of these kinds of

lesions (high blood pressure, diabetes, embolic vs stenosis as cause for a

stroke, etc.). Only at this level can the pathogenetic process be stopped and

prevented. The same holds true for neurodegenerative diseases such as

Alzheimer’s dementia or frontotemporal lobe dementia. The goal is to find a

specific neuropathological process that causes the cognitive decline, not to

identify a specific brain structure that is related to the cognitive process.

example, there is the typical variant of AD, in which patients suffer from

severe memory impairment that dominates the clinical picture. However,

there is also the atypical logopenic variant, which begins with language dis-

orders, and the postcentral cortical atrophy variant of AD, which begins

visual perceptive deficits. These three variants show different symptoms,

which are caused by the same pathological process, that is, Alzheimer’s dis-

1.3.1 Summary

The prevalence and incidence of patients with dementia in the population

is increasing because of the increasing life expectancies. Dementia is a

clinical syndrome not a cause of disease. Neuropsychological testing in

dementia has two goals: to increase the correctness of the differential

diagnosis concerning the neuropathological cause and to help predict the

psychosocial consequence of mild cognitive impairment and dementia

(the ability to live at home and the need for external help). The first goal

is based on the assumption that a specific neuropathological cause will

also have a specific functional consequence. In reality, however, this

assumption will not always be valid since some kinds of dementia have

specific functional consequences and others have unspecific or varying

impairments. Neuropsychology can only be of help for the first group of

diseases by providing a possible diagnosis. However, for both groups

neuropsychological assessment can help to measure the handicaps and to

estimate whether the patients are still able to live independently. Up to

now, there are no valid biomarkers for most kinds of dementia. Therefore

the differential diagnosis is based on the clinical assessment, whereby

neuropsychology plays a major role.

1.4.1 Screening instruments: the mini mental status examination

Screening for dementia is an important aspect of neuropsychological assess-

ment. The German S3 Guideline for Dementia (https://www.awmf.org/

uploads/tx\_szleitlinien/038-013l\_S3-Demenzen-2016-07.pdf) states that a

approximate degree of cognitive decline and therefore the presence of a

dementia syndrome. However, the guideline also states that the sensitivity of

these screening tests is limited in cases of mild or doubtful dementia.

to deciding when to look for a nursery home and to normalize possible feel-

that influenced whether the patient lived at home or in a sheltered

1.4.3 Other screening instruments

Another screening test, which is often used in Germany, is the DemTect

(Kalbe et al., 2004). The DemTect is composed of an auditory wordlist learn-

ing task (10 words, 2 learning trials and 1 delayed recall trial), a flexibility

test where the patients have to write down or spell numbers, a word fluency

task (articles which can be bought at a supermarket) and a backwards digit

span task. The DemTect has some similarities with the MMSE as it assesses

different cognitive functions and it has been argued that the DemTect is

much more sensitive in detecting patients with mild cognitive impairment

than the MMSE. On the other hand, the DemTect has no gender- or

education-specific norms for the verbal learning task. Because there is a

large gender and education bias in wordlist learning performance (compare,

e.g., the standardization of the California Verbal Learning Task) this is a genu-

ine problem when relying on the results of the DemTect. There are also only

two age ranges (younger and older than 60 years), but many of the patients

screened with the DemTect are older than 70 years. Additionally, two learning

trials are not enough to assess learning ability because the patient may be dis-

tracted during the first trial. Last, but not least, because recognition perfor-

mance is very important in memory testing and the DemTect has no

recognition trial it has, in our view, a limited role in the assessment

of dementia.

The clock drawing task is another test often used in the assessment of

patients with dementia not only in Germany but also all over the world. The

CDT is also part of the MoCA (Nasreddine et al., 2005), which has gained

considerable attention for assessing cognitive impairment during the last

decade. In the clock drawing task, the patients are asked to draw a circle and

then to draw two hands of a clock that show the time of 10 past 10.

Table 1.3 (Riedel et al., 2008) compares the MMSE with the CDT in a group

of Parkinson’s disease patients and shows that the CDT has a strong gender

and education bias. Moreover, the ability to complete the clock drawing task

can be influenced if a patient suffers from a unilateral stroke (e.g., resulting

in subclinical neglect) and the CDT does not assess memory performance

(which is a major deficit in dementia). Thus the CDT is not a test that should

be used to screen for dementia, at least not as the only task. We will discuss

the MoCA in detail later comparing it with the CERAD-NAB.

1.5 Assessing patients for differential diagnosis

1.5.1 Some general remarks on the meaning of the medical history for the neuropsychological assessment

In the next section, tests for a core assessment of patients with a suspected

neurodegenerative cause for cognitive impairments will be introduced. In the

following chapters of this book, the core set will be accomplished by other

tests depending on the probable etiology and the pattern of the impairment.

The core set defined later is something like a global framework that helps to

define diagnostical predictions and the more specific tasks discussed in the

following chapters hopefully add the necessary information to provide a pos-

sible diagnosis.

Before defining the core set of tests, it should be made clear that the

validity of any neuropsychological assessment depends on what is known

about the medical and psychosocial background of the patient. The medical

background defines the appropriateness of the symptoms for a specific diag-

nosis. For example, gait disorders are common in elderly people and are

often due to peripheral polyneuropathies. On the other hand, gait disorder is

an important symptom for the diagnosis of Parkinson’s disease or of normal

pressure hydrocephalus. Moreover, patients suffering from typical

Alzheimer’s disease will not present gait disorders. Therefore establishing

the medical background of the patients is an important aspect of

neuropsychological-based differential diagnosis. If the patient suffers from

gait disorder one should ask, for example, for possible comorbidities such as

diabetes or chemotherapeutically treated cancer, both of which increase the

risk of suffering from polyneuropathy. If the patients suffer from polyneuro-

pathy the observed gait disorder does not necessarily bias the diagnosis

toward Parkinson’s disease or normal pressure hydrocephalus. It also does

not argue against a possible Alzheimer’s disease.

Another topic of the medical history are metabolic disorders, which may

explain the state of the patients. Metabolic disorders, for example, renal

insufficiency, hyponatremia, dehydration, and inflammatory processes, may

induce global cognitive impairments. Although most of the metabolic distur-

bances and diseases can easily be detected by blood probes, such probes may

be absent during the time-point of neuropsychological assessment. Most of

the metabolic-induced cognitive deficits are global and diffuse cognitive

impairments. Moreover, their “history” is short-lived and many of these

patients experience fluctuation and report a fast decline during the last days

or weeks. If the attention of the patient is impaired on many cognitive levels,

if there are any signs of unexplained global drowsiness, and the symptoms

started only a few days or weeks ago one should be very cautious with any

attempt for a differential diagnosis.

The medication the patient is taking can also influence his or her cogni-

tive state. For example, some of the drugs used to treat urinary incontinence

have an anticholinergic component, which impairs memory functioning.

Severe and focal cognitive impairments, however, are rare after such kind of

medication. Some patients suffering from chronic pain use opioid-based

drugs which may impair attention. Elderly people, sedated for any kind of

surgery, often show prolonged signs of reawaking with delirium-like states.

Although the presence of a delirium after a surgery may be a first sign of a

neurodegenerative disease, this is not always the case. Drowsiness, motor

and cognitive unrest, and global attentional impairment are cardinal signs of

a delirium. If there are any hints that the patient’s state might be explained

by an acute event, one should cease with the neuropsychological differential

diagnosis and under the assumption of global cognitive functioning improve-

ment propose another meeting with the patient in the future.

Another important topic are psychosocial influences like depressive

mood and anxiety. As long as the symptoms are limited to a mild-to-

moderate range these do not really impact cognitive performance (at least

not if the task execution is cued and supervised by the assessor). Depressive

mood may be a sign for a beginning neurodegenerative disease (compare

Chapter 3: The Alphasynucleinopathies: Parkinson’s Disease, Lewy Body

Dementia, and Multisystem Atrophy). However, if assessed accurately,

patients with only mild-to-moderate mood disorders perform well on most of

the core set of tests defined later. For a clear exception concerning divided

attention, the reader is referred to Section 1.5.6.

1.5.2 The CERAD-NAB as core assessment battery

German memory clinics and hospitals that are specialized for dementia

mainly use the CERAD-NAB (Monsch, 1997; Schmid et al., 2014) as a

neuropsychological core set battery. However, from an international perspec-

tive the CERAD-NAB is not the most widely used test battery in patients

with suspected dementia. For example, the ADNI group (a very important

initiative to improve research on Alzheimer’s disease) uses the logical mem-

ory task from the Wechsler Memory Scale and the Auditory Verbal Learning

Test. However, the advantage of the CERAD-NAB is the very extensive

German standardization due to more than 1100 control subjects all aged

50 years or older. This means that there are sufficiently large subgroups for

different combinations of age, sex, and education. Moreover, the CERAD-

NAB is easy to perform and not overly difficult for older participants suffer-

ing from cognitive deficits. The CERAD-NAB also enables verbal memory

testing in patients with hearing loss because the patients must read the words

that will be recalled aloud. This kind of testing makes sure that the patients

have processed the correct words, whereas during oral presentation there is

always a possibility that the patients misunderstand the spoken word because

of their hearing problems. Moreover, the test battery permits a qualitative

inspection of the difference between free recall and recognition (at least for

verbal information), different versions of word fluency, naming, construction,

and visuoconstructive memory standardized in the same large group of healthy

controls. This makes the CERAD-NAB a very suitable neuropsychological

testing battery for the assessment of patients with suspected cognitive

decline. As such, we will focus on the CERAD-NAB as core test set in

the following.

The CERAD-NAB is composed of the following subtests: the MMSE, a

short form of the Boston Naming Test (15 drawings of objects or animals),

semantic word fluency (for animals, one minute), wordlist learning for

10 words based on three learning trials, visuoconstruction or copying of four

geometrical figures, delayed free recall of the wordlist, recognition of the

wordlist (10 target items and 10 semantically nonrelated distractors) and

recall of the four copied figures. Possible and/or similar items of the

CERAD-NAB can be found in Fig. 1.10. Usually the test battery starts with

the semantic word fluency task, proceeds to the Boston Naming test, con-

tinues with the wordlist learning, copying of the four geometrical figures,

free recall of the wordlist, recognition of the wordlist and free recall of the

four-copied geometrical figures. Moreover, phonological word fluency

(words starting with “s” for 1 minute) and the TMT-A and TMT-B are part

of the CERAD-NAB and they are usually assessed at the end of the testing

session. Although the sequence of the subtests reported earlier is common

when assessing patients with the CERAD-NAB, the subtests except for the

memory tasks can be conducted in a different order.

z-scores a specific participant achieved during testing. However, the

CERAD-NAB also has some methodological disadvantages and problems,

which should be mentioned, before discussing whether the CERAD-NAB is

helpful for a differential diagnosis and not only for distinguishing between

1.5.3 The CERAD-NAB for differential diagnosis

Up to now, we discussed the CERAD-NAB mainly as a tool to detect cogni-

tive impairment and not as a means to classify patients according to the pos-

sible cause of this impairment. For contributing to the differential diagnosis

in single cases, it would be very helpful if there were definite cut-off scores

for individual test scores, which have a strong association with one or

another cause of the cognitive impairment. For example, patients above the

cut-off score might suffer from Alzheimer’s disease (or any other neurode-

generative disease that is of interest) and patients below that cut-off score

might suffer from dementia not caused by AD. It should be noted that

defining such a cut-off score for differential diagnosis is not the same as

arguing that a patient is impaired in, for example, memory performance.

Abnormal memory performance is, from a neuropsychological assessment

view, defined by a score 1.5 standard deviations below the gender, age,

and education corrected mean. However, such an abnormal score may

have various causes and many patients suffering from a neurodegenera-

tive disease will show impaired memory performance at some stage of

their disease. Therefore using neuropsychological tests for differential

diagnosis presupposes more specific cut-off scores that are sensitive for

the specific neurodegenerative disease which causes the functional

impairment.

Instead of focusing on single subtest cut-off scores, the neuropsychologi-

cal differential diagnosis could be based on specific impairment patterns, for

example, a set or structure of impaired and not-impaired test results. Such

patterns of deficits may allow for more fine-grained assumptions on dis-

turbed cognitive processes and may therefore correlate better with the neuro-

anatomical and neurophysiological causes of the functional impairment. If

this is the case, they, and no single test per se, may provide information

about possible diseases causing the cognitive decline. The learning tests of

the CERAD-NAB can be used, for example, to distinguish between patients

suffering from global or modality-specific (verbal and nonverbal vs only ver-

bal or only nonverbal) memory impairments, indicating a bilateral or a uni-

lateral brain lesion. A large difference between performances in verbal

compared to visuospatial memory argues for a different involvement of the

left or right hemisphere. The different parameters of the wordlist learning

task of the CERAD-NAB also allows examiners to distinguish between sub-

types of memory deficits, being related, for example, to the question of corti-

cal or frontosubcortical dementia. Cortical (AD-like) memory deficits

concern consolidation of information, whereas fronto-subcortical memory

deficits concern only free recall of information. Table 1.6 provides an over-

1.5.4 Additional tests and the assessment of behavioral disorders, depressive mood, and instrumental activities in daily living

The CERAD-NAB targets learning, memory, cognitive speed, access to the

semantic and word lexicon, and cognitive flexibility. One important cogni-

Wechsler Memory Scale as part of our core test set. We do not use the block

additional information and because it is time intensive.

1.5.5 Summary

A standard assessment of dementia and mild cognitive impairment in

patients older than 60 years should encompass the CERAD-NAB, a special

reading and writing probe, a test of verbal understanding, the digit span test,

a mood questionnaire (e.g., BDI) and questionnaires related to IADL and

behavioral disorders. For younger patients, other tests might be used (dis-

cussed later). There is no single cut-off score of these tests to distinguish

between AD patients and patients with other causes of their dementia, which

are independent of the disease progression. Therefore any differential diag-

nosis must rely on the specific patterns of subtest results and additional tests

sensitive to the different suspected disease etiology (which will be discussed

in detail in the following chapters).

1.5.6 Assessing middle-aged and/or high performing patients

One major advantage of the CERAD-NAB is the large number of controls

participants, which allows to use well-matched gender, education, and age-

corrected norms for the assessment. However, the population used to calcu-

late the z-scores is older than 50 years and the tests of the CERAD-NAB are

comparatively easy tasks, at least for younger and high performing subjects.

Therefore for this patient groups the CERAD-NAB is not very sensitive, par-

ticipants with mild impairment will have no problems to perform at ceiling

(at least for the short version of the Boston naming test, for copying, but also

for word list delayed recall and recognition). For this group of patients more

challenging tests should be used. There is no definite answer, which of the

existing memory tests might be the most sensitive and specific ones to look

for mild cognitive impairment due to neurodegeneration in middle-aged

patients. The ADNI group decided for the Auditory Verbal Learning Test

and the story recall (logical memory test) of the Wechsler Memory scale and

nothing is wrong with this decision. We use the California Verbal Memory

Test (CVLT) and story recall from the Wechsler Memory Test. Story recall

is an everyday based memory task and therefore helpful to analyze how far

the patient can understand and reproduce verbal information being told to

him or her. The CVLT assesses word list learning as the CERAD-NAB.

However, not only 10 but 16 words are presented. The CVLT has five learn-

ing trials, a short delay free and cued recall test, but also provides informa-

tion about long-term delayed free and cued recall (20 minutes delay) and the

recognition performance. The higher number of words and the delay allows

for detecting mild or even subtle impairments. Moreover, the recognition test

incorporates different foils and provokes the production of false positives.

Additionally, as a further tests of visuospatial memory, we use paired associate

learning for picture drawings at different place to assess middle-aged patients

(refer Chapter 2: Alzheimer’s Disease: Neuropathology, Neuropsychological

46 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

Differential Diagnosis and Treatment, on Alzheimer’s disease, why one should

use this task in patients with preclinical AD) and we assess the ability to remem-

ber a pathway through an imagery town map.

Although there are population norms for the TMT-A and TMT-B for

young- and middle-aged patients, the test is relatively easy for this groups of

persons. We therefore use PC-based attention testing to assess such patients.

One should look for the ability to divide attention between different sensory

sources (hearing and seeing targets) and for selective responding. We use the

Testbatterie zur Aufmerksamkeitsprüfung (TAP) (Zimmermann, 1992),

which provides subtests for divided and selective attention. In the divided

attention task, a square built up by 4 3 4 points is shown. Small crosses

cover seven or eight of the points. During the task these crosses jump irregu-

larly to the different points and sometimes four of them are grouped like a

small square (covering four adjacent points of the large square). The task is

to press the button as soon as four crosses form a such square. At the same

time participants hear high and low tones. They also must press the button as

soon as two same tones are presented directly in a sequence. The duration of

the task is between 4 and 5 minutes. The selective attention task is a variant

of the Simon paradigm: the participant must press the left or the right button

as soon as an arrow appears pointing in this direction. The arrows appear

very shortly on the left and the right side of the screen, thus sometimes pro-

ducing a conflict between the side where they are presented and the side

where they point to. Accordingly, the test probes selective visuospatial atten-

tion and response inhibition.

Experienced deficits in attention are state marker for depression, and a

lack of self-conscience in one’s own cognitive performance due to depressive

mood can motivate the patients to attend a neuropsychological assessment.

Subtle deficits in the divided attention have been demonstrated in patients

suffering from depression and they seem not to recover even after remission

from the mood disorder (Reppermund et al., 2007; Reppermund, Ising,

Lucae, & Zihl, 2009).

Quite often middle-aged patients do not suffer from memory impairment

but from concentration problems and they interpret problems of “losing

track” of what to do as a memory deficit, whereas it is in fact a working

memory deficit. In our experience, the digit span task is not sensitive for

such kind of deficits in middle-aged patients. The TAP offers the possibility

to test verbal working memory with a two-back task (based on numbers

between 1 and 9). For visuospatial working memory one may use the visuo-

spatial working memory task of the CANTAB. In the case of an isolated but

reproducible deficit in the TAP 2-back task, one should also ask for hypo- or

hyperthyroidism, which might lead to such a deficit (Samuels, 2014; Zhu

et al., 2006).

The copying task of the CERAD-NAB is much too easy for mild impair-

ments in visuoconstructive functions. Therefore we use the Block design test

of the Wechsler Intelligence Examination in middle-aged patients. On the

other hand, the block design test is hypersensitive for visuospatial perfor-

mance and an impairment in this task should only be taken as indication to

reproduce the deficit with additional tasks (e.g., copying the ReyOsterrieth

complex figure) and with dorsal visuospatial subtests from the Visual Object

and Perception Battery (compare the Chapter 3: The Alphasynucleinopathies:

Parkinson’s Disease, Lewy Body Dementia, and Multisystem Atrophy, on

Last, but not least, the TAP offers the opportunity to use the object alter-

nation task for differential diagnosis. The object alternation task is sensitive

for orbitofrontal, insular, and anterior temporal lobe lesions (compare

Chapter 6: Behavioral Variant of Frontotemporal Dementia, on the behav-

1.6 Structured and efficient testing presupposes a structured and efficient anamnesis

The specific strategy of testing patients for a differential diagnosis should be

based on a preliminary idea/suspicion of the patient’s disease (whereby this

hypothesis should be reformulated if test results are discrepant). The anam-

nesis provides the necessary information to formulate a first hypothesis

for the testing strategy (Chapter 8: A Short Guideline for the

Neuropsychological Differential Diagnosis of the Dementias, provides a

guideline which information should be looked for after having formulated a

first hypothesis for the diagnosis). For many patients, one will have to go

back to the anamnesis more than once or patients and their relatives must be

given questionnaires to validate the etiological hypothesis. An anamnesis

should start with patients providing a description of their problems (by them-

selves). It is important to ask relatives not to intervene during this phase and

to wait with comments and additional information until the anamnesis of the

patient is finished. After the spontaneous delineation of the problems, that is,

the reasons for attending the neuropsychological assessment, the anamnesis

should primarily focus on the functions of the autonomous nervous system.

This includes problems with swallowing and speaking (hoarseness), pro-

blems with vertigo, incontinence and constipation, erectile dysfunctions, and

any sleep issues (duration, depth, and motor unrest such as speaking during

sleep, and restless legs). Disorders of the autonomous nervous system, in

situations where they cannot be explained by additional diseases, are fre-

quently an indication for typical or atypical Parkinson’s disease.

Incontinence might be a symptom of normal pressure hydrocephalus. Finally

48 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

regularly sleeping 10 or more hours a day can be a sign of ongoing

neurodegeneration.

The anamnesis should then focus on walking impairments, experienced

falls, and postural instability as well as the use of the hands, handwriting,

and any existing tremor. Resting tremor, frequent falls, problems with pos-

tural balance, and limited handwriting abilities are frequent problems in idio-

pathic and atypical Parkinson’s disease. The third major topic of the

anamnesis concerns the senses, that is, deficits in seeing, hearing, smelling,

and taste. Hypoacusis (partial loss of hearing ability) is a common problem

in elderly individuals which leads to social isolation and may have detrimen-

tal effects on cognition. Therefore advising the patient to use hearing aids

the whole day might be helpful. Olfactory deficits may be the first signs of a

beginning Parkinson’s disease. Furthermore the patient should be asked

about his or her appetite as well as possible weight loss since weight loss is

common in patients with dementia. In this case, weight loss is usually

accompanied by some loss of appetite or of having a regular meal. A signifi-

cant weight loss (. 8 kg in less than half a year) without any explanation

(e.g., another disease, voluntary reduction of caloric intake) should be han-

dled as a warning signal for a possible cancer or tumor growth.

The next step of the anamnesis should focus on the cognitive functions of

memory, working memory, attention, and language. Patients with subjective

memory impairments should always be asked whether they have problems

with orientation in new environments (e.g., when they are on vacation). The

anamnesis should also try to determine whether the subjective memory

impairment concerns episodic memory (Can you remember things that you

heard yesterday?) or working memory (Do you (only) forget what your

intention is when you move into the next room?). Working memory pro-

blems are uncommon in Alzheimer’s disease; however, patients often con-

fuse them with episodic memory problems. To gain a better impression of

the problem, one may also ask whether the patients usually remember the

forgotten events through recognition, that is, after receiving some cues about

the event by relatives or friends. Intact recognition but high forgetfulness

biases the diagnostic hypothesis away from AD.

Attentional performance should be assessed by differentiating between

distractibility and tiredness. Again, excessive tiredness is unusual for

Alzheimer’s disease. Falling asleep during the day may be caused by sleep

problems (e.g., sleep apnea) or by cardiovascular diseases. Both are often

associated with cognitive problems. If patients stress that they tire quickly,

one should also ask if they experience mental slowing when confronted with

problems. Mental slowing is a typical problem in patients with white matter

lesions (vascular dementia) as well as in patients with Parkinson’s disease.

The questions regarding language impairments concern understanding,

word finding problems, and reduced drive for verbal communication. It is

important to inquire about comprehension and word finding problems

separately and to make sure that the patients report their verbal understand-

ing abilities independent of hearing problems. If the patient reports word

finding problems, one should examine (as with memory impairments)

whether this impairment started suddenly, if it is slowly increasing or if it is

constant. Another important question is whether it concerns the speaking or

the access of the words. Moreover, it is important to look for paraphasia dur-

ing the interaction with the patient. Paraphasia may concern the use of a

wrong, but semantically related word or producing phonological similar but

deviating words. It is important to note which of these possibilities are pres-

ent. Another aspect of language is how the patient’s speech sounds: are there

any signs of dysarthric speech or reduction in the force of the voice? If the

patients are answering only in short sentences, one should also ask if they

have lost interest in or reduced their amount of verbal interactions.

The last topic of the anamnesis regarding subjectively experienced

changes concerns mood. Questioning patients about mood changes should

cover aspects such as depressed or euphoric mood, loss of drive, social with-

drawal, increased irritability, anxiety and fearfulness, and being driven by

unrest. Feeling more dependent on others is typical for patients with AD,

whereas social withdrawal and increased irritability in the presence of stran-

gers might be an indication of the behavioral variant of frontotemporal

dementia. On the other hand, increased short-term irritability and affect labil-

ity are symptoms often associated with vascular dementia. If the patient tries

to encourage a relative to answer the questions during the anamnesis, the

development of the patient’s interpersonal dependency, for example, a recent

aversion of being home alone, should be examined.

The last part of the anamnesis concerns the medical history. The patient

should be asked about any drugs or medications he or she is taking. The

meaning of the question has also been introduced above. It is also important

to ask if they have already experienced any neurological, psychiatric or psy-

chological disorder, or disease during their life. This question should be

broadened to include the medical history of the patient’s family as well.

There are some kinds of dementia that have a strong genetic component

(e.g., behavioral frontotemporal lobe dementia, early onset Alzheimer’s dis-

ease). Finally, whether the patient has been assessed by cranial Computer

Tomography or Magnet Resonance Imaging should be established and the

results should be requested.

1.7 Summary: Prospect of neuropsychological testing of dementia

As worldwide life expectancy raises, the upcoming decades will see a global

increase in the number of patients with dementia. This will be accompanied

by an enormous medical and psychosocial challenge for the patients, their

relatives, and society. The diagnosis of dementia is a syndrome diagnosis

and should therefore be based on accepted criteria (e.g., the ICD-10). The

syndrome of dementia can be caused by several diseases which must be dif-

ferentiated. Only an etiological classification (e.g., which is listed in ICD-10)

permits a well-founded statement about disease progress and necessary treat-

ment. Dementia is a clinical, descriptive diagnosis; it does not necessarily

imply a prognostic statement and even if the cause is progressive neurode-

generative the long-term course may vary considerably between patients.

Effective treatment for dementia relies on precise and early diagnosis

because in later stages the neurodegenerative process already caused irrevers-

ible atrophy. During later phases of dementia, delaying its progression will

be the most important concern. There are currently no specific or clear-cut

biomarkers for many types of dementia. Therefore the differential diagnosis

relies on the clinical investigation of the patients and on the combination of

different information to arrive at the most appropriate diagnosis (“probable”

in the case of a combined positive functional and somatic validation and

“possible” in the case of only the functional impairments coinciding with the

diagnosis). Neuropsychological testing is one method that can assist the dif-

ferential diagnosis of dementia at the earliest clinical period of the disease.

While screening tests like the MMSE help in determining the severity of the

dementia, they do not provide information for a differential diagnosis, or do

they detect the first signs of cognitive decline. Moreover, most of the screen-

ing tests do not rely heavily enough on age-, gender-, and education-based

test norms. Because these variables influence the results of screening tests,

these tests do not convey reliable information. Rather, they provide only an

estimation of the disease’s progression. Such a rating is important to judge

the probable handicaps of the patient in everyday life and to assist the

decision-making process regarding, for example, driving or nursing homes.

Neuropsychological testing in patients with suspected dementia therefore has

two purposes: predicting the ability to live independently and to offer infor-

mation about the possible cause of the impairment (supporting a specific dif-

ferential diagnosis). Hopefully in the near future, there will be a third

purpose of neuropsychological testing: to inform patients about the best func-

tional treatment as well as its optimal level of difficulty (the scarce literature

concerning neuropsychological rehabilitation in the specific dementias will

be discussed at the end of the following chapters).

With respect to the purpose of differential diagnosis, Table 1.14 provides

an overview of a set of core tests that may be used for a first impression of

The CERAD-NAB is a standardized test battery that is based on the

results of more than 1100 control subjects (Schmid et al., 2014) and there-

fore the use of the CERAD-NAB as a core set of tests is recommended. In

theory, a performance below an absolute cut-off score should be indicative

of the specific neurodegenerative disease the patient is suffering from.

Unfortunately, there seems to be no absolute cut-off scores for any subtest of

the CERAD-NAB concerning differential diagnosing.

The statement of a possible diagnosis depends on the formulation of

assumptions regarding the kind of disease that might be present. Such

assumptions depend on the anamnesis, which should encompass questions

concerning cognitive deficits as well as autonomous functioning, walking,

control of the hands, mood, and previous relevant diseases of the patient and

their relatives. Together with the results of the CERAD-NAB and the other

tests from the core set, it should be possible to define specific impairment

patterns that help to develop a hypothesis for the differential diagnosis. This
