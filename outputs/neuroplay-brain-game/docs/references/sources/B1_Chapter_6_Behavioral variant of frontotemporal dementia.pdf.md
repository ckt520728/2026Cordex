#### Behavioral variant of frontotemporal dementia

6.1 General summary

Decrease of social contacts, emotional impoverishment, early decrease of criti-

cism, and lack of insight are cardinal symptoms of the behavioral variant of fron-

tal temporal dementia (FTD). On a behavioral level conspicuous behavior,

disinhibition in social interaction, loss of interest (e.g., in self-care), and risky

decision making characterize the disease. Neuropsychological testing should

encompass prefrontal lobe functions sensitive tests \[e.g., working memory and

flexibility of the Testbatterie zur Aufmerksamkeitsprüfung (TAP)\], the reading

the mind in the eyes test (RMET), and picture arrangement of the WIE. Tests for

affective empathy and object alternation task are especially sensitive in the early

stages of bvFTD. Standard neuropsychological tests such as the Consortium to

Establish a Registry for Alzheimer’s Disease, Neuropsychological Assessment

Battery (CERAD-NAB) and the DemTect are not effective for the diagnosis of

bvFTD. If there is any indication for bvFTD, the neuropsychiatric inventory

(NPI) should be answered by close friends or relatives of the patient. Observed

changes in eating behavior and in irritability, disinhibition and distraction are of

major interest. Moreover the clinical observation should look for signs of sponta-

neous prehension behavior and for increasing tension in the relationship to

other persons not only related to increase irritability but also to a loss of

affective empathy.

Up to now there is no specific treatment for bvFTD neither on a func-

tional nor on a pharmacological level. Serotonergic modulation might

have the potential for symptom modulation (reducing disinhibition in the

patients), but there is no large-scale randomized controlled trial (RCT) to

show that on a high level of evidence. The German guidelines for demen-

tia argue for risperidone to reduce the behavioral impairments in bvFTD.

In any case relatives of bvFTD should be offered professional education

on how to deal with their affected family members because their behavior

leads to severe losses of quality of live and often poses the question how

to respond correctly.

The behavioral abnormalities listed in Table 6.2 lead to psychosocial con-

example, the risk to develop criminal behavior is high: more than 14% of the

patients exhibited some kind of criminal behavior and 6.4% displayed violent

manifestations of criminal behavior in the bvFTD group included theft, traf-

those afflicted with AD tend to commit traffic violations or misconducts

often related to cognitive impairments, for example, forgetting to pay for

their bills.

protein tau. This group is also called tauopathy. The second, much smaller, group

is related to fused in sarcoma (FUS) positive inclusions. Patients suffering from

this neuropathological process exhibit the classical behavioral symptoms of fron-

totemporal lobe dementia. It is important to keep in mind, however, that there

are numerous patients with clinically similar symptoms which do not stem from

FUS-positive inclusions pathology. The third major group of pathology leading

to frontotemporal lobe degeneration goes back to the protein progranulin, which

governs the distribution of TAR binding protein 43 inside and outside the cell

nucleus. Patients suffering from this neuropathological degenerative process often

suffer from semantic dementia or motor neuron disease symptoms, however, this

may not be the primary symptom of their disease.

The behavioral variant of the FTD is a clinical syndrome and may be a

consequence of all three neuropathological processes. This does not mean

that there are no specific neuropathological models for the neuropathological

pathway leading to bvFTD. We will discuss one very interesting and specific

model later in this chapter. A specific neuropathological model for the

behavioral variant of FTD is possible, although the processes causing it may

differ because all these processes selectively impair one or two specific neu-

ronal cell types. In this case the “far” cause may differ but the “near” cause,

the degeneration of the few specific cell types, may be the common core fea-

ture of the disease.

FUS pathway. Starting with the latter, the RNA-binding Protein FUS is a

protein of the cell nucleus (Dormann & Haass, 2011). The N-terminal seems

to be involved in the activation of the DNA transcription to RNA informa-

tion, while the C-terminal is important for the binding of proteins and RNA.

FUS is normally localized in the cell nucleus. The presence of FUS in the

cell body contributes to cell death. Therefore the neuropathological cascade

begins with the presence of the FUS protein in the cell body. Accumulation

of FUS then may add to cell stress granules, lead to gene mutation and, in

the long-run or by a tertiary unknown pathological process, to neuronal dis-

integration (Fig. 6.2). The protein ubiquitin, which changes the structure of

other proteins to improve cell regeneration, appears to play a role in the

inclusion of FUS protein into the cell body.

Ubiquitin is also involved in the second major neuropathological pathway

to FTD: the TDP-43 pathology (Fig. 6.1). Like the FUS protein, TDP-43 pri-

marily resides in the cell nucleus, although the protein has the capacity to

shuttle between the nucleus and the cytoplasm. TDP-43 has been reported to

play a role in regulating gene expression at the transcriptional level, regulat-

ing gene splicing, and stabilizing mRNA. TDP-43 may also significantly

contribute to cell traffic and transport. Under pathophysiological conditions,

TDP-43 is cleared from the nuclear compartment and accumulates in the

cytoplasm (Fig. 6.3). The granulin peptide appears to play a major part for

the concentration of TDP-43 in the cell nucleus, the cell body and for the

clearance of TDP-43 aggregations, by directing lysosomes toward the aggre-

gations (Prudencio et al., 2012). It is possible that TDP-43 aggregation

results from a decrease of progranulin release from microglia, which reduces

the clearance process of TDP-43 aggregations (Zhou et al., 2017).

6.5 A case report

The 63-year-old gentleman entered the neuropsychological assessment together

with his wife. He stated that there have been some episodes in which he has

been idle. Memory performance had declined. Furthermore upon asking for

more specific symptoms he answered that gait velocity had slowed a little bit.

He had experienced some weight gain, becomes tired more quickly, and the

mood status is depressed. He also replied that he was not taken any medical

drugs. According to his wife, however, he was taking Simvastatin and

L-Thyroxin. Upon inquiries regarding prior psychiatric or neurological disease,

he answered that he had experienced something like a panic disorder 17 years

ago, but that he has never suffered from a neurological disease. With respect

to familial medical history, his mother had suffered from a psychosis or

dementia (onset: 62 years of age), the father still seems to be healthy and the

statuses of the half-brothers were unknown. An MRI scan conducted several

weeks before did not show any impairment or atrophy.

The wife’s view of the situation was very different to that of the

6.6 Treatment of behavioral frontal temporal dementia

The German guidelines for dementia state that there is currently no

evidence-based pharmacological treatments of FTD. Thus the major target

for pharmacological intervention is to reduce the behavioral symptoms and

not to treat the disease. Memantine and cholinesterase inhibitors have not

demonstrated clinically significant efficacy in treating FTD patients and may

also hasten cognitive decline or worsen behavioral symptoms. As FTD

patients do not seem to suffer from a cholinergic deficit, one would also not

expect an improvement.

There is limited evidence that selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors

(SSRIs) may reduce impulsivity, irritability, disinhibition, and changes in eat-

ing behavior (Young et al., 2018). For example, in a small RCT, paroxetine

reduced symptoms severity scored with the NPI. However, a study using the

same drug with higher dosage showed no effect at all. Quite the contrary high-

er dosage of paroxetine seemed to induce cognitive impairments. The outcome

differences of these studies are unclear. It is possible that higher dosage of par-

oxetine lead to an overstimulation of the serotonergic system. On the other

hand the patients of the second study were somewhat later in their disease pro-

gression and pharmacological treatment may no longer have been effective.

Similar effects have been documented for citalopram, which is also an (newer)

SSRI. There are also some case studies showing an effect for sertraline.

Hughes, Rittman, Regenthal, Robbins, and Rowe (2015) treated 12

patients with citalopram for one neuropsychological and neurophysiological

test session and compared their performance with another placebo session.

On a behavioral level, citalopram did not improve the performance of the

patients. On a neurophysiological level, however, they could show that cita-

lopram improved the P3 at PZ (according to the 10/20 system of the EEG)

for NoGo trials, moving its amplitude in the direction of a healthy control

group (Fig. 6.11).

Moreover citalopram enhanced activation in the right inferior frontal

gyrus. Hughes et al. (2015) argued that the study indicates that increasing

the serotonergic neurotransmission might help reduce the dysfunctional fron-

tal cortical systems and disinhibition in bvFTD patients. Although these

results are interesting, larger RCT studies treating bvFTD patients with

SSRIs, which show effective results in clinical measures, that is reduced dis-

inhibition, impulsivity, and irritability, are needed before a regular treatment

with SSRI in bvFTD patients can be recommended.

The German guidelines state that any use of neuroleptics may be associ-

ated with a higher risk of death and cerebrovascular events. Therefore neuro-

leptics should be avoided. Haloperidol shows the highest risk and quetiapine

the lowest. If not effectively reduced by psychosocial intervention, agitation

may be controlled by atypical antipsychotics such as risperidone or aripipra-

zole, but not by olanzapine (due to the anticholinergic side effects). It should

330 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

be noted that risperidone and aripiprazole also act on the serotonergic trans-

mitter system. Another option for treating agitation is carbamazepine and

antidepressants. Hallucinations and delusions might also be treated by risper-

idone and aripiprazole, whereas none of the atypical neuroleptics have shown

any evidence in treating these symptoms.

Functional treatment studies for patients with bvFTD are rare and we are

not aware of any group study showing that cognitive training or cognitive

behavior therapy is effective in this patients’ group. There is one group study

using transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) in bvFTD patients to

improve the understanding of communication intention showing a positive

significant result (Cotelli et al., 2018). However, this study was based on

only one session and monitored the effects during the tDCS stimulation.

Casaletto et al. (2020) analyzed the effect of lifestyle (physical and cog-

nitive activities) in carriers of autosomal dominant frontotemporal lobar

degeneration, that is, the genetic variant of FTD. Higher physical and cogni-

tive activities were each associated with approximately 55% slower clinical

decline per year. This association was independent from brain atrophy.

6.7 Summary

Decrease of social contacts, emotional impoverishment, early decrease of criti-

cism, and lack of insight can be defined as cardinal symptoms of the behav-

ioral variant of FTD. On a behavioral level, conspicuous behavior,

disinhibition in social interaction, loss of interest (e.g., in self-care), and risky

decision making characterize the disease. bvFTD is caused by different neuro-

pathological pathways but may be related to focused loss of two specific cells

types of the brain: the von Economo and the fork cells (discussed later).

Neuropsychological testing of patients with bvFTD should encompass

prefrontal lobe functions sensitive tests (e.g., working memory and flexibility

of the TAP), the RMET, Picture arrangement of the WIE, and the NPI

answered by close friends or relatives. Tests for affective empathy and the

object alternation task are especially sensitive in the early stages of bvFTD.

Patients with bvFTD exhibit declined performance on tests assessing cogni-

tive empathy and executive functions but not so much in the very early phase

of their disease process. Standard neuropsychological tests such as the

CERAD-NAB and the DemTect are not effective for a differential diagnosis

Apart from the core set of tests, the neuropsychological differential diag-

nosis of bvFTD should include at least two social perception or understand-

ing tasks and the “RMET” has proven to be sensitive and of prognostic

value. Patients with bvFTD suffer from emotion recognition deficits and

these concern negative emotions more than positive emotions. On a cognitive

level the object alternation (termed flexibility) task of the TAP should be

used for the assessment. If there is any indication for bvFTD, the NPI should

be answered by a relative of the patient. Changes in eating behavior and in

irritability, disinhibition, and distraction are of major interest. Moreover the

clinical observation should look for signs of prehension behavior and the

anamnesis and increasing tension in the relationship not only related to

increase irritability but also to a loss of affective empathy.

The neuroradiological assessment of the patients might show atrophy in

the insular and the anterior cingulate and ventromedial prefrontal cortex;

however, the diagnosis cannot be based on MRI scanning. There may be an

increase level of total tau and phosphor-tau in bvFTD patients. Unfortunately

this is not always the case. Therefore the diagnosis primarily depends on the

clinical assessment.

Up to now there is no specific treatment for bvFTD neither on a func-

tional nor on a pharmacological level. Serotonergic modulation might have

the potential for symptom modulation (reducing disinhibition in the patients),

but there is no large-scale RCT to show that on a high level of evidence.

The German guidelines for dementia argue for risperidone to reduce the

behavioral impairments in bvFTD. In any case relatives of bvFTD should be

offered professional education on how to deal with their affected family

members, because their behavior leads to severe losses of quality of live and

often poses the question how to respond correctly. First data show that an

active cognitive and physical lifestyle may delay cognitive and behavioral

decline in carriers of autosomal dominant frontotemporal lobar degeneration.

6.8 Behavioral frontal temporal dementia and neurodegeneration of von Economo and fork cells in the right anterior insular and cingulate cortex

As argued earlier, there seems to be a right hemisphere frontopolar-cingulate-

insula-head-of-the-caudate-nucleus network, which is affected by the neuro-

pathological processes leading to the syndrome of bvFTD. Seeley (2008)

(compare Allman, Tetreault, Hakeem, & Park, 2011; Allman, Tetreault,

Hakeem, Manaye, et al., 2011; Kim et al., 2012; Seeley et al., 2008) recently

proposed that there may be neuropathological processes (i.e., based on tauopa-

thy, FUS in sarcoma and TDP43) converging on two distinctive cell types,

which are responsible for the core symptoms of the bvFTD.

Fig. 6.12 provides a summary of the model of Seeley (2008) and Seeley

et al. (2008): the three different neuropathological processes leading to fron-

totemporal lobe degeneration may converge in bvFTD on two specific cell

types (fork cells and von Economo cells). This leads to atrophy in the insular

cortex and the anterior and dorsal cingulate. As these structures play a cru-

cial role for these kinds of social-emotional processes, the patients will suffer

from social-emotional salience function deficits.

Following his model, the distinctive cell types involved are the von Economo

and the fork (Gabel) cells. These cells are localized predominantly in the (right)

insular cortex and the right anterior cingulate cortex (Figs. 6.13 and 6.14). These

areas show major atrophy and loss of tracer enhancement in FDG-PET.

von Economo and fork cells have large dendrites and axons but small

receptive fields. From a structural point of view, they look like neurons that

transmit and integrate information over long ranges in the brain. There is a

strong correlation between the number of these cells in the insular cortex and

the evolution of social behavior. For example, the relative number of von

Economo cells increases from chimpanzees to gorillas and from gorillas to

humans. Across these three species, there is a constant right hemispheric

dominance of von Economo cells in the frontal insular cortex (Fig. 6.14).

According to the voxel-based morphometrical studies mentioned earlier,

patients with bvFTD suffer from atrophy especially in regions, where the high-

est density of von Economo and fork cells can be found (Rankin et al., 2011;

Rohrer et al., 2015). Kim et al. (2012) (compare Santillo, Nilsson, & Englund,

2013) measured postmortem the numbers of these cells in the insular cortex in

bvFTD patients, AD patients, and healthy controls. They included 21 patients

with bvFTD, 5 patients with a tauopathy, 14 patients with TPD-43 positive

inclusions, and 2 FUS-positive patients. The results of this study are portrayed

It has already been mentioned that there is clear evidence that bvFTD patients

suffer from affective empathy but that it is not so clear if they also suffer from

cognitive empathy or theory of mind impairments. Affective empathy and social

cooperative behavior, based on theory of mind or perspective taking, are also not

the same in evolutionary terms. Affective empathy is present in many animal spe-

cies, whereas prosocial behavior in the sense of cognitive perspective taking is a

genuine human cognitive function (Tomasello & Carpenter, 2007). Monkeys usu-

ally do not show cooperative behavior that presuppose shared intentionality, that

is, the common goal and insight that a specific environmental state can be repro-

duced again and again only by cooperation (and not by one animal alone)

(Warneken, Chen, & Tomasello, 2006). Ontogenetically shared intentionality and

cognitive perspective taking start late in the childhood at the same time as execu-

tive functions develop, whereas affective empathy and the automatic drive to

cooperate can already been observed during the first year of living (Tomasello &

Gonzalez-Cabrera, 2017; Tomasello, 2018). The distinction between affective

empathy and perspective taking also differ for the specific view of social pro-

cesses, which is egocentric in the case of affective empathy, but allocentric in the

case of integrated perspective taking (Tomasello & Carpenter, 2007). Fig. 6.16 try

can be distinguished, which differ either in the perspective (observing some-

#### Observational (egocentric) perspective:

 person A perceives the mood state of person B (affective empathy); and

 person A represents the intention of person B (first-order theory of mind).

#### Birds’ view (allocentric) perspective:

 person A and person B share the intentionality for an environmental state,

which becomes possible only by their cooperation; and

 person A and person B know the social rules for cooperating defined by

their common culture.

Being based on different cognitive structures, it is possible that different

brain mechanisms are involved in generating social behavior, emotions, and

thought. Stietz, Jauk, Krach, and Kanske (2019) recently reviewed the differ-

ence between cognitive empathy (perspective taking and theory of mind men-

talizing) and affective empathy on a more global level, that is, without

focusing on the RMET and on lesion studies. They suggest that existing fMRI

studies in healthy subjects show that affective empathy is related to the ante-

rior insular and cingulate cortex in the case of negative emotions, to ventral

striatum and the orbitofrontal cortex in the case of positive emotions.

Cognitive empathy, on the other hand, relies on a temporal-frontal neuronal

network including, for example, the temporoparietal junction and the cuneus

(Shdo et al., 2018; Stietz et al., 2019; Thiel, Studte, Hildebrandt, Huster, &

Weerda, 2014), which are not primary sites for atrophy in bvFTD. The model

of Seeley (2008) postulates that the disease starts in the anterior right insular

and cingulate cortex, and these brain areas are related to the discrimination of

(negative) emotions (Stietz et al., 2019). In this respect, the two meta-analytic

review of Bora et al. (2016) and Henry et al. (2014) lend the model of Seeley

strong plausibility, because the first one argues that bvFTD patients do not suf-

fer from a deficit of recognizing emotions globally, but only of that emotions

which are related to the integrity of the insular cortex. The second one shows

that bvFTD patients are definitely impaired in affective empathy, whereas the

impairment in cognitive empathy might be derived from executive functions

deficits, which covary with cognitive empathy.

However, as mentioned earlier, the study of Roca et al. (2013) also dem-

onstrated deficits in cognitive discounting in patients with early bvFTD (not

only in affective empathy). Problems in discounting impulsive choice or in

shifting preferences have been documented often in patients in an early stage

of bvFTD (Manes et al., 2011). In any case, they are present in the later

phase of the disease. However, their presence in early bvFTD patients has

not always been replicated (Kloeters, Bertoux, O’Callaghan, Hodges, &

Hornberger, 2013). Moreover the possibility to discriminate bvFTD patients

from AD patients using discounting tests like the IOWA gambling task and

reversal learning seems to lower than that of emotion recognition, that is,

tests for affective empathy (Bertoux et al., 2013; Torralva et al., 2009;

Torralva et al., 2015).

Consequently, BvFTD seem to start with impairment in affective empa-

thy; however not necessarily with theory of mind problems and problems of

discounting or reversal learning. As these latter impairments are also part of

the clinical syndrome, however, their presence is more variable in the early

phase of the disease. And they may be related to executive functioning

impairments, which can be assessed with more traditional neuropsychologi-

cal tests. The existing neuropsychological findings therefore endorse the

model of Seeley (2008), at least up to now.
