#### Normal pressure hydrocephalus

7.1 General summary

Apart from the enlarged cerebral ventricles \[ventriculomegaly as a possible

cause of the increased hydrocephalic pressure\], which is the most important

neuroradiological sign, the clinical features of normal pressure hydrocepha-

lus (NPH) are gait disturbance, urinary incontinence, and cognitive

impairment. The diagnosis is mainly based on the effect of a spinal tap test,

that is, large-volume lumbar puncture. Patients presenting the clinical symp-

toms of idiopathic NPH (iNPH; especially gait disturbances) are diagnosed

with iNPH if the symptoms improve after removing approximately 50 mL of

cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Although the rationale for diagnosing NPH is

straightforward, the sensitivity of this procedure is not very high (B70%).

There is some indication that the optimal reevaluation time point for cogni-

tive improvement is at least 24 hours after the puncture and that waiting

longer (up to 2 days) may even increase the chance of finding cognitive

improvement.

Neuropsychological descriptions of iNPH often focus on executive

impairments. More specifically, several studies showed improvements in

mini-mental state examination (MMSE), phonological word fluency, trail

making test A (TMT-A), and upper extremity fine motor tests \[nine-hole peg

test (NHPT)\] in patients profiting from the spinal tap test. Most of the studies

investigating patients with iNPH before and after shunt surgery, the standard

treatment of iNPH, found improvement in exactly the same tests. Thus a

neuropsychological examination of iNPH should involve at least the cogni-

tive tasks mentioned earlier.

A second major purpose of cognitive evaluation of suspected patients

with iNPH is the differential diagnosis for a possible Alzheimer’s disease

(AD). Patients with AD (as a comorbidity) will show decreased accuracy in

verbal recall (intrusions) and in picture recognition. Patients showing signs

of AD may be shunted; however, it should be clear that any improvement

will soon diminish due to the progressive character of the (additional) AD.

7.2 Clinical, neuroradiological features of idiopathic normal pressure hydrocephalus, and its diagnosis

The clinical meaning and prevalence of NPH are topics of intensive debate

(Oliveira, Nitrini, & Roman, 2019). Some proponents argue that it is highly

underdiagnosed and being a leading cause for dementia that could be cured

if it gets much more attention. Other advocates argue that NPH is not a spe-

cific cause of dementia but rather only a state indicating increasing neurode-

generation. Thus any causal intervention reducing pressure exerted on the

brain parenchyma will only lead to a temporary relieve of the symptoms.

Apart from the enlarged cerebral ventricles (ventriculomegaly as a possi-

ble cause of the increased hydrocephalic pressure), which is the most impor-

tant neuroradiological sign of NPH, the three major clinical symptoms (the

triad or the triage) of NPH are as follows:

1. Gait disturbance: problems with initiating gait, with balance; bride, irreg-

ular steps; tendency to shuffle, stumble, and fall; and problems in turning

around. In extreme cases: complete loss of walking ability.

2. Urinary incontinence: imperative urinary urge with involuntary urinary

loss, rarely also fecal incontinence

3. Cognitive impairment: memory deficits, personality change (apathy), and

rarely disorders of consciousness.

Neurological diagnosis is mainly based on gait disturbance (as the cardi-

nal sign) and on the results from neuroimaging, whereas incontinence and

cognitive impairment enhance the safety of the diagnosis but are not primary

features (supportive features) (Razay, Wimmer, & Robertson, 2019).

If there is an independent clinical syndrome such as NPH, it should be

potentially reversible: reducing the hydrocephalic pressure should relieve the

symptoms. Treating patients with NPH due to known causes like traumatic

brain injury or nontraumatic subarachnoid bleeding show that this might be

the case. Such patients often experience a relatively fast and clinical mean-

ingful recovery after shunting. Instances in which the cause of NPH is

known are called secondary NPH. If the cause is unknown, NPH is termed

idiopathic (iNPH). Up to now it is unclear whether this distinction is mean-

ingful in terms of diagnosing, treating, and prognosis. Arguing that it does

not make any important difference would imply that shunting should also

help patients with iNPH. However, patients with secondary NPH can show

clinical features like confabulation, motor unrest, and severe attention defi-

cits, which are not common in iNPH. If this clinical difference implies that

iNPH and secondary NPH show similar neuroradiological features but rely

on a different and in the case of iNPH on a subtle progressive cause would

also change the expectations about the effectiveness of shunting for patients

with iNPH. The worst consequence might be that patients with secondary

NPH can be treated by surgery, but patients with iNPH cannot be treated by

configurations that are also progressive in short term (e.g., due to a tumor).

As these kinds of hydrocephalus leads to a fast decline of ability, to convul-

sions, and loss of consciousness, it is important to distinguish them from

iNPH, which is long lasting and slowly progressive, if at all. Short-term pro-

gressive hydrocephalus does not involve the production and absorption

dynamics of CSF but its flow through the brain. Therefore it is called hydro-

between the ventricles), whereas idiopathic and secondary NPH are called

hydrocephalus malresorptivus.

Without being combined into a triage, the single clinical symptoms of

iNPH are highly unspecific. Cognitive impairment in the elderly is common

as well as urinary incontinency (especially in women) and gait impairment.

Up to now, there are no simple clinical assessments, which would be able to

distinguish iNPH from patients with AD or vascular dementia with high

certainty. Therefore the neuroradiological features of iNPH are important for

the diagnosis (Fig. 7.1). They involve an Evans’s index greater than 0.3, no

tumor explaining ventriculomegaly and as supportive aspects enlarged tem-

poral horns without hippocampal atrophy, a callosal angle of approximately

80 or 90 degrees (Virhammar, Laurell, Cesarini, & Larsson, 2014a), periven-

tricular signal changes on cranial Computertomography (cCT) not caused by

microvascular changes, and flow void in the Sylvian aqueduct or fourth ven-

tricle on MRI scans. Other guidelines claim that the periventricular changes

are unimportant, whereas the narrowing of the dorsal sulci and enlarged syl-

vian fissures and basal cisterns are important for diagnosing iNPH.

The Evan’s index, the callosal angle, disproportional enlarged subarach-

noid space hydrocephalus, and increased temporal horns are the most distinc-

tive features of iNPH (Table 7.1) (Virhammar et al., 2014a, 2014b). On the

other hand, ventriculomegaly is also a common feature of neurodegeneration.

Even when taking other defining or supporting features of the neuroradiolog-

ical criteria into account, there are many patients that do not show the clini-

cal symptoms of iNPH and there are patients with clinically probable iNPH

that do not exhibit the corresponding neuroradiological signs. Thus the clini-

cal diagnosis is mainly based on the effect of a spinal tap test, that is, large-

volume lumbar puncture. Patients presenting the clinical symptoms of iNPH

(especially gait disturbances) and some of the neuroradiological features are

diagnosed with iNPH if the symptoms improve after removing approximately

50 mL of CSF. A proposed diagnostical procedure to evaluate patients suf-

fering from iNPH therefore involves a repeated measure of their performance

and experienced symptoms before and after the spinal tap test (Table 7.2).

7.3 Neurophysiology and neuroanatomy of idiopathic normal pressure hydrocephalus

The CSF is produced in the choroidal plexus of the lateral, third, and fourth

ventricle (Fig. 7.2). It then shuttles between the ventricular system and the

brain parenchyma. Because of continued production, the CSF flows in the

direction of the intraventricular foramen and into the fourth ventricle through

the aqueduct. The CSF enters the subarachnoid space at the level of the pons

and the medulla oblongata. At this position the direction of the flow is

reversed and the CSF travels toward the superior sagittal sinus and enters

through the arachnoid granulations the venous system where it is absorbed

together with the blood from the brain circulation.

The factors causing iNPH are unknown, however, two major explanation

have been developed. iNPH may be caused by problems of CSF drainage

into the venous transport system, for example, at the level of the arachnoid

granulations, limiting CSF flow into the superior sagittal sinus. Another

explanation relies on hypertension of the venous system. Because of this

hypertension (or loss of adaptation), more force is necessary to transport the

CSF out of the brain. Both theories would explain ventriculomegaly and the

slow or absent progress of the hydrocephalus: having reached a specific level

where the force inside the brain is high enough, CSF would start to flow out-

side the ventricular system again. However, this force has to be higher than

in healthy subjects even if it is stable for months or years. Irrespective of

which explanation for iNPH is correct, the immediate physiological conse-

quences of increased intracranial pressure are as follows:

 A decreasing difference between arterial and venous blood pressure, and

thus a potential lowering of brain blood supply.

 The development of brain vasogenic edema with CSF backwater (concen-

tration of CSF in the intercellular space of white matter structures adja-

cent to the ballooned ventricles).

 The potential reduction of local blood supply by relocation and shifting

of blood vessels.

When diagnosing iNPH, the causes and their physiological consequences

may be less relevant than their functional consequences (the triad of gait dis-

turbance, urinary incontinence, and cognitive impairment). Unfortunately, it

is not fully understood why iNPH leads to these three specific disorders. One

explanation proposes that the pressure causes the clinical consequences.

According the homunculus of the primary motor areas, the cortical areas

responsible for walking are located medially. Moreover, their fibers run

medially and adjacent to the frontal horn of the lateral ventricle. The dorsal

anterior cingulate cortex is highly involved in bladder control and this brain

structure is localized inferior and next to the homunculus representing leg

movements (Griffiths, Derbyshire, Stenger, & Resnick, 2005). Thus the

increased pressure in the lateral ventricle might lead to a breakdown of the

barrier between the ventricle system and the brain resulting in the invasion

of CSF into adjacent brain structures and impairing the fiber system for gait

and urinary continence. The explanation of memory performance impairment

is quite similar: the hippocampus is located at the bottom of the temporal

horn of the lateral ventricle as such increased pressure or transgression of

CSF into the hippocampus could cause memory declines.

Although the explanations of the consequences of iNPH have many

advantages, it is only partly compatible with the changes that should take

place after lowering hydrocephalic pressure by external drainage. Reducing

pressure should lead to a fast relieve of the problems, whereby this relieve

should continue only as long as the reduction of the pressure takes place.

Unfortunately there is only a weak (or even absent) relationship between the

ventricular size changes due to external drainage and the clinical conse-

quences of the iNPH. Lenfeldt et al. (2012) measured the ventricular size

after 3 days of external drainage and found that clinical improvement

occurred in patients with NPH despite unaltered ventricles and that ventricu-

lar size was of little relevance for improvement in the patients. In this respect

it should also be mentioned that the choroidal plexus of the brain reproduces

the CSF volume two or three times a day. Hence evacuating the brain of all

its CSF would be counterbalanced by the production of new CSF after less

than a day. On the other hand the main effect of CSF drainage is not neces-

sarily present directly after the procedure but only after at least one or even

more than 2 days. Thus a direct (physical) effect of the pressure related to

iNPH on motor and cognitive performance can be excluded.

A recent explanation regarding the detrimental effects of iNPH on motor

and cognitive performance argues that the increased pressure has two differ-

ent effects. The first one is focal and explains the specific motor and cogni-

tive changes. Compression of the dorsal and mesial brain structures around

the cerebral convexity, where the gait centers of the brain are located, eluci-

date the problems in walking. Decompressing these centers is a major conse-

quence of shunting surgery. The second consequence of iNPH may be a

reduction of CSF circulation in the parenchyma (disturbance of the glympha-

tic system of the brain). One function of the glymphatic system is the elimi-

nation of neurotoxic substances such as beta-amyloid(1-42), tau-protein, and

proinflammatory cytokines out of the brain. Thus increased pressure in spe-

cific brain areas might lead to the accumulation of said neurotoxins and

therefore to reduction of neuronal functioning. It is well-known that the inci-

dence and prevalence of patients suffering from AD increases in patients

with iNPH (Kang, Ko, Jin, Suk, & Lee, 2014; Koivisto et al., 2013; Koivisto

et al., 2016) and that reduced clearance of soluble beta-amyloid(1-42) pro-

teins accumulating into insoluble plagues might be the reason for this

increased association between iNPH and AD. The involvement of the glym-

phatic system may also explain why the spinal tap test does not have an

immediate but a delayed effect on the patient’s performance. The acute emp-

tying of CSF from the brain might induce a slow transport processes from

the interstitial space to the venous system and therefore a clearance of the

extracellular space from disruptive proteins. Because this process takes time

(related to the production and travelling velocity of new CSF), maximal

behavioral changes can be observed only after 1 or 2 days.

7.4 Treatment of normal pressure hydrocephalus

Implanting a shunt for extracranial drainage of CSF by neurosurgery is the

treatment of choice for NPH. There are only two other interventions with

unclear success and without evidence-based studies showing their effective-

ness. The first one concerns the use of acetazolamide, a carbonic anhydrase

inhibitor, leading to increased urinary secretion, and reduction of edema in

the body. Up to now, there are only case series demonstrating effective

treatment of NPH with this drug. The other possibility is the continuous rep-

etition of the spinal taps after a few weeks.

Using shunt surgery as treatment results in successful treatment in up to

80% of the patients (depending on the cause of the hydrocephalus, with

unclear endurance and a high rate of complications). The chance for a full

recovery is high early in the disease (,2 years after the presentation of the

first symptoms) (Vakili et al., 2016; Yamada et al., 2017); if all three major

symptoms are present, cognitive deficits are weak, and there is no additional

disturbance of oxygen supply of the brain (e.g., sleep apnea or chronic

obstructive pulmonary disorder). The recovery prospects for the symptoms

differ: gait disorder shows up to 80% recovery, whereas cognitive deficits

only show 60% recovery. The degree of improvement, however, differs con-

siderably between studies (Shaw, Mahant, Jacobson, & Owler, 2016; Solana,

Sahuquillo, Junque, Quintana, & Poca, 2012).

Not all reviews of the shunt surgery are as optimistic as discussed ear-

lier. A recent review from Espay et al. (2017) alleges that NPH might not

be distinctive disease but rather an early sign of neurodegeneration. One

important argument of this review is that also there seems to be a large

number of patients that benefit from shunt-delivered external brain drain-

age, however, the gain in walking and sometimes cognitive performance

was short-term. After 1 or 2 years the number of patients profiting from

shunts drops down to less than 30%. If NPH really was a distinctive dis-

ease, shunt treatment should reveal much higher rates in patients profiting

from this treatment. Kang et al. (2014) and Koivisto et al. (2016) showed

7.5 Neuropsychology of normal pressure hydrocephalus

Neuropsychological assessment in patients with iNPH has three different

purposes. The first goal is to obtain additional information regarding whether

the patient really suffers from iNPH and not (additionally) from another dis-

ease. In the case of iNPH, this task is more important than in neurodegenera-

tive diseases since the treatment of iNPH is causal treatment and may have

severe aversive consequences (e.g., shunt infection). It is therefore necessary

to know in advance whether the patient is also suffering from any other

neurodegenerative diseases. Should this be the case, a shunt implantation

will only lead to short-term relieve and will probably not be offered to the

patient. The second goal is to make sure that the shunt implantation will help

the patient. This purpose aims to monitor the effects of the spinal tap test

and compare the preperformance and postperformance. Last but not least,

neuropsychology is involved in evaluating the efficacy of the shunt surgery.

7.5.1 Are there characteristic cognitive features of idiopathic normal pressure hydrocephalus?

The concrete causes of the neuropsychological symptoms of an iNPH are

unknown, although there are several assumptions (diffusion of the CSF into

the white matter, impaired removal of beta-amyloid and other “waste” pro-

teins by reduced CSF flow, local hypoxia, and local inflammation). From a

neuropsychiatric perspective, the main problems of patients with iNPH are

apathy, slowing, and emotional blunting (Peterson, Housden, et al., 2016).

Together the cognitive and emotional changes point to frontal deficits, and

one explanation could be that they are caused by caudate nuclei impairments.

The caudate nucleus is located adjacent to the lateral ventricles, and the head

of the caudate nucleus is part of a frontostriatal cognitive network. However,

we will discuss this issue later.

Starting with the first task of neuropsychological assessment, that is, to

distinguish between patients with iNPH and patients with cognitive deficits

due to other cause, iNPH is often classified as those with predominant execu-

tive deficits (Gleichgerrcht et al., 2009; Iddon et al., 1999; Kanno et al.,

2012; Ogino et al., 2006). Other authors argue that patients wth iNPH show

classical symptoms of frontosubcortical dementia (Devito et al., 2005). Thus

the frontal assessment battery (FAB), which is discussed in more detail in

the Chapter 3, The Alphasynucleinopathies: Parkinson’s Disease, Lewy

Body Dementia, and Multisystem Atrophy (Section 3.2.4: Cognitive

Impairment and the Functional Independence of the Patients), or subtests of

the FAB have been used to differentiate patients with iNPH from patients

without iNPH. Because the FAB measures mental abstraction and motor

inhibition, patients with iNPH should be impaired in the FAB if iNPH is

being a dysexecutive syndrome. Iseki, Takahashi, Wada, Kawanami, and

Kato (2013) found that the fistedgepalm test is sensitive to iNPH.

Furthermore Ko, Lee, and Kang (2017) argue that the total score of the FAB

predict treatment effects in iNPH. We used our data from previous studies to

analyze differences between patients with iNPH that profited from the spinal

tap test (n5 22), those that were evaluated for iNPH but did not profit from

the spinal tap test (n5 34), and CSF-validated patients with AD (n5 21)

(Table 7.3). However, we did not discover any significant differences

between suspected patients with iNPH (those who profited from the spinal

tap test vs those who did not) for the fistedgepalm test, Lurijas bimanual

coordination task, and not for the applause sign.

span, and semantic word fluency. In these task, the two patients’ group did

sensitive to distinguish between patients with iNPH and patients with AD.

This study has not been replicated since its emergence. Thus impaired work-

ing memory does not currently count as a neuropsychological criterion for

We can conclude with Damasceno (2009) that testing of patients with

iNPH should cover attention or speed of information processing, executive

functions, working memory, and memory. The expectation is that iNPH

should be impaired in the first two functions. Working memory performance

and memory testing serves as a tool to exclude accompanying AD and

should encompass delayed recall and recognition. For this purpose specific

attention should be given to false positives in recall and recognition because

patients with iNPH are not performing more false positives, but patients with

AD do. Concerning higher order motor tasks, patients with iNPH seem to

suffer from some disinhibition compared to patients with AD. However, up

to now there are no established clear-cut cognitive tasks to distinguish

between iNPH and patients with other cause for frontosubcortical

impairments.

7.5.2 Does neuropsychological examination predict the success of shunt surgery?

Identifying patients with possible iNPH by neuropsychological tests does

not answer whether impaired cognitive performance of patients with iNPH

will be improved by relieving the ventricle system from the increased CSF

pressure. The answer to this question can be based on pre and post compar-

isons of shunted patients and of patients receiving a spinal tap procedure.

Starting with the former comparison, Peterson, Savulich, et al. (2016)

reviewed the effect of shunt surgery on cognitive performance. They were

able to include 23 studies with 1059 patients in their review. All studies,

except for one, concerned patients with iNPH, whereby the one exception

only contribute 14 patients. Thus the results of this meta-analytic review

were not be biased by this one study and should hold true for patients with

iNPH. The following neuropsychological tests could be reviewed as they

were used often enough in the empirical studies: MMSE (19 studies), Rey

auditory verbal learning test (RAVLT) (7 studies), digit span backward (6

Normal pressure hydrocephalus Chapter | 7 361

studies), phonological word fluency (8 studies), trail making test B (9 stud-

ies), and TMT-A (13 studies). Digit span backwards and TMT-B perfor-

mance were scarcely influenced by shunt surgery. Performance changes in

the remaining tests were highly significant, especially in the MMSE,

RAVLT, and TMT-A. Improvement in MMSE encompassed in the average

2.2 points, for the RAVLT delayed recall 1.9 words, for phonological word

fluency 2.7 words and for the TMT-A 25.9 seconds. Another important

result of the review is that age, time-to-retest, and gender had no impact on

the MMSE difference score. This suggests that the effect of shunt surgery

does not depend on these variables. Although the authors looked at long-

term effects of shunt surgery, they were unable to identify sufficient studies

that offer data for 2 years or longer. Hence the meaning of this review is

restricted to the first year after shunt surgery. Moreover for most neuropsy-

chological tests, the review by Peterson, Savulich, et al. (2016) was based

on less than 10 studies. Therefore the clearest evidence for improved per-

formance after shunt implantation exists for the MMSE and the TMT-A

(Fig. 7.3A and B).

Fine motor performance has also been used to differentiate between

patients suspected with iNPH that might or might not benefit from shunt-

ing surgery. Two studies showed that performance in the NHPT signifi-

cantly improved after shunting surgery (Gallagher, Marquez, &

Osmotherly, 2018; Isik, Kaya, Ates Bulut, Dokuzlar, & Soysal, 2019),

one study showed that performance in the grooved pegboard was sensitive

to outcome of shunting in patients with iNPH (Hellstrom, Klinge, Tans, &

Wikkelso, 2012) and one study showed that finger tapping speed pre-

dicted postshunting outcome (Liouta et al., 2017). Unfortunately the

effect of shunting on cognitive performing in everyday life is largely

unknown. This holds especially for the question if there is a relationship

between cognitive impairment prior shunting, cognitive improvement

after shunting (both in specific tests) and instrumental activities of daily

life or quality of life after shunting.

7.7 The mesial frontal system and the functional impairment of idiopathic normal pressure hydrocephalus

As stated earlier, a specific explanation between the neurophysiology and

neuroanatomy of iNPH and its functional consequences is still lacking. The

neuropsychological pattern of iNPH, discussed earlier, is ambiguous: impair-

ments in visual search or cognitive speech (TMT A) and in phonological

word fluency do not convey information about specific and localized brain

malfunctioning and may be a consequence of reduced mental speed.

Reduced mental speed may also explain the learning and recall deficits in

patients with iNPH. Increased pressure, slowed absorption of CSF, and there-

fore decreased clearance of soluble beta-amyloid(1-42) proteins may result

Normal pressure hydrocephalus Chapter | 7 369

in global malfunctioning, that is, reduced mental speed, speed of walking,

and postural problems.

However, it is also possible to argue that the focal impairments of

gait (and not handling) disturbances, urinary incontinence, phonological

but not semantic word fluency reduction indicate a localized disorder of

brain functioning. If there is at least a partial explanation of iNPH symp-

toms by localized brain function, which parts of the brain might be dis-

turbed then? Gait apraxia is the most widely accepted symptom of iNPH.

The most dorsal and medial parts of the motor cortex are involved in

walking and tightening of the cortical areas above the falx cerebri, mea-

sured by a smaller callosal angle, is one of the primary neuroradiological

signs of iNPH. Postural stability, related to gait problems in iNPH is con-

trolled on different low levels of the nervous system (spinal, brain stem,

mid-brain, etc.) (Nieuwenhuys et al., 2008). Cortical control of postural

stability involves the supplementary motor cortex, the cingulate, and the

caudate nucleus (Goel et al., 2019; Solis-Escalante et al., 2019). The cau-

date nucleus is located adjacent to the lateral ventricles, the supplemen-

tary cortex, and the cingulate cortex involved in tightened above the

callosal corpus. Urinary incontinence is the second neurological sign of

iNPH. The dorsal anterior cingulate cortex, one of the higher ordered

brain centers for bladder control (Griffiths et al., 2005), is localized

directly inferior to the brain structures for the control of leg movements.

In contrast to semantic word fluency, where associations between the

exemplars help to generate the words, phonological word fluency is

based mainly on strategic search. A study of Whitney et al. (2009) com-

pared free verbal association with semantic word fluency and phonologi-

cal word fluency using functional MRI in healthy subjects. The common

area for the both word fluency task was the most dorsal anterior part of

the cingulate gyrus and the main difference was that during phonological

word fluency the hippocampus was inactive (as an indication that seman-

tic associations did not play a role for generating words with the same

first letter).

The supplementary and presupplementary motor area (SMA and pre-

SMA), adjacent to the dorsal and anterior parts of the cingulate cortex is

related to cognitive speed and spontaneous (internally triggered) behav-

ior. Both are part of the basal ganglia loop and bradyphrenia is the car-

dinal sign of PD (compare Chapter 3: The Alphasynucleinopathies:

Parkinson’s Disease, Lewy Body Dementia, and Multisystem Atrophy;

Section 3.2.1). In patients with PD, the SMA and the pre-SMA receive

less excitatory glutamatergic modulation by the thalamus which seems

to be related to slowed cognitive speed. Moreover, the SMA and the

pre-SMA relate internal to external cues. Lesions in the SMA and pre-

SMA are highly associated with apathy, a common neuropsychiatric

370 Neuropsychological Tools for Dementia

consequence of iNPH. The pre-SMA is highly involved in switching and

in inhibition. Monkeys with artificial lesions in the pre-SMA are

impaired in stopping actions after “Go” cues. Furthermore they seem to

perseverate in task execution, being primed by external affordances.

Perseveration and disinhibition, measured by the motor tasks of the

FAB, are frequent sign of iNPH when compared with healthy controls

and with patients with AD. Thus the compression of the cerebral con-

vexity, encompassing the dorsal (anterior and posterior) cingulate cor-

tex, the leg area of the homunculus, and the SMA and pre-SMA could

also explain most of the functional impairments of iNPH. If the com-

pression of these areas plays a major role for functional impairment in

iNPH one would assume that other functions relying on callosal transfer

should also be impaired and should resolves after shunt surgery. There

are a few studies showing a relation between the amount of pressure on

the callosal corpus and cognitive functions (Mataro et al., 2006, 2007)

and the recovery for callosal malfunctioning displayed by transcranial

magnetic stimulation after effective shunt implantation (Roricht, Meyer,

Woiciechowsky, & Lehmann, 1998). It should be added that the expla-

nation of the iNPH symptoms by local pressure does not exclude global

effects of pressure and does not contradict the glymphatic theory: local

pressure might especially decrease clearage of neurotoxins in these

structures. Future studies on specific impairments of patients with iNPH

might investigate whether they suffer from dysfunctions of the dorsome-

dial frontal cortex.
