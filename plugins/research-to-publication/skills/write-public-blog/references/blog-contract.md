# Public blog contract

## Language

- Use natural Traditional Chinese as the narrative language.
- At first necessary use, write `中文（English, acronym）`. Later use Chinese or the chosen acronym consistently.
- Do not repeat a full English term merely because it appeared in the academic manuscript.
- Retain English only when it carries identity or precision that translation would lose: model and trial names, generic drug names, genes/proteins, standard biomarkers, units, statistical symbols, and established acronyms.
- Keep reference titles and bibliographic metadata in the source language.
- Translate generic research prose after first definition. Words such as population, outcome, time horizon, calibration, validation, evidence, mechanism, marker, benefit, safety, and framework are not automatic English exceptions.
- Apply this contract to title, subtitle, headings, captions, callouts, tables, metadata, tags, and homepage cards.

## Structure and evidence

- Explain one conceptual dependency at a time.
- Keep narrative paragraphs within 240 Chinese characters unless the content type requires otherwise.
- Identify papers by author, year, and title at first substantive mention.
- Preserve uncertainty, counterexamples, and the difference between evidence and hypothesis.
- Do not change effect estimates, causal strength, or citation scope during language revision.
- Match the target site's current typography and content width.
- Require explicit content approval before publication.

## Required pre-publication audit

1. Build and verify the first-use terminology table.
2. Exclude references, code, formulas, and verbatim quotations from the prose-language audit.
3. Inspect each remaining sentence containing three or more non-whitelisted English lexical items.
4. Translate the sentence or document why the English is necessary.
5. Check that subsequent occurrences do not repeat the full English term.
6. Recheck all numbers and citations against the approved manuscript or claim ledger.
7. Report paragraph-length exceptions and retained-English categories.
