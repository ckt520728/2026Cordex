# Case study: English-heavy Taiwan diabetes blog revision

## Problem

A Traditional-Chinese clinical blog was evidence-correct but difficult to read because ordinary narrative nouns and clauses remained in English. The original instruction—Chinese-first terminology with English at first use—was too vague to prevent repeated code-switching.

## Intervention

- Preserved model names, trial names, generic drug names, biomarkers, and standard acronyms.
- Used `中文（English, acronym）` only at the first necessary occurrence.
- Replaced later general English terms with Chinese.
- Applied the rule to headings, figures, captions, tags, and homepage copy, not only paragraphs.
- Kept each narrative paragraph within 240 Chinese characters.
- Rechecked all effect estimates, cohort sizes, references, causal strength, and author metadata.

## Measured result

- Narrative English tokens decreased from 435 to 189, a 56.6% reduction.
- All narrative paragraphs were within 240 Chinese characters.
- The revised article retained 17 references, 2 inline SVGs, all critical numerical values, and the exact author name `朱國大醫師（Kwo-Ta Chu）`.
- Desktop and mobile page-level horizontal overflow remained absent.

## Lesson

Token reduction is diagnostic, not the goal. The durable rule is sentence-level: English remains only when it preserves identity or technical precision. General reasoning should read naturally in Traditional Chinese, and language editing must never weaken provenance or alter claims.
